# Content Recipe: Capable Wealth

> This document is the actionable guide for producing content that sounds like Jared while serving orthopedic surgeons. It defines HOW content gets made — the workflow, the building blocks, the quality gates, and the validation filters. Its architecture is grounded in the operating principles from *Becoming an AI-Ready Leader*.

## 1. The Content Value Flow

Every piece of content follows a value chain. Understanding the chain — and where value can leak — is the foundation for producing content that matters.

```
Audience Need → Discovery → Content Creation → Delivered Content → Engagement → Loyal Advocates
      |                                                                              |
      +---------------------------[ Reputation Flywheel ]----------------------------+
```

**Node 1 — Audience Need:** What is the orthopedic surgeon struggling with, thinking about, or facing right now? This is informed by the Content Calendar (static cycles, research checkpoints, signal triggers). Content that doesn't start with a real need is content that leaks value at the first node.

**Node 2 — Discovery / Pull Signals:** Content is designed to generate organic discovery. Satisfied readers become advocates who share content with peers — "New customers discover you not because you shouted louder, but because satisfied customers spoke for you." Every piece should contain genuine value that a surgeon would forward to a colleague.

**Node 3 — Content Creation System:** The composable production workflow defined in this document (see Building Blocks below).

**Node 4 — Delivered Content:** The finished piece, quality-gated and relevance-validated.

**Node 5 — Engagement / Retention:** Does the reader take a next step? Subscribe, share, schedule a conversation, read another piece?

**Node 6 — Loyal Advocates:** Readers who generate pull signals for new readers. The flywheel spins: loyalty produces social proof, social proof creates visibility, visibility creates new demand.

**Leakage diagnostic:** At each node, ask: What information is being lost? What context vanishes between steps? Where does accountability blur? Fix leaks first; amplify second.

## 2. Audience Translation Matrix

### The Shift

| Dimension | Original Audience | New Audience |
|-----------|------------------|--------------|
| Who | Young professionals, general consumers | Orthopedic surgeons, ages 45-65 |
| Income | $50K-$200K | $700K-$2M |
| Financial literacy | Beginner to intermediate | Intermediate to advanced |
| Primary concern | Getting started, building habits | Optimization, transition, preservation |
| Time availability | Variable | Severely limited |
| Decision style | Exploratory | Precise, evidence-driven |
| Life stage | Building career | Approaching or planning exit |
| Status | Employees, early homeowners | Practice owners, established wealth |
| Communication preference | Casual, digestible | Respectful of expertise, direct |

### Key Shifts in Content Approach

- **Higher financial sophistication.** These readers understand complex instruments. Do not explain what a 401(k) is — discuss cash balance plans, backdoor Roth strategies, and installment sale structuring.
- **Less need for basics, more need for optimization.** The question is not "should I save?" but "am I structured to keep the most?"
- **Clinical precision is valued.** Surgeons think in terms of diagnosis, treatment plans, and outcomes. Content that mirrors this structured thinking resonates.
- **Time-scarce readers.** Respect their time. Get to the point. Dense but clear beats long and meandering.
- **Complexity-tolerant.** Do not oversimplify. These readers can handle nuance and trade-offs — they navigate them daily in the OR.

## 3. Voice Calibration Guide

How Jared's voice adapts for orthopedic surgeons without losing its essence.

### Keep (Non-Negotiable)

These elements are the DNA of the voice. Without them, it is no longer Jared:

- **The warmth.** Content must feel like a conversation with a trusted advisor, not a memo.
- **The storytelling.** Every piece opens with a human moment — an anecdote, a conversation, a scenario.
- **The contrarian framing.** Challenge what surgeons think they know about their finances. Lead with "here's what most people in your position get wrong."
- **The personal anecdotes.** Jared's real-life experiences ground abstract concepts. Adapt them for surgeon relevance where possible.
- **The "knowledgeable friend" role.** Not a vendor. Not an institution. A person.
- **The philosophical depth.** Connect tactical advice to bigger questions about control, purpose, and legacy.
- **Math-as-persuasion.** Always include specific numbers. Surgeons respect precision.
- **"Capably Yours" sign-off.** On all long-form content (blog posts, emails, reports). Omit only on social posts where format doesn't support it.

### Elevate

These elements get dialed up for the new audience:

- **Technical depth.** Move from "here's what a 401(k) is" to "here's why a cash balance plan stacked with a 401(k) creates $200K+ in annual tax-deferred contributions."
- **Specificity of examples.** Replace generic salary examples ($50K) with surgeon-relevant numbers ($745K W-2, $1.2M practice valuation, $3M exit scenario).
- **Structured decision frameworks.** Surgeons think in protocols. Present financial decisions as structured trade-off analyses, not open-ended advice.
- **Stakes and scale.** The numbers are bigger, the consequences weightier. A poorly structured practice exit can cost $500K-$1.4M in unnecessary taxes. Make the stakes clear.

### Retire

These elements are appropriate for the original audience but not for surgeons:

- **Entry-level topics.** How to budget, basic debt payoff strategies, asking for a raise, what a credit score is.
- **Overly casual markers.** ";)" winks, excessive exclamation points, "EEK!" reactions. These work in casual blogging but undermine credibility with a senior professional audience.
- **Pop culture references that don't land.** Star Wars metaphors and bodybuilding analogies can be replaced with references that resonate with the surgical mindset.
- **Basic math examples.** "$5/day on coffee = $X/year" type examples feel patronizing at this income level.

### Add

New elements introduced specifically for this audience:

- **Medical-practice-specific context.** Practice valuation, succession planning, partner buyouts, S-Corp structuring for medical practices, physician employment contracts.
- **Transition and succession language.** "Life beyond the OR" (the brand tagline). Content should acknowledge that practice exit is not retirement — it is a transition to a different kind of productivity and purpose.
- **Surgical/clinical bridge metaphors.** "A financial plan is a treatment plan." "Diagnosing the real problem before prescribing a solution." "Precision in the OR, precision in your finances."
- **Time-constraint awareness.** Acknowledge that these readers have 12-hour surgery days. Respect that. Get to the value fast.
- **Peer-level credibility signals.** Reference surgeon-specific data, AAOS statistics, orthopedic practice benchmarks. Show that you understand their world, not just "high-income professionals."

## 4. Content as Building Blocks

The content pipeline is decomposed into discrete, composable jobs. Each block has defined inputs, processing, and outputs. Blocks are swappable and recombinable — a blog post might use all eight, a quick social post uses three.

### The Eight Content Building Blocks

**Block 1: Research**
- Inputs: Topic + content calendar context + current date
- Processing: Gather relevant facts, data, legislation, market conditions, and surgeon-specific context. Verify timeliness. Identify the angle.
- Outputs: Research brief with sources, current data, and relevance assessment
- Autonomy: L3 (AI researches, human reviews)

**Block 2: Brief**
- Inputs: Research brief + voice profile + audience matrix
- Processing: Define the content angle, structure, key points, target length, and target channel
- Outputs: Content brief
- Autonomy: L2 (human-led with AI assist)

**Block 3: Draft**
- Inputs: Content brief + voice profile + content recipe
- Processing: Write the first draft following Jared's structural DNA and voice characteristics
- Outputs: First draft
- Autonomy: L3 (AI drafts, human reviews)

**Block 4: Relevance Validation**
- Inputs: Draft + content calendar + current date
- Processing: Fact-check all references. Confirm timeliness. Check for conflicting current events. Apply the Relevance Filter (Section 5 below).
- Outputs: Validated draft with relevance score (Green / Yellow / Red)
- Autonomy: L3 (AI validates, human confirms)

**Block 5: Voice Validation**
- Inputs: Draft + voice profile
- Processing: Evaluate the draft against Jared's voice characteristics. Flag deviations. Check anti-patterns.
- Outputs: Voice-aligned draft with deviation notes
- Autonomy: L3 (AI checks, human judges)

**Block 6: Edit / Polish**
- Inputs: Validated draft
- Processing: Final editing for clarity, flow, grammar, and brand alignment
- Outputs: Final content
- Autonomy: L1 (human-led)

**Block 7: Distribution**
- Inputs: Final content + channel specifications
- Processing: Format for target platform (LinkedIn, YouTube, Facebook, blog). Schedule.
- Outputs: Formatted and scheduled content
- Autonomy: L4 (AI handles, human approves)

**Block 8: Performance**
- Inputs: Published content + engagement data
- Processing: Capture metrics. Feed into Recursive Learning Loop (Section 10).
- Outputs: Performance brief
- Autonomy: L4 (AI monitors, human reviews)

### Block Assembly by Content Type

| Content Type | Blocks Used | Notes |
|-------------|------------|-------|
| Blog post / article | All 8 | Full sequence |
| LinkedIn post | 1, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8 | Lighter brief, shorter draft |
| YouTube script | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 | Full sequence, script format |
| Facebook post | 1, 3, 5, 7, 8 | Lightest touch, adapted from LinkedIn or blog |
| Lead magnet / report | All 8 + Design block | Adds layout/design step (see note below) |
| Email sequence | 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8 | Brief is critical for sequence arc |

**Note on the Design block:** For lead magnets, reports, and designed PDFs, an additional Design/Layout step sits between Block 6 (Edit/Polish) and Block 7 (Distribution). This step uses the page templates in `templates/` and the brand config for visual standards. It is not numbered as a formal building block because it follows a different workflow — driven by the PDF generation pipeline in `src/` rather than the content production sequence. Inputs: final content + page templates + brand_config.json. Outputs: branded PDF. Autonomy: L2 (human-led with AI rendering).

## 5. The Research and Relevance Filter

A mandatory validation gate applied to every piece of content before publication. Three dimensions:

### A. Static Calendar Alignment

Is this content timed correctly relative to known annual cycles? Reference [content-calendar.md](content-calendar.md) Layer 1.

- Tax-related content should precede relevant deadlines by 4-6 weeks (planning window)
- Year-end content should begin in September-October (not December when it is too late)
- Contribution limit content should publish in January when new limits take effect
- Practice transition content peaks in May-June (valuation cycle) and August-September (succession planning)

### B. Dynamic Fact Validation

Are all referenced facts, figures, and legal references current as of publication date?

**Mandatory checks:**
- Tax rates, brackets, and thresholds (federal and relevant state)
- Retirement contribution limits (401k, IRA, HSA, cash balance, defined benefit)
- Estate and gift tax exemption amounts and sunset provisions
- Current interest rate environment (Fed funds rate, mortgage rates, bond yields)
- Orthopedic practice economics (average compensation, reimbursement trends, valuation multiples)
- Any pending or recently enacted legislation affecting physicians, taxes, retirement, or healthcare

**Process:** Every draft passes through a fact-check against current data. Stale numbers get updated. If a major assumption has changed since the content was briefed, the brief is revised before the draft proceeds.

### C. Signal-Driven Relevance

Is there something happening right now that this content should acknowledge, incorporate, or be delayed for?

- Has a major tax bill been introduced or passed?
- Has the Fed made a rate decision that changes the calculus?
- Is there a market event that would make this content tone-deaf or urgently timely?
- Is the orthopedic industry experiencing a shift (consolidation, PE activity, reimbursement change)?
- Is there mainstream media coverage of physician finances, burnout, or retirement that primes the audience?

### Relevance Score

Every piece receives a score before publication:

- **Green:** Timely, factually current, no conflicting signals. Proceed to publication.
- **Yellow:** Mostly current but needs specific updates or could benefit from timing adjustment. Revise and re-score.
- **Red:** Contains stale information, conflicts with current events, or is poorly timed. Hold and rework.

Only Green content publishes. Yellow content is revised. Red content is shelved until conditions change.

## 6. Human Checkpoints

Content quality is governed by three checkpoint modes, calibrated to the stakes of the content.

### Review Mode
Human sees and edits every output before it moves downstream.

**Used for:**
- First-ever content in a new format
- Content touching regulatory, compliance, or legal topics
- Anything representing a new brand position or philosophy
- High-stakes lead magnets and reports

### Approval Mode
Human authorizes publication but does not line-edit.

**Used for:**
- Recurring blog posts following established patterns
- Social content derived from approved long-form pieces
- Content using validated templates with proven track records

### Escalation Mode
AI operates within defined bounds. Human is notified only when metrics deviate or flags trigger.

**Used for:**
- Content reformatting across channels
- Routine scheduling
- Republishing or repurposing previously approved content

### Progression Rules

- New content formats always start in Review Mode
- A format graduates to Approval Mode after 10+ successful uses with less than 5% rejection rate
- Escalation Mode is only for mature, high-volume, low-risk operations
- Any content that touches compliance, tax specifics, or legal claims always stays in Review Mode regardless of format maturity

## 7. Content Architecture Templates

### Blog Post / Article (800-1,200 words)

Follows Jared's six-step structural DNA, adapted for surgeons:

1. **Hook** (1-2 paragraphs): A scenario or conversation that a surgeon would recognize. Tighter than Jared's original 1-3 paragraph hooks — surgeons are time-scarce. "A colleague recently asked me..." or "I was reviewing a client's practice financials when something jumped out..."
2. **Reframe** (1 paragraph): Challenge what the surgeon thinks they know. "Most surgeons in your position default to X. Here is what that is actually costing you."
3. **Core Teaching** (3-5 sections with bold subheadings): The structured argument. Use numbered steps, frameworks, or comparison tables. Mirror the diagnostic thinking surgeons use daily.
4. **Math Example** (embedded in core teaching): Specific to surgeon-level numbers. "$745K W-2 income, 40% effective tax rate, $298K to taxes. Here is how to reduce that by $85K."
5. **Philosophical Tie-Back** (1-2 paragraphs): Connect to control, purpose, legacy, or "life beyond the OR."
6. **Sign-Off:** "Capably Yours, Jared" (or adapted brand sign-off)

### LinkedIn Post (150-300 words)

- **Line 1:** Hook — a bold statement, contrarian observation, or provocative question. Must earn the "see more" click.
- **Body:** One core insight, clearly stated. Use line breaks for readability. Include one specific number or example.
- **Close:** A question to the reader or a clear takeaway. No hard CTA — the value IS the CTA.
- **Tone:** Same warmth as blog posts but tighter. Every word earns its place.

### YouTube Script (Talking Points Outline)

- **Hook** (15-30 seconds): The contrarian question or surprising stat that earns the viewer's attention.
- **Context** (30-60 seconds): Why this matters specifically to orthopedic surgeons. Set the stakes.
- **Key Points** (3-5 points, 60-90 seconds each): Structured teaching. Each point is self-contained.
- **Close** (30-60 seconds): The philosophical tie-back + invitation to engage (subscribe, comment, visit site).
- **Tone:** Conversational, as if talking to one surgeon across a desk. Not a lecture.

### Facebook Post (100-200 words)

- Adapted from LinkedIn content or standalone insight
- More conversational and personal than LinkedIn
- Can include a question to drive comments
- Lighter touch — one idea, one takeaway

### Email (300-500 words)

- **Subject line:** Contrarian or curiosity-driven (mirrors Jared's blog titles)
- **Body:** One story, one insight, one takeaway. Same warmth as blog posts but compressed.
- **CTA:** Single, clear, invitational. "If this resonates, let's have a conversation."
- **Sign-off:** "Capably Yours, Jared"

## 9. Language and Terminology Guide

### Use These Words and Phrases

Aligned with [brand_config.json](brand_config.json) voice standards:

- Strategic, deliberate, coordinated
- Complexity, trade-offs, options
- Alignment, structure, planning
- Transition, succession, next chapter
- Precision, optimization, positioning
- Conversation, process, decision-making
- "Life beyond the OR"
- "Structured for" (as in "Is your wealth structured for what comes next?")

### Avoid These Words and Phrases

- Guaranteed, no-brainer, can't miss
- Secrets, hacks, shortcuts
- Crush, dominate, optimize everything
- Fear-based urgency ("time is running out," "before it's too late" — unless factually true, e.g., estate exemption sunset)
- Jargon without explanation
- "Wealth management" (generic — prefer specific: "tax planning," "practice transition planning," "retirement structure")
- "High-net-worth" (clinical label — prefer "surgeon," "practice owner," or "you")

### Bridge Metaphors

Metaphors that connect surgical thinking to financial planning:

- "A financial plan is a treatment plan — diagnosis first, then intervention."
- "Precision in the OR, precision in your finances."
- "You wouldn't operate without imaging. Why would you exit your practice without a valuation?"
- "The best surgeons don't just fix the problem — they anticipate what comes next. The same applies to your wealth."
- "Your practice is your greatest asset and your greatest concentration risk."

### The Golden Rule

From brand_config.json: *"If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it. If it sounds like a thoughtful professional explaining a decision, it's correct."*

## 10. The Recursive Learning Loop

Every piece of content feeds a five-stage loop that makes the next piece better.

### Stage 1: Observe

Capture signals from published content:
- Engagement metrics (views, clicks, shares, comments, saves)
- Conversion data (email signups, consultation requests, downloads)
- Time-on-page and scroll depth
- Social sharing patterns (who shares, where, what they say)
- Direct feedback (replies, DMs, conversation mentions)

### Stage 2: Detect

Identify patterns across content performance:
- Which topics consistently outperform?
- Which formats (long vs. short, list vs. narrative) drive more engagement?
- Which channels produce the most qualified engagement?
- Are there timing patterns (day of week, time of year)?
- Is any topic losing relevance? Gaining urgency?

### Stage 3: Interpret

Synthesize root causes (human + AI together):
- Why did that post outperform? Was it the topic, the timing, the angle, the headline, or the format?
- Why did that piece underperform? Was it the wrong audience, wrong channel, wrong moment?
- What does the data suggest about what the audience wants next?

### Stage 4: Refactor

Translate understanding into system improvements:
- Update the Content Calendar with adjusted topic priorities
- Refine content architecture templates based on what works
- Adjust the voice calibration if certain tones resonate more
- Add new topic mappings based on emerging audience needs
- Document changes with rationale (version the updates)

### Stage 5: Reflect

Surface learnings and close the loop:
- Publish a brief "What We Learned" summary
- Feed insights back into the Content Recipe and Content Calendar
- Share learnings with anyone involved in content production
- Identify experiments for the next cycle

### Cadence

| Review Type | Frequency | Scope | Output |
|------------|-----------|-------|--------|
| Lightweight | Monthly | Top 3 learnings, quick metrics check | Brief update note |
| Deep review | Quarterly | Full flywheel cycle, all five stages | Updated master documents |
| Strategic | Annually | Topic priorities, audience evolution, voice refinement | Revised editorial strategy |

## 11. Quality Checklist

The final gate before any content publishes. Every item must pass.

### Voice Alignment

- [ ] Opens with a story, conversation, or specific scenario (not a definition or statistic)
- [ ] Contains at least one contrarian reframe or myth-bust
- [ ] Uses direct "you" address throughout
- [ ] Reads like a conversation with a trusted advisor, not a brochure or sales piece
- [ ] Avoids all banned language from brand standards (Section 9)
- [ ] Ends with a clear takeaway and appropriate sign-off
- [ ] Tone matches the voice profile — warm, encouraging, gently contrarian

### Audience Specificity

- [ ] Could only have been written for orthopedic surgeons (not generic financial advice)
- [ ] Includes a concrete numerical example relevant to surgeon-level income ($700K-$2M)
- [ ] References practice-specific context where appropriate (valuations, transitions, compensation structures)
- [ ] Respects the reader's intelligence and time — no unnecessary explanation of basics

### Relevance Validation

- [ ] All facts, figures, and legal references are current as of publication date
- [ ] Content is appropriately timed relative to the annual calendar (Section 5A)
- [ ] No conflicting current events that would undermine the message (Section 5C)
- [ ] Relevance Score is Green (or Yellow with documented justification for proceeding)

### Pull Signal Design

- [ ] Content provides genuine value that a surgeon would share with a colleague
- [ ] Ties tactical advice back to a bigger life principle (control, purpose, legacy)
- [ ] Includes a natural next step that deepens the relationship (not a hard sell)
- [ ] The reader finishes feeling more informed and more in control — not more anxious

## 12. Example Transformations

### Example A: "Compound Interest" → "Cash Balance Plan Compounding"

**Original angle (general audience):**
> "Einstein called compound interest the 8th wonder of the world. Here's why. If you invest $500/month starting at age 25..."

**Transformed angle (orthopedic surgeons):**
> "You've spent a decade building surgical skill. But there's a financial instrument most surgeons don't discover until it's almost too late — and it can shelter $150K to $350K per year while compounding tax-deferred. Here's how a cash balance plan works, and why the math changes everything when your W-2 is north of $700K."

**What changed:** Same underlying concept (compounding), but the entry point is surgeon-specific, the numbers are at their scale, and the hook acknowledges their expertise rather than starting from scratch.

### Example B: "Stop Planning for Retirement" → "Life Beyond the OR"

**Original angle (general audience):**
> "I have no intention of ever retiring. I have no retirement age in mind. The concept of planning for some future life that you will eventually love is foolish."

**Transformed angle (orthopedic surgeons):**
> "You've spent 20+ years in the OR. The idea of walking away entirely probably feels as wrong as it does appealing. Here's the thing most advisors won't tell you: the 'retirement' model — some magic number, some target age, some beach somewhere — doesn't work for people who built their identity around precision and purpose. What works is designing your next chapter with the same intentionality you brought to your practice."

**What changed:** Same philosophy (reject traditional retirement, design your life now), but the hook acknowledges the surgeon's identity relationship with their work. The tone shifts from rebellious to empathetic. The call is not to reject retirement but to redesign it.

### Example C: "The Raccoon Trap" → "The Surgeon's Spending Trap"

**Original angle (general audience):**
> "Like a raccoon trap, we reach into a hole for the shiny object, grab it, and won't let go — even when letting go would free us."

**Transformed angle (orthopedic surgeons):**
> "Walk through any surgeon parking lot and you'll see $2M in depreciating assets. The cars, the watches, the vacation homes — they're the shiny objects in the raccoon trap. And at $745K in annual income, the trap is more seductive and more expensive. Here's what I see when I review a surgeon's finances for the first time: lifestyle expenses that consume 85-90% of after-tax income, leaving almost nothing compounding for the future. You don't have an income problem. You have a structure problem."

**What changed:** Same metaphor (raccoon trap), but grounded in the surgeon's specific world. The parking lot image is visceral and recognizable. The numbers are at scale. The diagnosis — "structure problem, not income problem" — mirrors clinical thinking.
