**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "You're Not Overtaxed. You're Understructured."
- **Target angle & key argument:** Most orthopedic surgeons fixate on their tax bill and assume they need a "better accountant." The real driver of how much they keep comes down to their entity structure. Walk through the three structural gaps that cost the most: (1) a misaligned S-Corp salary, (2) an underfunded or nonexistent cash balance plan, and (3) a missing cost segregation study on owned practice or investment real estate. The argument: fixing structure is worth more than chasing deductions.
- **Math example:** An $800K-earning orthopedic surgeon with an unoptimized S-Corp salary of $400K, no cash balance plan, and no cost segregation study. Close with the philosophical tie-back: structure is control, and control is what separates wealth from high income.

### Week 2: March 2–8

#### Theme: The S-Corp salary question

---

**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "The S-Corp Salary Trap: Why Your CPA's 'Reasonable Compensation' Might Be Costing You"
- **Target angle & key argument:** S-Corp reasonable compensation review window opens in Q1, and most surgeon-owners are running on a number their CPA set years ago. Challenge the assumption that "reasonable" means "optimal." The S-Corp salary is a balancing act: set it too high and you overpay self-employment tax; set it too low and you limit retirement plan contributions (which are based on W-2 compensation). At $745K+ in total income, getting this wrong in either direction costs real money — and most surgeons have never been shown the math on the trade-off.
- **Math example:** A surgeon with $745K in practice income. Scenario A: S-Corp salary set at $300K (the "low" default). Self-employment tax savings are maximized, but the 401(k) employer match and cash balance plan contributions are capped by the lower W-2. Scenario B: Salary set at $360K (the IRS compensation cap). Higher self-employment tax, but unlocks an additional $35K–$60K in retirement plan contributions depending on age and cash balance plan design. Net benefit of Scenario B: ~$15K–$25K per year in additional tax-deferred compounding, even after the higher payroll tax cost. The "lower salary saves money" assumption is wrong once retirement plan math enters the picture.
- **Structural approach:** Conversation Hook — open with a conversation with a surgeon client who says "my CPA told me to keep my salary as low as possible." Then Reframe: "Your CPA is optimizing for one variable. There are three." Core Teaching via a decision framework: the three variables (payroll tax, retirement contributions, QBI deduction) and how they interact. Math example as the persuasion anchor. Close with: the right salary is the one that puts the most money to work.

### Week 3: March 9–15

#### Theme: The reimbursement shift and what it means for your wealth

---

**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "The 2.5% Cut Nobody's Talking About — and What It Actually Means for Your Practice"
- **Target angle & key argument:** CMS's 2026 "efficiency adjustment" cuts reimbursement on non-time-based orthopedic procedures by 2.5%. Most surgeons will hear about this from their practice manager, not their financial advisor — and most will respond by trying to increase volume. Challenge that reflex. When revenue per procedure drops, the strategic response isn't more procedures — it's restructuring how practice income flows through to personal wealth. Frame the reimbursement cut as a strategic inflection point: a signal to evaluate practice overhead, optimize entity structure, and ensure every dollar of reduced revenue is being kept as efficiently as possible.
- **Math example:** A surgeon performing 400 procedures/year at an average reimbursement of $3,200. The 2.5% cut reduces per-procedure revenue by $80, totaling $32,000 in annual revenue loss. If the surgeon responds by adding 10 procedures to make up the difference, the marginal cost of those additional procedures (OR time, anesthesia, supplies, staff) consumes ~60% of the revenue — netting only $12,800 recovered. Compare this to a structural optimization: reducing practice overhead by 2% (realistic for most practices) recovers the full $32K with zero additional procedures. The point: working harder is the most expensive way to replace lost revenue.
- **Structural approach:** The Diagnostic Framework — open with the CMS announcement as the "imaging result," then diagnose the real problem (most surgeons will treat the symptom — lost revenue — rather than the underlying condition — structural inefficiency). Core Teaching walks through three response strategies: (1) volume increase (the instinctive but expensive response), (2) overhead optimization (the overlooked middle ground), and (3) income flow restructuring (the highest-leverage move). Tie back to the thesis: reimbursement cuts don't change how much you earn as much as they reveal how much you're wasting.

### Week 4: March 16–22

#### Theme: The number that matters more than collections

---

**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "Before Q1 Closes: The One Number That Matters More Than Collections"
- **Target angle & key argument:** Surgeons track collections religiously — it's the headline number in every practice meeting. But collections tell you how much the practice earned, not how much the surgeon kept. Introduce the "surgeon effective hourly rate" — a single number that captures what remains after taxes, overhead, debt service, and mandatory savings. If a practice collects $2.4M annually but the surgeon's take-home after everything is $380K, and the surgeon works 2,600 clinical hours, the effective rate is $146/hour. Compare that to what the surgeon assumes they're earning. The reframe: you don't have a revenue problem — you have a keep rate problem. Position this as the framework to apply as soon as Q1 data is available at month-end.
- **Math example:** A $2.4M-collecting practice with 55% overhead ($1.32M), leaving $1.08M for the surgeon. After federal + state taxes (~40% effective on $1.08M = $432K), mandatory retirement contributions ($72K), and debt service ($96K/year on practice loans), the surgeon keeps ~$480K. At 2,600 clinical hours/year, the effective hourly rate is $185/hour. Now model the same surgeon with structural improvements from Weeks 1–3: overhead reduced to 52% ($1.25M), S-Corp salary optimized, cash balance plan added. After-tax keep rate improves to ~$560K, effective hourly rate rises to $215/hour. The $30/hour difference — over 2,600 hours — is $78,000 annually.
- **Structural approach:** Math-First Approach — open with the collections number everyone celebrates, then immediately introduce the effective hourly rate calculation that deflates it. The contrast creates the hook. Core Teaching walks through the five components that determine the effective rate (gross collections, overhead, taxes, mandatory savings, debt service) and which ones the surgeon can actually influence. Close with the Q1 checkpoint: when Q1 closes at the end of March, calculate your effective hourly rate and compare it to last year. If it's lower, the CMS cuts and structural gaps from the previous three weeks are the likely culprits. Give surgeons the framework now so they're ready to run the numbers as soon as Q1 data is available.

### Week 5: March 23–29

#### Theme: Your tax return is a diagnostic report

---

**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "Your Tax Return Is a Diagnostic Report. Here's How to Read It."
- **Target angle & key argument:** Surgeons understand diagnostic imaging — they don't just look at the image, they interpret what it means for treatment. Apply the same framework to a tax return. Walk through 3–4 specific line items on a surgeon's return that reveal structural problems: the effective tax rate line (is it higher than it should be at your income level?), the QBI deduction (present or absent — and why), the retirement contribution total vs. what was available (how much capacity went unused?), and the state tax burden (is your state costing you more than you think?). The return is a compliance document for your CPA. For you, it should be a strategic planning tool.
- **Math example:** A surgeon's return showing $820K in AGI, $298K in total tax (36.3% effective rate), a QBI deduction of $0 (because their S-Corp salary was too high relative to pass-through income), retirement contributions of $31,000 (the 401(k) max but no cash balance plan), and $52K in state income tax. #### Theme: Personal goodwill — the tax strategy hidden inside your reputation

**ANCHOR PIECE**

- **Working title:** "Personal Goodwill: The Tax Strategy That Could Save You $300K+ on Your Practice Sale"
- **Target angle & key argument:** For surgeons 3–5 years from exit, personal goodwill planning must start now — not during sale negotiations. Personal goodwill is the value attributable to the surgeon's individual reputation, skills, referral relationships, and patient loyalty. It's distinct from enterprise goodwill (the practice's brand, systems, and transferable value). The tax impact is massive: personal goodwill is taxed as long-term capital gains (20% + 3.8% NIIT) while enterprise goodwill allocated to certain assets can be taxed at ordinary income rates (37% + 3.8%). But — and this is critical — personal goodwill must be established and documented before negotiations begin, not asserted during them. The IRS scrutinizes after-the-fact personal goodwill claims. The time to start is now.
- **Math example:** A $4M practice sale. Without personal goodwill documentation: the entire amount is allocated to enterprise assets and taxed at a blended rate averaging 32% (mix of ordinary income on some asset classes and capital gains on others) = $1.28M in tax. The surgeon keeps $2.72M. With proper personal goodwill documentation allocating 40% ($1.6M) to personal goodwill: the $1.6M is taxed at 23.8% (LTCG + NIIT) = $381K, and the remaining $2.4M is taxed at the blended 32% = $768K. Total tax: $1.149M. The surgeon keeps $2.851M. Difference: $131K. Now extend the personal goodwill allocation to 55% (achievable with strong documentation): personal goodwill of $2.2M taxed at 23.8% = $524K, remaining $1.8M at 32% = $576K. Total tax: $1.1M. Surgeon keeps $2.9M — saving $180K+ compared to zero personal goodwill planning. Over the full range of realistic allocations, the savings typically fall between $130K and $350K.

Core Teaching explains: what personal goodwill is (and isn't), why the IRS cares about the distinction, how to document it before a sale (non-compete agreements, patient referral documentation, personal branding evidence), and the timeline for proper establishment. Math example shows the tax savings at different allocation percentages. Close: "Personal goodwill is the value of everything you've built that walks out the door when you do.
