## Content

I sat down with Dr. K last Tuesday, a 54-year-old orthopedic surgeon running a mid-size practice, and after reviewing his second-quarter numbers he told me it changed everything for him.

Now that Q2 has closed, your own numbers are in too, and this is the moment in the year when the picture finally gets honest. The first quarter is noise, but by the end of June you can see the real trajectory — your collections, your effective tax rate, the gap between what you are earning and what you are keeping.

Here is what most surgeons miss at the half-year mark. The window to act on this year's tax position is still open, but it narrows every month from here, and the moves that matter most, a cash balance plan contribution, a reasonable-compensation adjustment, a Roth conversion in a lower-income year, all take time to implement properly.

Dr. K's situation was a textbook example of waiting too long, and once we restructured things the savings were immediate and substantial.

The mid-year review is your follow-up appointment with your own finances. You would never tell a patient to skip a six-month check after a major procedure, so do not skip your own.
