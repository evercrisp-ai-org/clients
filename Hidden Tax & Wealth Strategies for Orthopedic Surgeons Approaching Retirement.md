## Executive Summary

Orthopedic surgeons in their peak earning years face a steep tax burden that can quietly erode their wealth as retirement nears. Earning between $700,000 and $2 million annually, many surgeons pay effective tax rates of 40% or more [1, 2], especially in high-tax states. In fact, the average full-time orthopedic surgeon earns around $745,000 (with top earners at $2 million) [3], placing them in the highest tax brackets. Yet 75% of physicians feel they overpay in taxes [4], and nearly half (48%) prepare their own taxes without specialized help [5] – a recipe for missed opportunities. This guide addresses that gap, revealing hidden tax and wealth strategies tailored for orthopedic surgeons aged 50–65 who are 5–10 years from retirement or practice exit.

**The central message:** proactive tax planning in your final working years can dramatically boost your retirement lifestyle and legacy. High-income surgeons often leave six-figure sums on the table by not leveraging advanced strategies in entity structuring, retirement plans, investment allocation, practice sale planning, and estate optimization. The cost of doing nothing is not just a higher tax bill – it's potentially working longer than desired or scaling back retirement goals.

For example, a surgeon who reduces their tax outflow by $100,000 per year in the last decade before retirement could accumulate roughly **$1+ million in extra savings**, translating to an additional $40,000–$50,000 in yearly retirement income (assuming moderate growth and a 4% withdrawal rate). Conversely, failing to plan might mean retiring with significantly less. About 20% of physicians retire with over $5 million saved [6] – roughly enough to generate a comfortable $200–250K annual income. Without strategic tax savings, reaching that level may require additional years in the OR or settling for a leaner lifestyle.

The tone is about opportunity and control: you have a limited window in your final working years to lock in tax efficiencies that can meaningfully enhance your retirement lifestyle and family legacy. ## The Problem: Why High-Income Surgeons Overpay Taxes

High-income professionals like orthopedic surgeons often overpay in taxes – not by breaking any rules, but by failing to leverage the full scope of legal strategies available. Orthopedic surgeons typically fall into the top 1% of earners, and thus the top marginal federal tax bracket (currently 37% in 2025). Add state income taxes (which can exceed 10% in states like California, New York, New Jersey) plus Medicare surtaxes, and surgeons can lose half of each extra dollar to taxes.

For example, an $800,000 single-filer income in a high-tax state might result in a total tax burden of ~40% – $320,000 paid to the IRS and state, leaving under $480,000 after taxes. In essence, **taxes are often the single largest expense in a surgeon's life**, larger than any mortgage or tuition bill.

### Why does this happen?

Primarily because surgeons have complex, high-income finances but limited time to manage them. They often default to straightforward approaches (W-2 salaries or simple pass-through income, basic 401(k) contributions) that are easy but not optimized. Consider these telling statistics:

- **Three out of four physicians feel overtaxed.** In a broad survey, 75% of physicians said they pay too much in taxes. This sentiment reflects that many suspect (correctly) that money is being left on the table.

- **Nearly half do their own taxes.** About 48% of physicians prepare/file taxes on their own, which suggests they may not be implementing sophisticated tax planning strategies that a seasoned CPA or advisor would pursue. High-earning surgeons juggling a practice may simply lack the bandwidth to delve into tax optimization, inadvertently overpaying as a result.

- **Audit risk is non-trivial.** Approximately 1 in 5 physicians reported being audited in a recent tax year. Complex financial lives with high incomes can draw IRS scrutiny, especially if aggressive deductions are taken without proper guidance. Fear of audits sometimes causes surgeons to err on the side of overpaying, taking fewer deductions or conservative positions (the "safe but expensive" route).

Moreover, the tax code contains special phase-outs and surcharges that uniquely hit high earners like surgeons:

- **No QBI Deduction:** Physicians generally do not qualify for the 20% pass-through deduction (QBI) on practice income because their income usually exceeds the threshold for specified service businesses. This means unlike some business owners, a successful surgeon can't shave off 20% of practice profits for a deduction – effectively a tax break denied.

- **Net Investment Income Tax:** Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains) faces an extra 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax if adjusted gross income is above $250,000 (married), which virtually all orthopedic surgeons exceed. These provisions create a stealth tax penalty on high earners.

**In summary,** the problem is not that surgeons aren't paying what they owe – it's that they're often paying more than they legally need to. The U.S. tax code, while complex, offers many avenues for relief to those who plan ahead. But without targeted planning, surgeons default to overpaying.

## Strategy 1: Entity & Income Structuring

### Overview

The way you structure your practice and allocate income can significantly impact your tax burden. Many surgeons operate as sole proprietors or partners in pass-through entities without optimizing for tax efficiency.

### Who This Is For

- Surgeons with ownership stake in their practice
- Those earning $500,000+ with flexibility in compensation structure
- Practice owners considering restructuring before retirement/sale

### Who This Is NOT For

- Employed surgeons with no ownership or control over compensation
- Those in restrictive employment contracts

### Key Strategies

#### S-Corporation Election

For surgeons operating as sole proprietors or single-member LLCs, electing S-Corp status can yield significant savings on self-employment taxes.

**How it works:**
- As an S-Corp owner, you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" subject to payroll taxes
- Additional profits distributed as dividends avoid the 15.3% self-employment tax (up to the Social Security wage base) and 2.9% Medicare tax on amounts above

**Example:**
A surgeon with $800,000 in practice income might pay themselves a $350,000 salary (meeting "reasonable compensation" standards) and take $450,000 as distributions. The Medicare savings alone on that $450,000 distribution equals approximately $13,000 annually.

**IRS Red Flags to Avoid:**
- Setting salary unreasonably low to maximize distributions
- Failing to document the basis for "reasonable compensation"
- Not paying salary through proper payroll with withholding

#### Income Allocation Among Family Members

Where legitimate, employing family members in the practice can shift income to lower tax brackets.

**Considerations:**
- Compensation must be reasonable for services actually performed
- Children under 18 employed by a parent's sole proprietorship are exempt from FICA
- Retirement plan contributions can be made on behalf of employed family members

### Dollar-Value Impact

| Strategy | Typical Annual Savings |
|----------|----------------------|
| S-Corp election (vs. sole proprietor) | $15,000 – $40,000 |
| Family employment optimization | $5,000 – $20,000 |
| Business expense optimization | $10,000 – $30,000 |

## Strategy 2: Advanced Retirement & Tax-Deferred Vehicles

### Overview

Most surgeons max out their 401(k) contributions and stop there. But for high earners, the basic $23,500 annual limit (plus $7,500 catch-up for those 50+) barely scratches the surface of available tax deferral.

### The Cash Balance Plan: The "Super Saver" for Surgeons

Cash balance pension plans allow dramatically higher contributions than traditional 401(k) plans, especially for older participants.

**2025 Contribution Limits by Age:**

| Age | Maximum Annual Contribution* |
|-----|----------------------------|
| 50 | ~$150,000 |
| 55 | ~$200,000 |
| 60 | ~$280,000 |
| 62+ | ~$350,000+ |

*Combined 401(k) and cash balance contributions; exact amounts depend on plan design and IRS interest rate assumptions

**How It Works:**
- Defined benefit plan providing a guaranteed "account balance" at retirement
- Contributions are tax-deductible as business expenses
- Assets grow tax-deferred until distribution
- Can be rolled into an IRA at retirement

**Case Study:**
Dr. Smith, age 58, earns $1.2M annually. By implementing a cash balance plan alongside his 401(k), he contributes $265,000 tax-deferred annually. At his 37% marginal rate, this reduces his annual tax bill by approximately $98,000.

**Who This Is Ideal For:**
- Practice owners with stable, high income
- Those within 10 years of retirement (contributions increase with age)
- Surgeons who have "catching up" to do on retirement savings

**Considerations:**
- Requires annual actuarial certification
- Must cover eligible employees (though contribution formulas can favor older, higher-paid owners)
- Multi-year commitment recommended (though not legally required)

### Roth Conversion Strategy

While high-income surgeons cannot contribute directly to Roth IRAs, strategic Roth conversions can provide significant long-term tax benefits.

**Optimal Timing for Conversions:**
- Years with lower income (sabbatical, reduced schedule)
- Gap between retirement and Social Security/RMDs
- Years with significant deductible losses

**The "Roth Conversion Ladder":**
Plan conversions over multiple years to avoid pushing into higher brackets while still benefiting from tax-free growth and distributions in retirement.

### Backdoor Roth IRA

For high earners above income limits, the "backdoor" Roth remains available:
1. Contribute to a non-deductible traditional IRA
2. Convert to Roth IRA (paying tax only on gains, if any)
3. Repeat annually for tax-free growth

**Warning:** The pro-rata rule applies if you have existing pre-tax IRA balances, potentially creating unexpected tax liability.

## Strategy 3: Investment Tax Optimization

### Overview

Even after maximizing retirement contributions, surgeons typically have substantial taxable investment accounts. How these assets are managed can create significant tax drag – or tax efficiency.

### Asset Location: The Right Investment in the Right Account

**The Principle:** Place tax-inefficient investments in tax-advantaged accounts; hold tax-efficient investments in taxable accounts.

**Optimal Placement:**

| Account Type | Best Assets to Hold |
|--------------|-------------------|
| **Tax-deferred (401k, IRA)** | Bonds, REITs, actively managed funds, high-yield investments |
| **Roth** | Highest expected growth (small cap, emerging markets) |
| **Taxable** | Index funds, ETFs, municipal bonds, long-term holdings |

**Why This Matters:**
- Bond interest is taxed as ordinary income (up to 37%)
- Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends taxed at 15-20%
- Municipal bond interest is often tax-free
- REITs pass through income taxed at ordinary rates

**Example Savings:**
A surgeon with $2M in investments earning 6% annually:
- Poor asset location: ~$28,000 annual tax drag
- Optimal asset location: ~$16,000 annual tax drag
- **Annual savings: $12,000+**

### Tax-Loss Harvesting

Systematically selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains while maintaining portfolio allocation.

**Key Rules:**
- Losses first offset gains of the same character (short-term vs. long-term)
- Net losses up to $3,000 annually can offset ordinary income
- Excess losses carry forward indefinitely
- Wash-sale rule: Cannot repurchase "substantially identical" securities within 30 days

**Strategic Application:**
- Harvest losses in down markets while staying invested (replace with similar, not identical, funds)
- Stockpile losses for future high-gain years
- Use losses to offset gains from concentrated stock positions

### Municipal Bonds for High Earners

For surgeons in the top bracket, municipal bond yields become competitive on an after-tax basis.

**Tax-Equivalent Yield Calculation:**

Tax-equivalent yield = Municipal yield ÷ (1 – marginal tax rate)

**Example:**
A 4% municipal bond for a surgeon in the 37% federal + 10% state bracket:

4% ÷ (1 – 0.47) = **7.55% equivalent taxable yield**

## Strategy 4: Practice Exit & Pre-Liquidity Planning

### Overview

For surgeon-owners, the sale or transition of a medical practice represents one of the largest financial events of their career. Proper planning can mean the difference between capital gains treatment (20% + 3.8% NIIT) and ordinary income (37%+).

### Key Concepts

#### Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale

**Asset Sale (Buyer Preferred):**
- Practice assets purchased individually
- Allows buyer to step up basis and claim depreciation
- Seller faces potential ordinary income on certain assets

**Stock/Equity Sale (Seller Preferred):**
- Entire entity sold
- Seller typically receives capital gains treatment
- Buyer inherits existing basis (less favorable for them)

**Negotiation Strategy:** Often, a stock sale with a price reduction can benefit both parties when tax savings are shared.

#### Personal Goodwill

One of the most valuable tax planning concepts for practice sales is personal goodwill.

**How It Works:**
- Goodwill attributable to the selling surgeon's personal reputation, relationships, and skills
- Taxed as capital gains (vs. ordinary income for corporate goodwill)
- Can represent a significant portion of practice value

**Requirements:**
- Must be genuinely personal (not contractually assigned to the practice)
- Should be documented before sale negotiations
- Independent valuation recommended

**Example:**
Dr. Jones sells her orthopedic practice for $3M. Through proper structuring:
- $1.5M allocated to personal goodwill (capital gains: 23.8%)
- Tax on goodwill: $357,000

Without personal goodwill treatment (ordinary income: 40.8%):
- Tax on same amount: $612,000
- **Tax savings: $255,000**

### Installment Sales

Spreading the sale proceeds over multiple years can:
- Defer tax liability
- Potentially keep income in lower brackets
- Provide seller financing (at interest)

**Considerations:**
- Interest charged to buyer is taxable income to seller
- Risk of buyer default
- Depreciation recapture typically recognized in year of sale

### 1031 Exchange Opportunities

If your practice includes real estate that you own separately, a 1031 exchange can defer capital gains.

**Requirements:**
- Like-kind property (real estate for real estate)
- Strict timelines: 45 days to identify replacement, 180 days to close
- Qualified intermediary must hold proceeds

## Strategy 5: Legacy & Estate Tax Awareness

### Overview

The federal estate tax exemption for 2025 is $13.99 million per individual ($27.98 million for married couples). While this excludes most estates from federal estate tax, several considerations remain critical:

### Current Law Landscape (2025)

- **Federal exemption:** $13.99M individual / $27.98M married
- **Top federal estate tax rate:** 40%
- **Annual gift exclusion:** $19,000 per recipient
- **Sunset provision:** Current exemption levels are scheduled to revert to approximately half (around $7 million) after 2025 absent legislative action

### State Estate and Inheritance Taxes

Several states impose estate or inheritance taxes at much lower thresholds:

| State | Estate Tax Exemption |
|-------|---------------------|
| Oregon | $1 million |
| Massachusetts | $2 million |
| Maryland | $5 million |
| New York | $7.16 million |
| Washington | $2.193 million |

**Planning Implication:** A surgeon with a $10M estate may owe no federal tax but significant state tax depending on residence.

### Strategies for Legacy Planning

#### Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT)

- Irrevocable trust benefiting spouse
- Removes assets from estate while maintaining indirect access
- Locks in current high exemption amounts before potential sunset

#### Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

- Transfer appreciating assets with minimal gift tax
- Grantor receives annuity payments; remainder passes to heirs
- "Zeroed-out" GRATs can transfer appreciation tax-free

#### Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

- Keeps life insurance proceeds out of taxable estate
- Provides liquidity for estate taxes or equalization among heirs
- Must be established with proper formalities (Crummey notices, etc.)

#### Annual Gifting Program

- $19,000 per recipient, per year, free of gift tax
- Married couples can give $38,000 per recipient
- Removes future appreciation from estate

#### Charitable Strategies

**Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs):**
- Immediate deduction for contribution
- Flexibility in timing of charitable grants
- Ideal for "bunching" deductions in high-income years

**Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT):**
- Contribute appreciated assets
- Receive income stream for life or term of years
- Remainder passes to charity
- Avoids capital gains on contributed assets

**Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD):**
- Available at age 70½
- Up to $105,000 (2025) directly from IRA to charity
- Satisfies RMD without increasing taxable income

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The Solo Practitioner Maximizing Retirement Contributions

**Profile:** Dr. Martinez, 57, solo orthopedic practice, $950,000 net income

**Issue:** Maxing 401(k) at $31,000 (with catch-up), leaving significant tax exposure

**Strategy Applied:**
1. Implemented cash balance pension plan alongside 401(k)
2. Total contributions: $245,000 annually
3. Maintained reasonable salary with S-Corp distributions

**Tax Impact:**
- Additional deferred income: $214,000
- Tax savings at 37% bracket: ~$79,000 annually
- 7-year impact before retirement: $553,000+ in tax savings

### Case Study 2: The Group Practice Partner Planning Exit

**Profile:** Dr. Patel, 62, partner in 5-surgeon orthopedic group, planning to sell interest in 3 years

**Issue:** Buyout structure would create significant ordinary income

**Strategy Applied:**
1. Restructured buy-sell agreement to recognize personal goodwill
2. Planned installment sale over 5 years
3. Established residency in no-income-tax state before sale
4. Maximized cash balance contributions in final working years

**Tax Impact:**
- Buyout value: $2.8 million
- Without planning: ~$1.05M in taxes (federal + state)
- With planning: ~$520,000 in taxes
- **Total savings: ~$530,000**

### Case Study 3: The High-Net-Worth Surgeon Addressing Estate Concerns

**Profile:** Dr. and Mrs. Thompson, ages 60 and 58, combined estate $18 million

**Issue:** Potential estate tax exposure if exemptions sunset; state estate tax in current residence

**Strategy Applied:**
1. Established SLATs for each spouse, funding with $6 million total
2. Implemented ILIT with second-to-die policy
3. Relocated primary residence to Florida (no state income or estate tax)
4. Annual gifting program to children ($152,000/year to 2 children and spouses)

**Tax Impact:**
- Removed $6 million from estate (plus future growth)
- Eliminated state income tax on retirement distributions (~$30,000/year)
- Protected against exemption sunset
- **Projected estate tax savings: $2.4+ million**

## Summary Checklist

Use this checklist to guide conversations with your financial advisors and CPAs:

### Entity & Income Structure
- [ ] Is my practice structured optimally for tax purposes (S-Corp election)?
- [ ] Am I paying a "reasonable salary" that can be documented?
- [ ] Are there legitimate opportunities to employ family members?

### Retirement & Tax-Deferred Vehicles
- [ ] Am I maximizing all available retirement contributions?
- [ ] Would a cash balance plan provide significant additional deferral?
- [ ] Do I have a Roth conversion strategy for lower-income years?
- [ ] Am I utilizing backdoor Roth contributions?

### Investment Tax Optimization
- [ ] Are my investments properly located across account types?
- [ ] Is my advisor actively harvesting tax losses?
- [ ] Should I hold municipal bonds in taxable accounts?
- [ ] Do I have concentrated stock positions that need planning?

### Practice Exit & Pre-Liquidity Planning
- [ ] If I'm selling my practice, is personal goodwill being recognized?
- [ ] Should I consider an installment sale to spread income?
- [ ] What's the plan for accounts receivable and deferred income at retirement?
- [ ] Does a state relocation before sale make financial sense?

### Legacy & Estate Planning
- [ ] Is my estate plan updated for current (2025) exemption levels?
- [ ] Should we accelerate gifting before potential exemption sunset?
- [ ] Would a SLAT, GRAT, or ILIT benefit my situation?
- [ ] Are all beneficiary designations current and coordinated?

### Retirement Distribution Planning
- [ ] Do I have a tax-efficient withdrawal strategy across account types?
- [ ] Am I aware of Social Security taxation and Medicare IRMAA thresholds?
- [ ] Should I plan Qualified Charitable Distributions from my IRA?

### Contingency Planning
- [ ] What's our plan if estate exemptions are reduced?
- [ ] If tax rates increase, should we accelerate income or conversions?
- [ ] Is my insurance and asset protection current for my wealth level?

## Questions to Ask Your Advisors

Each of these questions opens dialogue on critical aspects of your financial plan:

- **Entity Structure:** "Is my practice entity structure optimal for tax purposes, or would an S-Corp election (or different allocation strategy) save me on self-employment taxes?"

- **Retirement Contributions:** "Am I maximizing retirement contributions? Could I benefit from a cash balance pension plan to shelter more income given my age and earnings?"

- **Investment Management:** "Is my investment portfolio tax-efficiently managed? Are we doing tax-loss harvesting, and are my assets located in the optimal account types (taxable vs. IRA vs. Roth)?"

- **Practice Exit Planning:** "What's the tax impact of my expected buyout or practice sale? Can we structure the deal to maximize capital gains treatment (e.g., personal goodwill allocation, stock sale)? Should I sell assets over time or in an installment to spread out income?"

- **State Relocation and Taxes:** "Do I plan to retire in a different state? If yes, how can we coordinate that move to reduce state tax on retirement distributions or the sale of my practice?"

- **Roth Conversion Gameplan:** "Once I retire (or in a low-income gap year), should we execute Roth conversions? What is our plan to manage RMDs and possibly convert IRA funds to Roth in the early retirement years when my tax bracket might be lower?"

- **Insurance and Asset Protection:** "Are my insurance policies and asset titling optimized to protect my wealth as I transition to retirement?"

- **Estate Plan & Gifting:** "Do I need to update my will and estate plan given current $13.99M exemption? Should we start gifting assets now to reduce future estate tax? Would a Spousal Lifetime Access Trust or irrevocable life insurance trust benefit my situation?"

- **Charitable Goals:** "Do I have charitable or legacy goals we should integrate? Should I set up a donor-advised fund or charitable trust? How can I donate appreciated assets instead of cash to be more tax-efficient?"

- **Cash Flow in Retirement:** "Can we project my retirement income needs and sources and see how to withdraw in a tax-savvy way? Which accounts should we tap first to minimize taxes over the long run?"

- **Contingency Planning:** "If tax laws change (like estate exemption dropping or new surcharges), do we have a plan B?"
