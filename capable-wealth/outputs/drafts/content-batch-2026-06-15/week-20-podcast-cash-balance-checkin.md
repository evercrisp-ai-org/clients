## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

A cash balance plan is the single largest tax shelter most surgeons will ever touch. It can absorb $150,000 to $350,000 a year, deductible, compounding tax-deferred. It also has a quiet trapdoor that the brochure never mentions.

Today we do a midyear check on that plan, because it is the one retirement structure that can actually punish you for improvising. The good news: if you are behind, July is exactly the right moment to find out.

---
## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

We are at the halfway point of the year, which is the natural moment to confirm that the biggest commitment in your plan is on schedule. For a 401(k), pacing is forgiving. For a cash balance plan, it is not, because the plan is a promise with an actuary attached.

At surgeon income, this is the structure that does the heaviest lifting. It is also the one where a funding gap caught in December is a genuine problem, and a funding gap caught in July is a simple fix. That gap between July and December is the whole reason for this episode.

---
## SEGMENT 1: A Promise, Not a Piggy Bank (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

The mental model that gets surgeons in trouble is treating a cash balance plan like a bigger 401(k). A 401(k) is flexible. Skip a month, dial it down, nothing breaks.

A cash balance plan is a defined benefit plan. An actuary sets a required contribution range every year, and the plan expects that range to be funded. It is a promise you made in January. Everything else today follows from taking that promise seriously.

---
## SEGMENT 2: The Pacing Math (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Picture a 55-year-old surgeon with a plan designed for $250,000 a year. To stay on schedule, that surgeon should be near $125,000 funded by mid-July.

If the real number is $40,000, that is not a rounding error. It means the funding plan and the payroll reality have drifted apart, and the back half of the year now has to carry more than it comfortably can. Caught now, you re-pace it across six months. Caught in December, you are trying to push six figures through payroll in the final weeks, which often simply cannot happen.

---
## SEGMENT 3: The Trapdoor (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Here is what underfunding actually costs, and it is the part most surgeons have never been told.

First, the deduction you built the plan to capture shrinks, so your current-year tax bill goes back up. Second, a funding deficiency can carry an excise tax. Third, and worst, a plan that repeatedly misses its required contributions can lose its qualified status, which unwinds years of tax-deferred treatment at once. None of that comes from recklessness. It comes from treating a year-long obligation like a year-end errand.

---
## SEGMENT 4: The Full Stack (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

When the cash balance plan is funded correctly, look at what it sits on top of. The 401(k) elective deferral is $24,500, with an $8,000 catch-up at 50 and older. The full defined contribution side can reach $72,000 with employer and profit-sharing dollars. Layer the cash balance plan on top, and a surgeon in their fifties can be deferring well past $300,000 a year.

That is the most powerful wealth-building structure available to a practice owner. It rewards consistency and punishes improvisation, which is exactly why the midyear confirmation matters.

---
## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

One action this week: pull your cash balance plan's annual target, find where you should be on the funding schedule right now, and compare it to what has actually gone in. If you are on pace, that is a fifteen-minute confirmation. If you are behind, book the call with your advisor and plan administrator this month, while six months of payroll can still close the gap.

---
## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

The cash balance plan is built for people who think in long horizons, which describes every surgeon I know. It rewards the same trait that makes you good at what you do, the discipline to honor a commitment on a schedule rather than in a scramble.

Control over your financial life is not built in one dramatic year-end move. It is built by confirming, at the midpoint, that the most important promise in your plan is being kept. Half the year is behind you. The other half is still yours to fund well.
