$250K of deferral only holds if the plan is funded on a real schedule.

A cash balance plan is the largest tax shelter most surgeons will ever have, $150K to $350K a year depending on design. It is also the only one that can punish you for treating contributions like a year-end decision.

Unlike a 401(k), it is a defined benefit plan. An actuary sets a required contribution range, and the plan expects that range funded across the year, not improvised in December.

A 55-year-old surgeon with a $250K plan should be near $125K funded by now. Sitting at $40K is not a rounding gap. It means the funding plan and the payroll reality have drifted apart, and the back half of the year now has to carry more than payroll comfortably can.

The cost of letting it slide is steep: a shrinking deduction, a possible excise tax on the shortfall, and in the worst case a plan that loses its qualified status and unwinds years of tax-deferred growth. None of that comes from recklessness, only from late attention.

Caught in July, it is a six-month re-pacing. Caught in December, it can be a contribution you cannot legally make.

Where does your plan sit against its halfway mark today?
