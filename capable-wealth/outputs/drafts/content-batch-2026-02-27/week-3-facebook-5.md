Weekend thought for anyone in orthopedics.

This year's CMS reimbursement cut is getting a lot of attention right now. And it should. It's real money. But I think the more important conversation is about what comes next.

The payment formula that Congress put in place through the OBBBA ties future Medicare updates to 75% of the cost index for running a practice. In plain terms: if your costs go up 3% next year, your reimbursement goes up about 2.25%. Every year, you fall a little further behind.

This isn't a one-time adjustment. It's a structural trajectory. And it means that the gap between what it costs to run your practice and what you're paid per procedure will widen gradually, year after year, for the remainder of most surgeons' careers.

I share this not to be discouraging. I share it because it changes the strategic question. If the reimbursement environment is permanently tightening, then optimizing your practice structure is no longer a one-time project. It's an ongoing discipline, something you revisit and refine every year, the same way you stay current on surgical techniques and evidence.

The surgeons who will build the most wealth over the next decade won't necessarily be the ones who operate the most. They'll be the ones who consistently pay attention to the full system: what comes in, what goes out, and how efficiently the difference reaches their personal balance sheet.

That kind of attention compounds, just like the savings it produces.
