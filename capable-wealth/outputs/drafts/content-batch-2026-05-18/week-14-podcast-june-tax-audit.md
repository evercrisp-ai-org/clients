## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

$35K underpaid by June 15 becomes a December problem. That is the kind of number that gets attention because it turns a planning concept into something you can feel.

Today we are going to take one issue that looks technical and turn it into a practical diagnostic for a surgeon practice owner. The goal is to know which decision deserves attention this week, even while the future remains uncertain.

---
## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

Sets up the June 15 Q2 estimated tax deadline as a planning moment ahead of the payment.

The reason this matters for surgeons is simple: the numbers are large, the calendar is unforgiving, and the planning windows close before most people feel ready. At this income level, delay is rarely neutral.

---
## SEGMENT 1: The June Number Is a Vital Sign (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

By late May, you have enough information to stop guessing. The full year is still developing, but you know your run rate, your practice distributions, your W-2 pacing, and whether Q1 was an anomaly or the start of a pattern. That is enough to calculate a useful estimate.

The practical question is what this means for the decisions in front of you right now. A good plan makes the next step obvious before the deadline makes it urgent.

---
## SEGMENT 2: Three Questions Before June 15 (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

First, is your year-to-date income tracking above or below projection? Second, are retirement contributions pacing with the tax plan or waiting for a year-end scramble? Third, does your cash reserve support the estimated payment without raiding operating liquidity?

A surgeon projected for $900,000 in 2026 income has paid only $145,000 through withholding and Q1 estimates. If the current-year tax projection is $315,000, the June 15 check cannot be guessed. A $35,000 shortfall by midyear can turn into penalties, cash strain, and rushed December decisions.

---
## SEGMENT 3: The 30-Minute Audit (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Pull year-to-date compensation, distributions, estimated payments already made, projected retirement contributions, and large known deductions. Put them next to last year, then ask what changed. The value is practical visibility. You see the gap before it becomes a December problem.

The practical question is what this means for the decisions in front of you right now. A good plan makes the next step obvious before the deadline makes it urgent.

---
## SEGMENT 4: What To Do With the Answer (3-5 minutes)

If you are underpaid, adjust the June 15 payment and the September plan. If you are overpaid, do not celebrate too quickly. Confirm whether the overpayment is real or just a timing issue. If retirement contributions are behind, decide whether payroll, employer contributions, or cash balance funding needs to change now.

The practical question is what this means for the decisions in front of you right now. A good plan makes the next step obvious before the deadline makes it urgent.

---
## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

One action this week: pull the numbers connected to this topic and place them on one page. If the page reveals a gap, name the decision, assign an owner, and set the next review date. That is how a planning rhythm begins.

---
## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

Financial planning works best when structure lets you make deliberate decisions while everyone else is guessing at headlines or deadlines.

That structure is what turns high income into control. And control is the point.
