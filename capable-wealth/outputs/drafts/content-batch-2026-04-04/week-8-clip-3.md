## Content

### Caption Script

If someone offers you a financial second opinion, here are the three questions to ask before you sit down.

Question one: what's your motive? A genuine second opinion is independent. The reviewer has no financial stake in recommending a change. If the "second opinion" comes with a pitch to move your assets or fire your CPA, that's a sales process wearing a lab coat. The cleanest reviews come from fee-only advisors who charge for the analysis itself, not for implementing the recommendations.

Question two: will the findings be specific? A real second opinion produces quantified results. "Your cash balance plan capacity is $225,000 and you're using zero of it" is a finding. "You're not optimized" is a fortune cookie. Ask for dollar amounts. Ask for the math.

Question three: will you respect my existing team? The purpose of a second opinion is to identify coordination gaps between your advisors and give them a better roadmap. If the reviewer's first recommendation is "you need to fire your CPA," that tells you more about the reviewer than about your CPA.

Independence. Specificity. Respect for the existing team. Those three criteria will tell you whether a second opinion is worth your time, or whether it's just a sales pitch in disguise.
