## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

I'm about to say something that might sound self-serving coming from a financial advisor: I think every surgeon should get an independent second opinion on their financial plan.

Your current advisor may be excellent. Your CPA may be catching everything in their lane. The cost of skipping an independent second read, at your income level, is still too high to leave to assumption.

You already know this intuitively. You've built an entire career around the principle that a second set of eyes catches what the first set misses. Complex imaging, ambiguous pathology, a surgical plan that doesn't sit quite right. You pick up the phone. You send the study. You ask a colleague to take a fresh look.

You do this because the stakes are high enough to warrant it. Your financial structure carries the same stakes. And yet most surgeons have never subjected it to the same discipline.

Today, I want to walk through why a financial second opinion matters, what it should cover, what it shouldn't look like, and how to tell the difference between a genuine review and a disguised sales pitch. Let's get into it.

## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

April 15 falls this week. Whether you filed, extended, or are wrapping things up right now, this is one of those natural inflection points where it's worth stepping back from the compliance work and asking a broader question.

That question is simple: has anyone ever looked at my full financial picture, all at once, with fresh eyes?

For most surgeons, the answer is no. The reason is structural, not personal. Financial services are built in silos. Your CPA does your taxes. Your advisor manages your portfolio. Your attorney handles your estate plan. Each one does their job well. Nobody is looking at how those pieces interact.

The clinical analogy is exact. Imagine sending a patient's knee MRI to an orthopedist, their bloodwork to an internist, and their cardiac history to a cardiologist, but never having a single physician look at all three together. You'd never practice medicine that way. But most surgeons manage their finances exactly that way.

The financial second opinion is the integrated read.

Let me start with why the second opinion concept resonates so naturally with surgeons, because the parallel is almost exact.

You've ordered hundreds of second opinions in your career. Have you ever gotten one on your financial plan?

In clinical practice, a second opinion serves a specific purpose. You're not asking a colleague to tell you that the first reading was wrong. You're asking them to look at the same data with different training, different pattern recognition, and a different vantage point. The value comes from completeness, from seeing what the first reader, through no fault of their own, wasn't positioned to see.

Financial planning at surgeon income levels carries similar stakes. The difference between an optimized and an unoptimized financial structure at $800,000 in annual income can be $50,000 to $80,000 per year. Over a decade, that's half a million dollars or more. Over a career, it compounds into a fundamentally different retirement.

And yet. Most surgeons have a financial structure that was set up years ago, reviewed annually for compliance, and never subjected to an independent, integrated review.

That gap between clinical rigor and financial passivity is where the second opinion creates value.

## SEGMENT 2: The Four Areas a Second Opinion Should Cover (3-5 minutes)

A good financial second opinion isn't a general "check-up." It's a structured assessment of four specific areas that together determine how much of your income you keep and how efficiently your wealth compounds.

**Area one: entity structure.** Is your S-Corp salary calibrated against your retirement plan capacity? Is your entity designed for your current practice reality, or for the practice you had when the CPA set it up? If you're within five years of a potential practice transition, is the entity positioned for an optimal sale?

**Area two: retirement plan design.** Are you using the full contribution capacity available to you? Most surgeons contribute $31,000 per year through a 401(k) and stop there. But at surgeon income levels, the available capacity with a cash balance plan layered on top is $180,000 to $280,000. That gap, the unused capacity, is the single most common finding in a financial second opinion.

**Area three: tax strategy.** Is your effective tax rate where it should be at your income level? Are you converting income from capital gains treatment to ordinary income through structural decisions without realizing it? Is tax-loss harvesting being coordinated between your investment accounts and your tax return?

**Area four: investment allocation.** Not stock-picking. Coordination. Are taxable accounts holding tax-inefficient assets? Is the portfolio risk calibrated to your timeline? Is the investment strategy talking to the tax strategy, or are they operating in separate rooms?

Each of these areas, individually, might be handled competently by the professional responsible for it. The second opinion looks at how they interact. Because the interactions are where the value lives.

## SEGMENT 3: The Silo Gap (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

This brings me to what I think of as the silo gap, and it's the core of why a financial second opinion matters.

Your CPA is great at compliance. Your advisor is great at investments. Your attorney is great at estate documents. But who's looking at the whole picture?

In most cases, nobody. And here's why that's expensive.

Your S-Corp salary affects your retirement plan capacity. Your retirement plan capacity affects your tax bill. Your tax strategy affects which accounts your investments should sit in. Your investment allocation affects your estate plan. These are interconnected systems, and optimizing any one of them in isolation can actually make another one worse.

Let me give you a concrete example. Consider a surgeon earning $850,000, with a CPA who recommends keeping the S-Corp salary at $280,000 to minimize payroll taxes. Good advice from a tax compliance perspective. But that $280,000 W-2 also caps the employer match on the 401(k) and limits the cash balance plan contribution formula. The payroll tax savings might be $9,000 per year. The lost retirement plan capacity might be $40,000 per year.

The CPA made a sound recommendation within their domain. The problem is that the domain was too narrow. Nobody looked at how the salary decision interacted with the retirement plan math.

That's the silo gap. It's not incompetence. It's structural. Each professional optimizes their piece. The gaps between the pieces are where the money leaks.

A financial second opinion reads the full composite study, not the individual images. It looks at entity, retirement, tax, and investment as a single integrated system. And it identifies the interactions that no single advisor, working within their domain, is positioned to catch.

## SEGMENT 4: What to Look For (and What to Watch Out For) (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

If someone offers you a financial second opinion, here are the three questions to ask before you sit down.

**Question one: what's your motive?** A genuine second opinion is independent. The reviewer has no financial stake in recommending a change. If the "second opinion" comes with a pitch to move your assets, fire your CPA, or transfer your accounts, that's not a review. That's a sales process wearing a lab coat.

The cleanest second opinions come from fee-only advisors or consultants who charge for the review itself, not for implementing the recommendations. The payment is for the analysis, not the outcome.

**Question two: will the findings be specific?** A real second opinion produces quantified findings. "Your cash balance plan capacity is $225,000 and you're using $0 of it" is a finding. "You're not optimized" is a fortune cookie.

Ask for specifics. Ask for dollar amounts. Ask for the math. If the person offering the second opinion can't describe what the deliverable will look like, they probably don't have a structured process for producing it.

**Question three: will you respect my existing team?** The purpose of a second opinion is not to replace your CPA, advisor, or attorney. It's to identify the coordination gaps between them and give the team a better roadmap.

The best outcome of a financial second opinion is that your existing professionals gain clarity on how their work interconnects with each other's. If the reviewer's first recommendation is "you should fire your CPA," that tells you more about the reviewer than about your CPA.

Those are the three things that tell you whether a second opinion is worth your time. Independence, specificity, and respect for the existing team.

## SEGMENT 5: The Math (3-5 minutes)

Let me walk through a scenario that illustrates what a second opinion typically reveals at surgeon income levels.

Consider a surgeon earning $850,000 through an S-Corp. The current strategy includes a 401(k) with $31,000 in annual contributions (the 2026 maximum with catch-up), a SEP contribution, and individual investment accounts managed by a separate advisor.

A second-opinion review of this structure identifies three findings.

Finding one: a cash balance plan opportunity. The surgeon is over 50 with no cash balance plan. Adding one, on top of the existing 401(k), allows an additional $150,000 to $250,000 in annual tax-deferred contributions. At a 42% combined marginal rate, the immediate tax savings on $200,000 in additional sheltered income is $84,000. Per year.

Finding two: a SEP restructuring. The existing SEP contribution is conflicting with the 401(k) employer contribution formula. Restructuring the SEP into a profit-sharing component within the 401(k) plan eliminates the conflict and frees up $15,000 to $25,000 in additional contribution capacity for the cash balance plan.

Finding three: tax-loss harvesting coordination. The individual investment accounts have $180,000 in unrealized losses that have never been harvested because the investment advisor and the CPA don't coordinate. Systematically harvesting over two years offsets $180,000 in gains and saves approximately $43,000 in capital gains taxes at the 23.8% rate.

Total Year 1 value: $50,000 to $80,000. Over a decade, the compounding benefit of the cash balance plan alone pushes the cumulative value past $400,000.

And here's the part that matters: none of these findings reflect badly on the CPA or the advisor. The CPA wasn't asked about retirement plan design. The advisor wasn't looking at the tax return. Each was doing their job well. The opportunities existed in the spaces between them.

You already practice the discipline of seeking second opinions where the stakes are high. You do it in clinical practice because you know that completeness matters more than confidence. A second perspective catches what the first one, through no fault of its own, wasn't positioned to see.

Apply that same discipline to your financial structure. Surgeon-level planning, where entity design, retirement vehicles, tax strategy, and investment allocation all interact, is exactly the kind of case where a second set of eyes changes the outcome.

April 15 falls this week. Whether you're filing or extending, this is a natural moment to ask one question: has anyone ever looked at my full financial picture, all at once, with fresh eyes?

If not, the second opinion is worth requesting. The math tends to make the case on its own.

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

The best surgeons I know aren't threatened by a second opinion. They're strengthened by it. They understand that seeking another perspective isn't a sign of doubt. It's a sign of discipline. It's the acknowledgment that the stakes are high enough, and the case is complex enough, that a single reading may not be sufficient.

Your financial plan deserves that same standard.

If your strategy is sound, a second opinion confirms it. You walk away with confidence that your structure is working. If your strategy has gaps, and at surgeon income levels there are almost always gaps in the coordination between advisors, you'd rather know now than in ten years.

The cost of a second opinion is a few hours of your time. The cost of not getting one, based on the math, tends to be measured in hundreds of thousands of dollars over a career.

The same rigor you bring to the OR, the same insistence on completeness, the same willingness to say "let me get another set of eyes on this," that's what separates the surgeons who build wealth from the surgeons who just earn a high income.

Bring that rigor home.
