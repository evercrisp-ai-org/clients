## Content

Consider a surgeon approaching 52 — $870,000 in annual practice income, sixteen years out of fellowship, solidly established.

His finances look responsible on the surface: maxing his 401(k), working with a CPA, reviewing his investments once a year. His advisor calls it "on track."

When we map where his $510,000 in after-tax income actually went last year, the pattern is familiar. Primary residence mortgage, $4,800/month. Lake house carrying costs, $3,100/month. Two car payments, $2,900/month. Two children's private school tuitions. Club membership. Annual family travel. None of it unreasonable individually.

Collectively, $446,000 of his $510,000 in after-tax income — 87.5% — exits through lifestyle expenses before a compounding dollar is captured.

He's not extravagant by his peer group's standards. His colleagues drive comparable vehicles, belong to the same clubs, vacation in similar places. His spending isn't a decision problem. The parking lot set his benchmark. His financial structure has no mechanism to override it.

$64,000 compounded toward wealth last year. The structure available to him — a properly funded cash balance plan, an optimized S-Corp salary, automated investment contributions before distributions reach checking — could have captured $200,000.

The gap between "on track" and genuinely building wealth isn't income. It's architecture.

When did you last calculate your actual spending rate, not your intention to spend less?
