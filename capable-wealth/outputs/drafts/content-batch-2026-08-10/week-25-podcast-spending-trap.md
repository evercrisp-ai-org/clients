## Content

### COLD OPEN (target: ~45 seconds)

Let me tell you about a parking lot.

A few years back, I was visiting a regional orthopedic hospital — nothing unusual, just a meeting. I arrived at 7:30 in the morning. The early shift was coming in. And I found myself standing in the parking structure, watching the cars arrive.

In about forty minutes, I counted somewhere close to $2 million worth of vehicles rolling in. Three European luxury SUVs. Two sports sedans that together retail for more than the median orthopedic surgeon earns in a year. A pickup truck that cost more than many people's first homes.

Nobody thought anything of it. It was Tuesday. This was just the parking lot.

I want to spend the next twenty minutes or so unpacking what that parking lot is telling you — about your finances, about your structure, and about what's actually happening between the income you earn and the wealth you're building.

---

### INTRO AND CONTEXT (target: ~90 seconds)

Here's the uncomfortable truth about high income: it does not automatically produce wealth. That feels obvious when you say it, but the behavior of high-income professionals suggests it's not actually internalized.

Orthopedic surgeons are, by every measure, among the highest-earning physicians in medicine. Median compensation is around $800,000. Many earn more. That income, structured well, produces the kind of wealth that funds a genuinely free life — the ability to practice on your terms, leave a partnership that isn't working, take six months off without financial catastrophe.

Structured poorly — or not structured at all — that same income runs through your life like water through a sieve. You earn more, you spend more, and the balance sheet doesn't move much.

Today's episode is about the mechanism that causes that. The raccoon trap. The peer comparison engine. The invisible drains. And most importantly, the structural fix that turns the same income into a materially different outcome.

Not through discipline. Through design.

---

### SEGMENT 1: The Raccoon Trap at Surgeon Scale [CLIP-CANDIDATE]

(target: ~4 minutes)

Let me explain the raccoon trap if you haven't heard me use this metaphor before.

There's a method for catching raccoons that works like this: you put something shiny in a hole in a tree. The raccoon reaches in, grabs it, makes a fist. And now it's trapped, because it won't let go of the shiny thing, and its fist is too big to pull out of the hole. The raccoon could free itself at any moment by releasing the object. It doesn't.

At surgeon income levels, the shiny objects are more sophisticated. But the mechanism is identical.

Here's the math. Take an $800K surgeon — which is right around median compensation. After federal and state taxes at a combined effective rate of roughly 40%, you're working with approximately $480,000 in after-tax income per year. That's your raw material. That's what you have to direct.

Now — and this is the diagnostic question I want you to hold onto — what percentage of that $480,000 is actively compounding toward wealth right now?

[CLIP-CANDIDATE — The raccoon trap math, approximately 55 seconds:]

$480,000. That's what an $800K surgeon takes home after taxes. The average surgeon is directing about $60,000 of that per year toward wealth accumulation. The other $420,000? That's going to the parking lot.

At an 87.5% spending rate, the math is: $420,000 exits through lifestyle expenses, $60,000 remains to compound. At 75%, the same income produces $360,000 in lifestyle and $120,000 compounding. The gap is $60,000 per year.

Over 10 years at 6%, that $60,000 annual gap becomes $791,000 in accumulated wealth difference. Same income. Same tax rate. Same specialty. Two very different balance sheets.

[END CLIP-CANDIDATE]

The surgeons who end up with the larger number didn't out-earn the others. They built a structure that captured more before it reached the spending pool. That's the only difference. And it's entirely within your control.

But to get there, we have to understand what's actually driving the 87.5% spending rate. Because it's not what most people think it is.

### SEGMENT 2: The Three Invisible Drains [CLIP-CANDIDATE]

(target: ~5 minutes)

When I talk to surgeons about their finances, the conversation almost always starts in the same place. They're frustrated about taxes. They feel like they're doing everything right — contributing to their 401(k), working with a CPA, maybe seeing a financial advisor — and the balance sheet just isn't growing the way they expected.

And then we map the actual flows. Where is the $480,000 actually going?

Three drains show up almost every time. Not one big thing. Three structural leaks that most people have never explicitly measured.

The first is lifestyle inflation that tracks income.

When compensation moves from $600,000 to $900,000, lifestyle spending tends to follow at roughly the same rate. Not because anyone made a deliberate decision to spend more. Because of social anchoring.

When you spend your days around colleagues who have comparable incomes and visible lifestyles, your reference point calibrates to that group. A new vehicle feels like a reasonable reward for a difficult year. A home renovation feels like a normal next step. A second property seems sensible. Each individual decision is defensible. The pattern is the trap.

The parking lot isn't causing you to make bad decisions. The parking lot is showing you what happens when nobody made a deliberate countervailing decision upstream.

The second drain: insurance and protection overhead.

At surgeon income levels, the combined cost of malpractice coverage, disability policies, life insurance, umbrella coverage, and practice overhead frequently runs $80,000 to $120,000 per year. This is legitimate. It's necessary. I'm not suggesting you underinsure.

What I am suggesting is that this portfolio of coverage almost never gets reviewed for efficiency. Surgeons set up their insurance programs when they joined a practice or opened their own, and those programs run on autopilot for years. Duplicated coverage, over-insured positions, improperly structured policies — these silently drain $15,000 to $30,000 annually for many practice owners. The premiums get paid. Nobody revisits the structure.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE — The savings illusion, approximately 50 seconds:]

The third drain is savings that feel real but aren't real at surgeon scale.

A 401(k) contribution of $32,500 per year — the standard amount with the age-50 catch-up — represents less than 7% of an $800,000 surgeon's gross income. Most surgeons feel like they're saving, because relative to the general population, they are. But there's a vehicle that could shelter 10 to 15 times that contribution in a single year — a cash balance plan — that the majority of surgeon practice owners have never set up. The gap between what's being contributed and what could be contributed is often $150,000 to $250,000 per year in foregone tax-deferred compounding.

That gap doesn't feel like a leak because nothing is visibly leaving your account. But it's the most expensive drain of the three.

[END CLIP-CANDIDATE]

Three invisible drains. One structural fix. That's what the next segment is about.

### SEGMENT 3: Architecture vs. Behavior [CLIP-CANDIDATE]

(target: ~4 minutes)

Here's the thing about spending traps: they're almost never solved by discipline.

I've worked with high-income professionals long enough to know that discipline-based solutions to structural problems produce temporary results at best. If the system funnels your entire after-tax income through a checking account, and lifestyle expenses have unlimited access to that account, willpower is fighting against gravity. Every day.

The fix is architectural. You move the capture point upstream.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE — The architectural fix, approximately 60 seconds:]

The raccoon can't grab what it can't reach. A structural solution to a spending problem removes the decision from the moment of temptation and embeds it in a mechanism that runs before the money is available.

Three specific interventions for surgeons:

One: a cash balance plan. When properly designed and funded, this captures $150,000 to $300,000 per year in tax-deferred contributions before your W-2 is calculated. That money never enters the lifestyle spending pool. It doesn't exist as a temptation.

Two: S-Corp salary calibration that optimizes for contribution capacity, not just payroll tax minimization. The salary sets the floor for retirement plan contributions. Getting it right typically unlocks $20,000 to $60,000 in additional annual contribution room.

Three: an automated transfer from practice distributions to a taxable investment account before those distributions hit your primary checking account. Same principle — remove the decision from the moment.

None of these are discipline. All of them are architecture.

[END CLIP-CANDIDATE]

Surgeons understand system design. You wouldn't run an operating room on willpower. You built processes, checklists, equipment configurations that produce consistent outcomes. Your financial life deserves the same approach.

---

### SEGMENT 4: The Parking Lot Question [CLIP-CANDIDATE]

(target: ~3 minutes)

Let me come back to that parking lot for a moment.

The parking lot isn't just a metaphor for spending. It's a measurement instrument. It tells you who your financial reference group is. And your financial reference group is one of the most powerful drivers of your spending behavior — possibly more powerful than any deliberate financial decision you've ever made.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE — The peer comparison engine, approximately 50 seconds:]

Here's the question I want you to sit with: who is your financial peer group right now?

For most surgeons, the answer is: the people in the parking lot. Colleagues at the hospital. Partners at the practice. The people you see every day. And these people represent a very specific segment of the income distribution — the top 1%, with visible consumption habits calibrated to that level.

The problem is that peer comparison doesn't operate on net worth. It operates on visible signals. The car. The house. The watch. The vacation. And when those signals are your daily environment, they become your floor, not your ceiling.

The surgeons I've seen build the largest wealth relative to their income — and I mean the ones who can genuinely walk away from their practice on their own terms — are almost uniformly people who opted out of the parking lot as a financial benchmark. Not by driving cheaper cars or being secretly frugal. By having a different reference point for what "enough" looks like.

[END CLIP-CANDIDATE]

That reference point is a financial plan. Specifically, a number — an actual net worth figure, by a specific age, tied to what you want your life to look like after the OR. Without that number, the parking lot fills the vacuum. It becomes the plan by default.

---

### LISTENER TAKEAWAY (target: ~90 seconds)

One specific thing I want you to do this week.

Calculate your spending rate.

Take your approximate after-tax income — gross compensation minus your effective combined federal and state tax rate. For most surgeons earning $700,000 to $1.2 million, you're working with something between $400,000 and $700,000 after taxes.

Now look at what compounded last year. Retirement plan contributions, taxable investment accounts, equity added to real estate you hold as investment (not primary residence). What was that number?

Divide it by your after-tax income. That's your savings rate. Subtract from 100% to get your spending rate.

If that number is between 10% and 15%, you're in the typical range for high-income professionals. You're saving, in the absolute sense. You are not building wealth at a rate proportional to your income.

If it's 20% or higher, you're in the group that's going to have structural choices other surgeons won't.

You don't have to change anything today. Just know the number.

---

### PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (target: ~90 seconds)

Here's what I want to leave you with.

The goal of structuring your finances this way — capturing more before it reaches the spending pool, building a cash balance plan, calibrating your S-Corp salary — is not to accumulate a large number. The number is a means to something else.

Control.

Control means practicing on your terms. Doing the cases you want to do, in the settings you prefer, with the patients you can genuinely help. Not because you need the RVUs. Because you want to be there.

It means being able to leave a partnership that isn't right for you, without that decision being financially impossible. It means being able to take time off after a brutal stretch without calculating how many procedures you'll need to bill to recover.

The surgeon parking lot full of vehicles is not the enemy of wealth. What it represents — an unexamined, peer-calibrated spending pattern in a system with no capture mechanism upstream — that's the thing worth examining.

Build the structure before the spending fills the space. The parking lot will still be there. You just won't be taking financial advice from it.
