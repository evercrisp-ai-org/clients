## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

When you sell your practice, the IRS is going to look at the total price and ask one question: how much of this was the surgeon, and how much was the business?

Your answer to that question, backed by documentation you either have or don't have, could mean the difference between a $950,000 tax bill and a $1,280,000 tax bill. That's a $330,000 gap. On the same practice, at the same sale price, with the same buyer.

And here's what makes this one different from most tax planning conversations: the window to act isn't measured in quarters. It's measured in years. If you're planning to sell your practice in the next three to five years, the most valuable tax strategy available to you is one that requires starting now. Today we're going to talk about personal goodwill, what it is, why it matters, and why the documentation timeline is the thing that catches most surgeons off guard.

## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

Let me set the stage. Conference season is active. Many of you have just come back from meetings where practice transitions were the topic in every hallway conversation. PE firms are buying dinner. Colleagues are sharing deal stories. And the numbers being discussed sound compelling.

But there's a number that almost never comes up in those conversations. The tax bill. Specifically, the difference between what you pay in taxes when your practice sale is properly structured and what you pay when it isn't.

The single largest driver of that difference, for a surgeon selling a practice, is something called personal goodwill. It's not a new concept. Tax attorneys and CPAs who specialize in practice transitions have been using it for decades. But the vast majority of surgeons I analyze practice structures for have never heard of it. And by the time they hear about it, the documentation window has often closed.

So today I want to walk through three things. First, what personal goodwill actually is and why the IRS cares about the distinction. Second, the specific math at surgeon-level practice values. And third, what you need to document and when you need to start.

## SEGMENT 1: What Personal Goodwill Is and Why It Matters (3-5 minutes)

Let's start with the concept. When you sell a practice, the sale price represents the total value of everything the buyer is acquiring. Equipment, real estate, patient records, contracts, staff agreements, the brand, the systems. All of that is enterprise goodwill. It's the value of the business as a going concern, separate from any individual.

But there's another category of value that's embedded in most surgeon-owned practices: personal goodwill. This is the value attributable to you as an individual. Your surgical reputation. Your referral network. The primary care physicians and sports medicine colleagues who send patients to your practice specifically because of your name and your clinical results. Your patient loyalty. Your community standing.

Here's the critical distinction. Personal goodwill walks out the door when you do. If you retired tomorrow and a replacement surgeon took over, the patients who came because of your reputation, the referrals that flowed through your personal relationships, and the clinical reputation you built over decades would leave with you. That's personal goodwill.

Enterprise goodwill stays with the practice. The building, the systems, the staff, the payer contracts, the brand. A new surgeon steps in and those assets continue to generate value. That's enterprise goodwill.

Why does this matter for taxes? Because the IRS taxes these two categories differently. Personal goodwill, when properly documented and allocated, is taxed as a long-term capital gain. That means 20% federal plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, for a combined rate of 23.8%. Enterprise goodwill, depending on how it's allocated across different asset classes, can be taxed at ordinary income rates reaching 37% federal plus the 3.8% NIIT, for a combined rate up to 40.8%.

At surgeon-level practice values, the rate difference between 23.8% and 40.8% translates to hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## SEGMENT 2: The Math at Surgeon-Level Practice Values (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Let me walk through the specific numbers. Consider a practice sale at $4 million. That's a realistic number for an established orthopedic practice with strong collections and a favorable market.

Scenario one: no personal goodwill documentation. The entire $4 million is allocated across enterprise assets. The blended tax rate across different asset categories averages approximately 32%. Total tax: $1,280,000. The surgeon nets $2,720,000.

Scenario two: proper personal goodwill documentation supporting a 40% allocation. $1.6 million is classified as personal goodwill and taxed at 23.8%. That's $380,800. The remaining $2.4 million in enterprise assets is taxed at the blended 32%. That's $768,000. Total tax: $1,148,800. The surgeon nets $2,851,200.

The difference: $131,200. Same sale. Same buyer. Same price. The only variable was documentation that existed before the transaction began.

Now let's push the allocation higher. If the documentation supports a 55% personal goodwill allocation, which is achievable for a surgeon whose reputation genuinely drives the practice's patient volume, the personal goodwill portion is $2.2 million taxed at 23.8%, and the remaining $1.8 million at 32%. Total tax drops to about $1.1 million. The surgeon keeps roughly $2.9 million, saving over $180,000 compared to the no-documentation scenario.

Across the realistic range of defensible allocations, the tax savings from personal goodwill documentation sit between $130,000 and $350,000. That's the hidden tax bill in every practice sale. It's not hidden because it's complicated. It's hidden because nobody tells surgeons about it until it's too late to document.

## SEGMENT 3: Why the IRS Cares About Timing (3-5 minutes)

Here's the part that catches surgeons off guard. You can't assert personal goodwill during the sale and have it apply retroactively. The IRS scrutinizes the timing of personal goodwill documentation, and courts have consistently ruled against allocations that appear to have been manufactured for a pending transaction.

Let me give you a real-world example of how this plays out. In one frequently cited case, a professional services firm sold for several million dollars. The selling owners attempted to allocate 75% of the sale price to personal goodwill. Their documentation? A non-compete agreement signed the same week as the purchase agreement. A referral analysis conducted specifically for the transaction. No prior formal separation of personal and enterprise goodwill.

The court reduced the allocation to 15%. The reasoning: the documentation was contemporaneous with the sale, which suggested it was created to support a tax position rather than to reflect a pre-existing economic reality. The tax difference in that case exceeded $400,000.

The principle is clear. The IRS and tax courts look at three things when evaluating personal goodwill claims.

First, was the documentation created before any acquisition discussions began? If the non-compete agreement, the referral analysis, and the appraisal all appear in the same quarter as the letter of intent, the claim is vulnerable.

Second, does the documentation reflect a genuine, pre-existing economic reality? Personal goodwill must actually exist. The surgeon must genuinely be the driver of patient volume, referral relationships, and practice reputation. The documentation should measure something real, not manufacture a fiction.

Third, is the allocation reasonable relative to the practice's actual dependency on the individual surgeon? A solo practitioner with 25 years of community presence can credibly support a 50-60% personal goodwill allocation. A surgeon who joined a large group two years ago and shares patients across six partners probably cannot.

## SEGMENT 4: The Three Documentation Components (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Three things you need to document now if you plan to sell your practice in the next five years.

Component one: the non-compete agreement. This is between you and your practice entity. It establishes that you, as an individual, have value to the practice that would be lost if you competed against it. The agreement should be executed well before any buyer conversations. Two years minimum. Three is better. The timing of this agreement is often the single most scrutinized element in a personal goodwill dispute.

Component two: the referral source analysis. Track where your patients come from. Specifically, track how many new patients arrive through relationships tied to you personally versus through the practice's institutional channels. If 55% to 65% of new patient referrals flow through physicians who refer because of your individual reputation, that's a quantifiable measure of personal goodwill. The analysis should cover at least 24 months of referral data to establish a credible, consistent pattern.

Component three: the personal goodwill appraisal. This is a formal valuation by a qualified appraiser who separates the practice's total goodwill into personal and enterprise components. The standard methodology is the "with and without" approach: what is the practice worth with you, and what would it be worth without you? The gap is your personal goodwill. This appraisal should be completed at least 18 to 24 months before any anticipated sale process.

The total documentation timeline: 12 to 24 months to build the full package. Longer is better for credibility. And the work can be done alongside normal practice operations. It doesn't require a buyer. It doesn't require a timeline. It only requires the decision to start.

One concept. Three documentation steps. A potential savings of $130,000 to $350,000 on your practice sale.

If you're three to five years from a potential practice transition, whether that's a PE deal, a sale to a hospital system, a merger with another practice, or a transition to a junior partner, start the personal goodwill documentation process this quarter.

Step one: execute a non-compete agreement between you and your practice entity. Step two: begin systematic tracking of referral sources. Step three: schedule a personal goodwill appraisal for next year, once you have at least 18 months of referral data.

The cost of this process is measured in thousands. The value is measured in hundreds of thousands. And the only thing that makes it unavailable is waiting too long to start.

Every skill you've developed, every relationship you've built, every patient who chose your practice because of your name. That's personal goodwill. And it deserves the same careful, documented treatment you bring to every surgical plan.

Document it. Because undocumented goodwill is worth zero in a tax court. And zero, at the income levels we're talking about, costs more than most surgeons realize.
