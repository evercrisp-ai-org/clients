## Content

$560,000 in practice value gone before a single negotiation started.

Consider a surgeon with a practice generating $1.45 million in normalized EBITDA. At a 4x multiple, that practice is worth $5.8 million. Straightforward.

Now a buyer runs their productivity stress test. They model forward revenue assuming a replacement surgeon operating at the hospital benchmark rather than at the seller's personal RVU output. A 10 percent revenue haircut reduces the normalized EBITDA to $1.31 million. At 4x, the practice is worth $5.24 million.

$560,000. The gap traces back to a market benchmark that shifted and a practice that hadn't responded to it.

This is how sophisticated buyers are building acquisition models right now. The hospital productivity benchmark has become the revenue stress-test baseline. A practice that outperforms it signals to buyers that productivity will hold post-transaction. A practice that tracks below it carries a recovery assumption that gets priced in at signing.

The surgeons best positioned for exit are the ones whose RVU data tells a story of independence premium: productivity that outperforms the employed model and signals transferable value.

Where does your practice's RVU output sit relative to the current hospital benchmark?
