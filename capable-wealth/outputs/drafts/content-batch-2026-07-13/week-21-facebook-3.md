## Content

Consider a surgeon who built an excellent independent practice over 20 years. Strong referral base, consistent procedure volume, a reputation in the community that took two decades to build.

When it came time to explore an exit, the offer came in lower than expected. The practice's financials were clean, the buyer was credible, and the multiple was fair. What moved the number was the buyer's productivity stress test.

Hospital-employed orthopedists in the same market were generating more work RVUs. The buyer modeled forward revenue using that benchmark and adjusted the valuation accordingly.

This scenario plays out routinely now. The hospital benchmark has become a standard input in acquisition models. A practice that outperforms it holds its value. A practice that tracks below it absorbs a discount.

The benchmarking data exists. The question is whether you have looked at it before a buyer does.
