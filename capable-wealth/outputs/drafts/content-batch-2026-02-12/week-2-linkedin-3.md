There's a deduction worth up to $29,800 per year for a surgeon earning $745K. Most practice owners either don't have it or have a smaller version of it than they should.

It's the Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction: 20% of your pass-through income, subject to limitations based on W-2 wages and the type of business.

Here's where it gets interesting for S-Corp owners.

The QBI deduction is calculated on the income that passes through your S-Corp after your salary is paid out. That means the salary you pay yourself directly affects the size of the deduction.

Set the salary too high, and you shrink the pass-through income (reducing QBI). Set it too low, and you may trigger the W-2 wage limitation (also reducing QBI).

There's a range in the middle where the deduction is maximized. At $745K in total practice income, the difference between an unoptimized salary and a calibrated one can mean $10K to $20K in additional annual deductions.

Most CPAs set the salary for payroll tax compliance. That's their job. But the QBI calculation requires a second lens: what salary ratio produces the largest deduction while still satisfying the retirement plan math?

If your S-Corp salary has been the same for more than two years, the QBI deduction is almost certainly not optimized. The variables have changed, even if your salary hasn't.

This is Q1. The right time to model it.
