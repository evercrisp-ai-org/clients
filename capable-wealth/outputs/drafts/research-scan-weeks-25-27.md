**Editorial plan source:** Month 7 (September 2026), Content 1

### Freshness Assessment

**Timing: Publish in late August to land before the September 15 Q3 estimated tax deadline.**

The editorial plan's September theme opens with the estate planning piece ("The $15 Million Question"). Publishing Aug 17 gives the surgeon a 4-week runway before the Q3 estimated tax payment (September 15), which is the September content's natural deadline anchor. Estate planning content doesn't have a September 15 tie-in specifically, but the editorial plan's September arc is a coordinated set: publishing the estate piece first, then Roth conversions (Week 27), then the October 15 extended deadline piece (Week 28), follows the logical sequence from long-horizon planning to near-term execution.

This timing is correct: late August is when the estate planning conversation starts, ahead of Q4 when the year-end execution pressure begins. The estate piece establishes the "why" before the Roth and extended deadline pieces deliver the "what to do this fall."

**Relevance: Excellent. OBBBA's permanent exemption is the biggest estate planning story of 2026 — still fresh and still underprocessed by most surgeons.**

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's permanent increase to $15M/$30M exemption was signed July 4, 2025. More than a year later, most orthopedic surgeons have heard about it but haven't examined the second-order implications: (1) they may now be "under the line" federally but still exposed to state estate taxes, (2) the absence of a sunset provision changes planning posture, (3) OBBBA's permanence removes the clock-driven urgency that previously motivated estate planning — which is actually dangerous, because it creates planning complacency. The angle: the new exemption doesn't eliminate estate planning. It changes what you're planning for.

**Angle refinements:**
- The editorial plan's working title is "The $15 Million Question: What the New Permanent Estate Exemption Means for Your Family." This title is strong and should be kept.
- The reframe that makes this piece timely for late August (vs. any other month): surgeons returning from summer often do financial reviews in August-September. The estate planning conversation fits naturally as part of that review — "you've looked at your mid-year income, your Q2 estimated tax, your open enrollment. Now look at the one financial structure most surgeons haven't touched since 2021."
- Expand the state estate tax angle: 17 states + DC still have estate taxes with exemptions as low as $1M (Massachusetts, Oregon). A surgeon with a $5M net worth may be entirely below the federal exemption but fully exposed at the state level. This is the most immediate action item for surgeons in those states.
- Step-up in basis optimization: with the exemption now permanent at $15M/$30M, a common planning response is to "undo" aggressive gifting strategies that were built around the old sunset cliff. This creates an opportunity to revisit irrevocable trust assets and evaluate whether the step-up in basis they would have received at death is more valuable than the transfer tax savings the trust was designed to capture.
- Brief mention of the ILIT (Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust): the editorial plan has a dedicated October piece on ILITs. Don't crowd the October piece — mention ILITs here only as a transitional reference ("there are specific structures worth revisiting even under the new exemption; we'll get to one of them in October").

**Continuity:** Week 25 (spending trap) to Week 26 (estate planning) is a natural philosophical progression: first you have to stop bleeding wealth through lifestyle, then you address what happens to the wealth you do accumulate. The transition line in Week 26 should be brief but intentional: "Last week we talked about the gap between what you earn and what you keep. This week — what happens to what you've already built, and whether your estate plan is still optimized for the world that exists, not the one the exemption sunset was designed for."

**Editorial plan source:** Month 7 (September 2026), Content 2

### Freshness Assessment

**Timing: Ideal. Late August is exactly when Roth conversion modeling should start.**

The editorial plan says the September–December window is "prime Roth conversion territory." Publishing the modeling piece Aug 24 gives surgeons 5 weeks before the Q3 estimated tax deadline (Sept 15) and 7 weeks before the extended filing deadline (Oct 15) — the two moments when the income picture for the year becomes clearest. The argument in the piece is that August is when you model it, not October — the data is sufficient now, the year is predictable enough to size a conversion, and waiting until Q4 creates execution risk.

**Relevance: Strong. The OBBBA permanence of the 37% rate changes the conventional Roth conversion narrative at surgeon income levels.**

The traditional "convert now before rates rise" argument doesn't apply when the 37% top rate is already permanent. The corrected argument: the relevant variables are (1) whether the surgeon's current marginal rate exceeds their expected retirement rate, (2) Medicare IRMAA surcharges on conversion income, and (3) state tax trajectory. All three require the full-year income picture — which is available by late August — to size correctly.

**Angle refinements:**
- The editorial plan title is "Roth Conversions Before Year-End: The Math That Most Advisors Get Wrong at Your Income Level." This is strong. Keep it.
- New variable to emphasize for 2026: with the July FOMC decision now past, the interest rate environment for 2026 is clearer. If the Fed cut, the relative attractiveness of pre-tax vs. Roth accounts may shift slightly based on expected future investment returns — though this is second-order. Mention briefly if the rate environment is materially different from the editorial plan's baseline (3.50–3.75%).
- Medicare IRMAA surcharge: this is a rarely-discussed but genuinely impactful issue for Roth conversions at surgeon income levels. IRMAA applies to Medicare Part B and D premiums for beneficiaries above income thresholds. The IRMAA lookback is 2 years, so a Roth conversion in 2026 affects 2028 Medicare premiums. For surgeons who expect to be on Medicare by 2028, a large conversion could trigger $5,000–$10,000+ in additional Medicare premiums annually. This is not a reason to avoid conversions — it's a variable that must be modeled.
- The Week 26 bridge: estate planning and Roth conversions interact directly. Roth IRA assets have no RMDs, pass to heirs with a 10-year distribution window, and are not includable in the estate for income tax purposes (though they are for estate tax purposes). If Week 26 raised the estate planning question, Week 27 should briefly connect: "One reason to consider a Roth conversion this year, even at the 37% rate: Roth assets pass differently than traditional IRA assets to your heirs."
- Decision tree structure: the editorial plan specifies a decision tree. Confirm the three branches and their criteria:
  1. Roth conversion makes sense: current rate expected to be lower than future effective rate (unusual at surgeon income unless massive estate draw-down expected in retirement), estate planning priority (heirs at lower rates), living in a high state tax state with possible future relocation to a no-tax state.
  2. Traditional/deferral makes sense: current 37% rate expected to exceed retirement rate (the common case for surgeons who will draw from practice sale proceeds plus investment portfolio at lower brackets).
  3. Partial conversion / depends: the surgeon expects income to vary significantly year-to-year in early retirement, creating brackets they can "fill" with conversions at 24% or 32%.

**Continuity:** Weeks 26–27 form a two-part September planning unit: estate planning (what your wealth does after you) → Roth conversions (how the vehicles holding your wealth are structured going forward). They are complementary, not redundant. A brief callback to Week 26 at the top of Week 27 is appropriate and useful.

### Verify Before Publish
- **2026 IRMAA income thresholds and surcharge amounts:** The editorial plan does not specify these figures. IRMAA surcharges for 2028 Medicare will be based on 2026 income; the 2026 IRMAA thresholds apply to 2024 income for 2026 Medicare. Verify the income thresholds at which IRMAA surcharges kick in and the dollar amounts. Flag for manual verification — these are the kind of figures that shift annually.
- **37% bracket threshold for 2026:** Confirmed in editorial plan at $640,600 (single) / $768,600 (MFJ). Use these when sizing conversion scenarios. Confirm no mid-year IRS adjustment.
- **SECURE 2.0 inherited Roth IRA rules:** Verify the current 10-year distribution requirement for non-spouse inherited Roth IRAs under SECURE 2.0. This is relevant to the estate planning connection in this piece.
