## Content

Most orthopedic surgeons know they should start succession planning earlier. Almost none of them know what "earlier" actually requires.

The assumption is that succession planning is a transaction event — something that kicks off when a buyer appears or when the surgeon decides to sell. The actual protocol runs three years before that conversation begins.

Here is what the work looks like.

Year 3 (starting now, for a 2029 exit): reduce practice surgeon-dependence from the typical 85-95% range to below 70%. Document personal goodwill with a valuation specialist — referral source attribution, patient loyalty analysis, non-compete scope. Evaluate revenue source diversification: ASC equity, consulting relationships, academic affiliations.

Year 2: hire the associate. This takes 12 to 18 months from search to independent revenue generation. Map the referral network formally, by primary care source and geographic catchment area. The documentation from Year 3 and the associate revenue history from Year 2 are what make the due diligence story defensible.

Year 1: engage buyers, structure the deal, execute the installment sale analysis and personal goodwill allocation. These are execution conversations — they go well when the groundwork exists, and badly when it doesn't.

The surgeons who get the highest multiples and the cleanest tax treatment on exit are the ones who started the protocol in Year 3 with no deal on the horizon.

For a surgeon planning to exit in 2029, Year 3 is August 2026.

What's on your Year 3 checklist?
