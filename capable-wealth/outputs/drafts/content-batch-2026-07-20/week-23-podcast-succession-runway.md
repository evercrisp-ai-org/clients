## Script

### COLD OPEN

*[No intro music. Begin speaking immediately.]*

Here is the question I want you to sit with before we get started.

If you had to sell your practice in 18 months — not because you wanted to, but because the decision was made for you — what would your net proceeds be?

Not the gross valuation. Not the multiple. The net number, after tax.

For most orthopedic surgeons between 50 and 57 who haven't started succession planning, the honest answer is: considerably less than it could be. And the gap between what it is and what it could be is almost entirely a function of lead time.

That's what this episode is about. The three-year succession runway, what the work actually looks like in each year, and why August — right now — is the start of that clock.

---

### CONTEXT — What the RVU Data Added This Year

*[Transition from cold open]*

Two weeks ago, I talked about a data shift that matters to every independent orthopedic practice owner.

Hospital and health system-employed orthopedic surgeons now outperform independent practices on median work RVU output. This is a recent reversal. For a long time, independent practice owned-surgeons generated more RVUs because they had full control over their scheduling, their OR access, and their patient flow. That pattern has shifted.

Why does that matter for practice valuation?

When a buyer — a private equity firm, a hospital system, another practice — evaluates your practice, they normalize your EBITDA against a replacement surgeon scenario. In plain terms: if you retire tomorrow and they have to replace you, what does the practice generate?

That replacement model now uses the hospital productivity benchmark as a reference. If a buyer's analyst models that a replacement surgeon will generate 10 to 15% fewer RVUs than the hospital-employed peer benchmark — because the replacement surgeon doesn't have your specific referral relationships, your clinical reputation, your personal relationships with the OR scheduling team — your forward revenue projection takes a haircut before a single negotiating term is discussed.

The practical implication is straightforward. A practice that depends heavily on one surgeon's personal productivity and relationships is more exposed to this valuation discount than a practice that has been genuinely systematized. The three-year runway is about building that systematization.

### MAIN SEGMENT 1 — Year 3: The Systemization Work

*[7:00 mark approximately — CLIP-CANDIDATE begins]*

The most important year in a three-year runway is Year 3. Which is right now, if you are planning to exit in 2029.

**[CLIP-CANDIDATE: "The Valuation Discount You Don't Know You're Taking"]**

Here is the clip-ready version of that last point.

Hospital surgeons now outperform independent practices on median work RVU output. Buyers know this. And that gap becomes a discount on your forward revenue model if your practice is still primarily running on one surgeon's personal productivity. The three-year runway is how you close that discount before it shows up in a term sheet.

*[End clip-candidate, continue segment]*

The Year 3 work has three components.

The first: **practice systemization**. The goal is reducing the percentage of the practice's EBITDA that is directly attributable to the selling surgeon's personal productivity, relationships, and clinical reputation. This isn't about making yourself redundant — it's about making the practice's revenue story credible without you in the room when a buyer is doing diligence.

This means formalizing scheduling systems. It means building case coordination capacity that doesn't require your direct oversight. It means documenting clinical protocols in a way that a buyer's consultant can review and conclude: this practice has institutional infrastructure, not just a talented surgeon.

A practice that goes from 95% surgeon-dependent to 70% surgeon-dependent over three years is a fundamentally more valuable asset at exit. Buyers pay for predictable post-close cash flow. Reduce the uncertainty, and you raise the multiple.

The second component: **personal goodwill documentation**.

This is the one that most surgeons skip — and it's the most expensive skip in the succession planning process.

Personal goodwill is the value attributable to your individual reputation, your skills, your specific referral relationships. When properly documented before a sale, it's taxed as long-term capital gains — 20% plus 3.8% NIIT. Enterprise goodwill, the portion that transfers with the practice entity, is typically taxed at higher blended rates.

The documentation process involves a valuation professional, not just your attorney. You're separating and quantifying the personal component: non-compete analysis, referral source attribution, patient loyalty metrics, and your own assessment of what leaves the practice when you do.

The important constraint is timing. The documentation has to be established before negotiations begin. Once you're in a deal process and a buyer is at the table, the personal goodwill allocation argument is very difficult to win because the buyer has every incentive to push back on it. The surgeon who documented three years earlier has a paper trail. The surgeon who tries to establish it in the deal room is negotiating against herself.

The third component: **revenue source diversification**. A practice with an ASC ownership stake, a consulting relationship, or an established academic affiliation has revenue streams that continue regardless of surgical volume post-close. Those streams add to enterprise value and make the post-close revenue narrative more defensible. Neither an ASC stake nor an academic relationship is difficult to establish in three years. Both require starting now.

### MAIN SEGMENT 2 — Year 2: Building the Transferable Infrastructure

The Year 2 work is about building what makes the practice transferable on paper, not just in principle.

This is typically when the associate hire happens.

Finding, recruiting, and integrating an orthopedic associate takes 12 to 18 months from initial search to the point where the associate is generating meaningful independent revenue. If you start the search in Year 2, you have 12 months of associate-generated revenue history by the time buyers are doing due diligence in Year 1.

That revenue history matters. A buyer modeling post-close cash flow wants to see that the practice can generate revenue without the selling surgeon performing every case. An associate track record is that proof.

This is also when referral network documentation becomes important. Where does your volume come from? By primary care referrer, by geographic catchment area, by subspecialty relationship? Referral source attribution makes the revenue story defensible in due diligence. It also helps identify the referral relationships that are genuinely personal — and therefore, personal goodwill — versus the institutional referral patterns that will continue regardless of who's operating.

---

### MAIN SEGMENT 3 — Year 1: Execution

Year 1 is execution.

The practice is systematized. Personal goodwill is documented. An associate is generating independent revenue. The referral network is mapped.

The Year 1 work shifts to buyer engagement, deal structure, and transaction tax planning.

For a surgeon planning a private equity deal, the rollover equity negotiation happens here. Rollover equity — retaining a stake in the PE-backed entity at close — is one of the most surgeon-specific tax planning opportunities in a succession transaction. If the structure is right, you're deferring capital gains on the rollover portion and participating in the upside of the platform's second-bite exit.

The earnout negotiation happens in Year 1 as well. The earnout structure, compensation arrangement, and the non-compete scope are all Year 1 conversations — but they're much more favorable conversations to have from a position of strength, where the practice is systematized and the personal goodwill allocation is already documented.

Installment sale analysis is another Year 1 tool. If the buyer isn't a PE firm — if you're selling to a hospital or a larger practice — spreading the gain across multiple tax years via an installment note can reduce the effective tax rate materially. The analysis requires lead time and the right deal structure; it doesn't happen without planning.

### MAIN SEGMENT 4 — The Math

**[CLIP-CANDIDATE: "$1.5 Million Gap: The 3-Year vs. 18-Month Exit Math"]**

Let me make this concrete.

Consider a surgeon at 55, planning to exit at 58. Current practice EBITDA: $900,000. Current valuation: approximately $2.7 million at a 3x multiple, before personal goodwill allocation.

*[Visual cue: if viewing on video, the timeline graphic appears here. Audio listeners — I'll walk you through the numbers.]*

After three years of runway work: the practice is 65% transferable rather than 95% surgeon-dependent. An associate hired in Year 2 has added $300,000 in annual EBITDA from non-surgeon-dependent revenue. Normalized EBITDA reaches $1.1 million. Personal goodwill is documented at 45% of the total transaction.

Exit outcome at Year 3:

$1.1 million EBITDA at a 4x multiple — supported by systematized infrastructure and documented personal goodwill — equals $4.4 million in enterprise value.

Personal goodwill allocation: 45% of $4.4 million is $1.98 million, taxed at 23.8%. That's $471,000 in tax on the personal goodwill portion.

Enterprise goodwill: $2.42 million, taxed at a blended rate of approximately 30%. That's $726,000.

Total tax: approximately $1.197 million. Net proceeds: $3.2 million.

Now the 18-month version. Same practice, same EBITDA, but no runway: no systematization, no associate track record, no personal goodwill documentation. $900,000 EBITDA at a 3x multiple because the practice is still highly surgeon-dependent — that's $2.7 million. Taxed almost entirely as ordinary income at 37% because the personal goodwill allocation wasn't established: approximately $999,000 in tax.

Net proceeds: $1.7 million.

The gap: $1.5 million.

$3.2 million versus $1.7 million. Same surgeon, same practice, same income history. The only variable is when the runway started.

*[End clip-candidate]*

### MAIN SEGMENT 5 — The August Diagnostic

So what does starting the runway actually look like in August?

It looks like three conversations.

The first is with your CPA or financial advisor: where are we on practice transferability, and what is the personal goodwill documentation process?

The second is with a practice valuation specialist: what is the practice worth today, and what would it be worth if it were 30% less surgeon-dependent?

The third is the hardest one. It's the honest internal question: are you the practice? In the sense that if you left tomorrow, how much of the practice's value would leave with you?

If the answer is most of it — August is the right month to start changing that answer. Not because there's a deadline in August. Because the three-year runway requires three years, and the runway has to start somewhere.

---

### PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE

The surgeons who exit on their terms share one characteristic. They started thinking about the exit long before it felt urgent.

Not because they were eager to leave. Most of them weren't. But they understood that the practice transition, like any complex procedure, produces better outcomes when the preparation happens before the intervention begins.

The succession runway is a three-year protocol. And the best time to start a three-year protocol is when you still have three years.

If that's you — if you're somewhere between 50 and 57 and the succession conversation hasn't started — August is the season. Not because the window is closing this month. Because the work that protects the most value requires the most lead time. And the surgeons who don't start until it feels urgent usually don't have enough of it.

I'll see you next week.
