## Content

23.8% versus 37%.

That's the difference between the tax rate on personal goodwill — properly documented before a practice sale — and the ordinary income rate a seller faces when the allocation argument wasn't established in advance.

Personal goodwill is the value attributable to the surgeon's individual reputation, clinical relationships, and referral network: the practice value that leaves when the surgeon does. When properly documented and allocated at closing, it qualifies as long-term capital gains.

The documentation process requires a valuation specialist, begins well before any deal is on the horizon, and results in a formal paper trail that establishes the personal versus enterprise split. Once a buyer is at the negotiating table, the allocation argument is very difficult to win — buyers have every incentive to classify as much as possible as enterprise goodwill.

For a surgeon with $2 million in total practice goodwill, a 45% personal allocation at 23.8% versus 37% produces approximately $250,000 in additional net proceeds.

The documentation clock starts three years before the sale.
