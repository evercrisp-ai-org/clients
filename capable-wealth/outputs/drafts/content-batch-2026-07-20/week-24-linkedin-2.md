## Content

If you opened an HSA three years ago and stopped tracking it, there's a meaningful probability you haven't contributed in two of those years without realizing it.

Consider a surgeon at 56 who set up an HDHP and HSA combination at her prior practice. She changed hospital systems. The new system's benefit package defaulted her to a PPO with a $1,200 deductible — below the 2026 HDHP threshold of $1,650 for self-only coverage.

HSA contribution eligibility requires active HDHP enrollment each month of contribution. The month she enrolled in the PPO, she was disqualified. The contributions she made that January were technically excess contributions — subject to income tax and a 6% excise penalty on the excess amount.

Two years of maximum HSA contributions foregone: $17,500 in pre-tax deferrals, $6,475 in federal tax savings, and the compounded growth on those amounts.

Open enrollment in October is the correction window. HDHP election effective January 1 restores eligibility. But the analysis — whether the new plan qualifies, whether the spouse's plan creates a disqualifier, whether a limited-purpose FSA can layer on top — has to happen in August, before the window opens.

The enrollment packet arrives in October. The decision should already be made.

What does your current plan's deductible structure look like?
