Picture this: it is November 15th. A surgeon I know — let's say she is 56, earning $950,000 this year, and she has $200,000 sitting in a single stock position with almost no cost basis.

She calls her advisor and says: "I want to set up that donor-advised fund we talked about. Transfer the stock. Take the deduction this year."

Her advisor calls the DAF sponsor. Sets up the application. Submits the documentation.

Six weeks later, the account is approved. The stock is in. The transfer settles January 3rd.

The deduction moves to 2027.

$121,600 in potential tax savings — gone from 2026. Not because the strategy was wrong. Because the timing was.

That is what we are talking about today.

### MAIN SEGMENT 1: The Charitable Vehicle Setup (3–5 minutes)

**[CLIP-CANDIDATE]**

A donor-advised fund — a DAF — is one of the most tax-efficient charitable vehicles available to high-income professionals. Here is the basic structure: you open an account with a DAF sponsor, transfer assets into it, take an immediate charitable deduction for the full fair market value of what you transferred, and then recommend grants to charities from the account over time.

The critical piece for surgeon practice owners is the asset type. A DAF accepts appreciated securities — stocks, mutual funds, ETFs — and when they transfer, no capital gains tax is triggered. You deduct the full fair market value. The charity eventually gets the full value. The government gets nothing.

Let me run the math on a real-scale scenario.

Consider a surgeon with $200,000 in a single stock position she has held for three years with a cost basis of $30,000. If she sells the stock directly and donates the cash, she pays capital gains tax first: $170,000 gain at 23.8% (long-term rate plus NIIT) equals $40,460 in tax. She donates $159,540. She deducts $159,540 at her 37% marginal rate, saving $59,030. Net tax benefit: $59,030 minus $40,460 equals $18,570.

If she transfers the stock directly to the DAF instead: no capital gains tax. Full $200,000 deduction. At 37%, she saves $74,000. Plus she avoided $40,460 in capital gains. Total tax savings: $114,460 versus $18,570.

The difference between these two approaches is $95,890 in a single transaction.

Now here is why timing matters.

Major DAF sponsors — Schwab Charitable, Fidelity Charitable, Vanguard Charitable — take four to eight weeks to process new accounts. The brokerage transfer itself requires additional time and coordination. To ensure the stock is in the DAF account, the gain is recognized by the DAF (not by the surgeon), and the deduction is eligible for 2026, the account should be established and funded by October 31 at the absolute latest.

That means paperwork starts in September. And choosing the right DAF sponsor, identifying the specific position to transfer, and coordinating with the brokerage takes the four to six weeks before that.

Start in July. Finish by December. Everything else is optional.

### MAIN SEGMENT 2: Equipment and Section 179 (3–5 minutes)

Section 179 allows you to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying business property in the year it is placed in service — rather than depreciating it over five to seven years. For an orthopedic surgeon practice, this includes surgical equipment, imaging tools, office technology, practice management software, and certain vehicles used for business purposes.

The planning question is not whether you qualify for Section 179. You almost certainly do. The planning question is which purchases your practice needs anyway that can be timed for 2026 to maximize the deduction.

Here is where July matters: the decision requires knowing your approximate current-year income. You need to know whether the practice will generate enough revenue to absorb a large deduction efficiently. At mid-year, after running the halftime assessment, you have that picture. In November, you are rushing.

I want to be careful here: the specific 2026 Section 179 deduction limit needs to be confirmed with your tax advisor before relying on a specific number. The 2025 limit was $1.16 million. The point is not the ceiling — most surgeon practices will not approach it. The point is the timing of the decision.

If your practice needs new equipment, new technology, or an infrastructure upgrade, the time to order it and arrange financing is September and October — not December. Equipment ordered in December may not be placed in service before December 31, and in-service status is the legal requirement for the current-year deduction.

Identify the purchases now. Get quotes in August. Pull the trigger in September or October.

### MAIN SEGMENT 3: Retirement Plan Amendments (3–5 minutes)

**[CLIP-CANDIDATE]**

This is the one that surprises most people, because it sounds administrative. It is actually significant.

Cash balance plans, 401(k) plans, and profit-sharing plans can be amended during the year — their contribution formulas changed, new features added, or the plan design restructured — but these amendments have deadlines tied to the plan year.

For most calendar-year plans, significant structural amendments need to be adopted before October 1 to apply to the current plan year. That is a hard deadline, not a soft guideline.

Here is the scenario where this matters. Consider a surgeon who established a cash balance plan three years ago. At the time, the plan was designed with a conservative annual contribution target — say, $100,000 per year — based on her income and risk tolerance at the time. Her income has since grown substantially. Her cash balance plan has room to expand — potentially to $150,000 or $175,000 per year in tax-deferred contributions based on her current age and compensation.

But the expansion requires a plan amendment. The amendment requires working with the plan's actuary and third-party administrator to revise the benefit formula. That process typically takes six to eight weeks once initiated.

If the surgeon starts the conversation in July, the amendment can be adopted before October 1 and applies to the full 2026 plan year. If she starts in October, the deadline has passed.

The additional contribution capacity she unlocks — let us say $60,000 more per year — represents $22,200 in immediate federal tax savings at 37%. Over ten years of compounding at 6%, that $60,000 grows to $107,000 in the plan. The tax savings on the compounding phase are deferred until distribution. That is a material outcome from a conversation that needs to start now.

If you have an existing cash balance plan and your income has grown significantly since it was established, ask your actuary this question before September 1: is the plan's current benefit formula capturing the full allowable contribution for my age and compensation? If the answer is no, you have a few weeks left to fix it for 2026.

### MAIN SEGMENT 4: Roth Conversion Analysis (3–5 minutes)

**[CLIP-CANDIDATE]**

The September and October window is considered peak Roth conversion territory. July is when the analysis should happen, so the decision is informed rather than rushed.

With the One Big Beautiful Bill Act making the 37% top federal rate permanent, the traditional "convert now while rates are low" argument needs reexamination. The argument was always: pay taxes today at a lower rate to avoid taxes in retirement at a higher rate. If today's rate is already 37% and permanent, the rate arithmetic has changed.

The Roth conversion question for a surgeon is no longer about the federal rate in isolation. It is about five variables in combination:

First, what is the surgeon's current combined marginal rate — federal plus state? A surgeon in California is paying 37% federal plus 13.3% state equals 50.3% on the conversion amount. Paying 50% today to potentially avoid 25% in retirement is clearly wrong.

Second, what is the expected retirement state tax situation? A surgeon converting in California who plans to retire in Texas avoids 13.3% in state taxes on every converted dollar in retirement. That changes the math substantially.

Third, what is the interaction with Medicare IRMAA? Roth conversion income is included in MAGI, which determines Medicare Part B and D premium surcharges. A $100,000 Roth conversion that pushes MAGI above a threshold could add $2,000 to $5,000 in annual Medicare surcharges for two years. That cost needs to be modeled.

Fourth, what is the interaction with other year-end moves? A $100,000 charitable deduction from the DAF established in move one can offset $100,000 in Roth conversion income. These moves coordinate.

Fifth, is the surgeon likely to have significant pre-tax assets at retirement? If the surgeon has a $3 million cash balance plan balance and no Roth assets, required minimum distributions from that balance starting at age 73 could push taxable income well above current levels. A Roth conversion now — at a known rate — may prevent a higher effective rate at RMD time.

The analysis is not "should I do the backdoor Roth" — at this income level, that is almost always a small, secondary decision. The analysis is whether a strategic conversion of $50,000 to $200,000 makes sense this specific year, given the specific combination of state tax trajectory, IRMAA thresholds, charitable offset, and RMD projection.

That analysis takes time and requires a full-year income projection. Start it in July, implement the result in October, and capture or decline the opportunity with clear reasoning rather than a December guess.

### MAIN SEGMENT 5: Installment Sale Structuring (2–3 minutes)

The fifth move is the narrowest in applicability but the highest in dollar stakes for the surgeons it affects.

If you are planning to sell a practice, a real estate property, or a significant business interest before December 31, the installment sale election must be made before or at the time the transaction closes. Once you accept full payment at closing, the capital gains are fully recognized in the year of sale.

An installment sale allows you to receive the proceeds over two or more years, spreading the capital gain across tax years. The practical benefit: a $2 million gain recognized entirely in 2026 pushes your income to approximately $3 million for the year, with the top $2.4 million taxed at 37% federal. The same gain spread over three years keeps each year below the top bracket threshold for a portion of the income — potentially saving $80,000 to $150,000 in total federal tax.

The catch: the installment structure must be negotiated with the buyer before closing, and it requires the buyer's agreement. Buyers sometimes accept installment terms; sometimes they do not. The conversation takes time, and the structuring requires tax counsel and deal counsel to coordinate.

If a transaction is on your horizon in the second half of 2026, this conversation needs to happen now. The mechanism is legally straightforward; the negotiation is the variable. Give yourself time.

### LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1–2 minutes)

Five moves. Five clocks running simultaneously.

The DAF: start the account in August, fund it by October.
Section 179: identify the purchases now, order by September, in service by December 31.
Retirement plan amendment: conversation with your actuary before September 1, adoption before October 1.
Roth conversion: model the analysis in July and August, implement in October.
Installment sale: before the transaction closes, period.

The single action for this week: pull up your Q1 and Q2 tax filings or estimates and identify which of these five moves you have not yet initiated. For each one that applies to your situation, write down the specific deadline and the first step to start. That list is what you bring to your next advisor conversation.

You do not need to execute all five. You need to know which ones apply to you, in time to do something about it.

### PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1–2 minutes)

There is a version of year-end planning that most surgeons experience: the December scramble. The CPA calls, asks what you want to do, and you make decisions under pressure with incomplete information in the last two weeks of a year that is already written.

And there is another version — the one where the decisions were made methodically, staged across the year, with enough lead time to actually capture the strategies that require lead time.

The interesting thing is that the second version does not require more expertise or more sophistication. It requires an earlier start. That is it.

You spend twelve-hour days making precision decisions under pressure in the OR. Your financial structure deserves the same intentionality — but with the advantage that in financial planning, you actually get to see the deadline coming months in advance.

The window is open. July is the time to walk through it.
