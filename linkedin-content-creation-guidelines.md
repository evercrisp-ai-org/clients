# LinkedIn Content Creation Guidelines — Jared Paul, CFP® CIMA® CEPA®

**Audience:** Orthopedic surgeons who own practices, earning $500K–$1M+
**Followers:** ~1,400 | **Analysis period:** Jan 10 – Apr 9, 2026 | **Posts analyzed:** 50

---

## What this document is

This is a performance-tested rulebook for creating LinkedIn content. Every rule below comes from analyzing 50 published posts, 26 content scripts, and three months of engagement data. It replaces guesswork with patterns that actually drove reach and engagement.

Use this document at the content creation stage — before writing begins — to filter out formats, hooks, and structures that underperformed, and to enforce the patterns that broke through.

---

## The core insight

**83% of total impressions came from 5 posts.** 64% of posts got zero engagements. The difference between a breakout post and a dead one is not topic quality — it's structural. The same theme (tax optimization, reimbursement shifts) produced both 7,274-impression posts and 56-impression posts in the same week.

What separated the winners: the first sentence, the emotional frame, and the posting cadence. Everything below addresses those three levers.

## Section 1: Hook rules

The opening line is the single highest-leverage element. Every A-tier hook in the dataset shared specific structural traits. Every underperformer violated them.

### Rule 1 — Lead with a dollar figure or percentage in the first 8 words

This is non-negotiable. Every breakout post in the dataset opened with a concrete number tied to a surgeon's personal financial outcome.

**Posts that broke through:**

- "One surgeon I spoke with was leaving **$130K** on the table. Every year. For six years."
- "Your practice collected **$2.4M** last year. You kept **$480K**. Your effective hourly rate? $185."
- "CMS cut your reimbursement by **2.5%**. Your practice manager told you. Your financial advisor probably didn't."
- "**$185** per hour to **$215** per hour. Same collections. Same hours. Same number of cases."

**Posts that got buried:**

- "There is a question worth asking every surgeon who owns a practice." (105 imp)
- "Your effective hourly rate has five components. You can influence four of them." (116 imp)
- "Most orthopedic practices track revenue closely." (123 imp)
- "OBBBA tied Medicare physician payments to 75% of the Medicare Economic Index." (112 imp)

**Enforcement:** If a draft's first sentence does not contain a dollar amount, a percentage, or a specific number tied to surgeon income/loss, rewrite it before anything else.

### Rule 2 — Create a math problem the reader solves in their head

The best hooks force mental arithmetic. "$2.4M collected, $480K kept" — the reader calculates the gap. "$130K on the table for six years" — the reader multiplies to $780K. This creates dwell time (LinkedIn's core distribution signal) without requiring the reader to click anything.

**Template:** [Big number in] → [Smaller number out] → [Implied gap the reader calculates]

### Rule 3 — Challenge a trusted advisor in the first two sentences

Three of the five breakout posts explicitly named a person the surgeon trusts (CPA, financial advisor, practice manager) and implied that person missed something. This works because it creates an immediate trust gap — the reader wonders what *their* advisor missed.

**Examples that worked:**

- "Your CPA told you to keep your S-Corp salary low. That advice might be costing you $25K a year."
- "CMS cut your reimbursement by 2.5%. Your practice manager told you. Your financial advisor probably didn't."
- "Your tax bill isn't the problem. Your structure is." (Implies the CPA focused on the wrong thing.)

**Examples that didn't challenge anyone:**

- "A cash balance plan can shelter $150K–$350K+ per year in tax-deferred contributions." (Brochure tone. No tension.)
- "Estimated taxes aren't a quarterly bill. They're a quarterly strategy session." (Reframe without antagonist.)

### Rule 4 — No throat-clearing

"There is a question worth asking" — that's one sentence of delay before the actual content begins. "Let me put that in perspective" — that's narrating the act of explaining instead of explaining. Every word before the point costs readers.

**Kill phrases (never use in opening lines):**

- "There is a question worth asking..."
- "Let me put that in perspective..."
- "Here's why that matters..."
- "Most people don't realize..."
- "It tends to land quietly..."
- "I keep coming back to..."
- "Not because X. Because Y." / "Not X, but Y." (formulaic AI pivot — state the actual cause directly instead of staging an artificial negative-then-positive contrast)

**Fix:** Delete the throat-clearing sentence and start with whatever came after it. For "not X, but Y" pivots, drop the negation and write the positive claim as a declarative sentence.

### Rule 5 — No acronym opens

"OBBBA tied Medicare physician payments to 75% of the MEI in 2026" was the worst-performing hook in the dataset relative to its theme. Policy acronyms exclude anyone who doesn't already follow CMS updates closely. If the acronym is necessary, introduce the impact first, then name the policy.

**Bad:** "OBBBA tied Medicare physician payments to 75% of the MEI."
**Better:** "Your Medicare reimbursement now covers 75% of what it actually costs to run your practice. The OBBBA locked that in for 2026."

## Section 2: Content structure rules

### Rule 6 — Target 180–230 words per LinkedIn post

The best-performing posts in the dataset were between 180 and 230 words. Posts in the 250–295 word range consistently underperformed. LinkedIn's mobile display truncates around 210 characters (~3 lines) before the "see more" fold. Everything above 230 words adds length without adding reach.

| Word count range | Avg performance in dataset |
|---|---|
| 168–205 words | Contained all breakout posts |
| 230–266 words | Mid-tier, no breakouts |
| 266–295 words | Lowest engagement rates |

**Enforcement:** If a draft exceeds 230 words, cut it. Do not add a second example, a third bullet, or a closing paragraph to fill space. Shorter is better for this audience.

### Rule 7 — One idea per post, no exceptions

Every post that tried to cover multiple concepts underperformed. The "Five Components of Your Effective Hourly Rate" post (5 sub-topics in one post) got 116 impressions. The post that isolated a single $185-to-$215 contrast from the same theme got 435.

**Rule:** Each post gets one number, one story, and one takeaway. If you have five components, that's five posts — not one listicle.

### Rule 8 — Use the loss frame, not the gain frame

The dataset is unambiguous: loss framing outperforms gain framing for this audience.

**Loss frame (worked):** "leaving $130K on the table" / "costing you $25K a year" / "cut your reimbursement by 2.5%"
**Gain frame (didn't work):** "can shelter $150K–$350K+ per year" / "allows a 20% deduction on qualified business income"

Surgeons are high earners who feel overtaxed and under-advised. They respond to the fear of silent losses more than the promise of optimization gains. Lead with what they're losing, not what they could gain.

### Rule 9 — End with a diagnostic question, not a CTA

The strongest closers in the dataset posed a question the reader could only answer by auditing their own finances. Avoid explicit CTAs ("book a call," "DM me," "link in comments"). Those reduce organic distribution and signal sales content to the algorithm.

**Strong closers:**

- "When was the last time someone looked at your return for strategy, not just compliance?"
- "Do you know your effective hourly rate? Not your collections — what each hour of your time actually produces?"

**Weak closers:**

- Generic "if this resonated, share it" engagement bait
- "DM me for a review" (algorithm suppresses)
- Summary recaps of what the post already said

## Section 3: Posting cadence and format

### Rule 10 — Three posts per week maximum: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday

The data is clear:

| Day | Avg impressions | Breakout posts |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | 108 | 0 |
| Tuesday | 771 | 1 (7,274 imp) |
| Wednesday | 805 | 1 (7,721 imp) |
| Thursday | 1,238 | 3 (3,751 / 2,335 / 3,387 imp) |
| Friday | 100 | 0 |

Monday and Friday posts averaged ~100 impressions with zero breakouts. Every dollar of reach came from Tue–Thu. Posting 5x/week trains the algorithm to expect low-engagement content from your account. Three high-quality posts on peak days will outperform five spread across the week.

**Weekly structure:**

- **Tuesday:** Hook post — the strongest opening, the most provocative claim, the biggest number. This is your scroll-stopper.
- **Wednesday:** Story post — a specific surgeon scenario that illustrates the Tuesday claim. First-person, conversational, narrative.
- **Thursday:** Mechanism post — the tactical "here's how it works" detail for readers who engaged earlier in the week.

### Rule 11 — Kill the weekly recap format

Posts titled "Four weeks. Four topics. One message." and "Four weeks of structural diagnosis. One document that confirms everything." received 75 and 54 impressions respectively. These are meta-content — they narrate your content strategy instead of delivering value. Your audience is not following a weekly series. Every post must stand alone.

**Enforcement:** Never produce a "summary of what we covered" post. If a theme needs a capstone, write a new standalone post with its own hook and its own number.

### Rule 12 — Repurpose video and audio into LinkedIn-native formats

The content batches already produce YouTube scripts, podcast segments, and short-form clips. None of that multimedia appeared on LinkedIn during the analysis period. All 50 posts were text-only.

**Action:** For each weekly content batch, extract one 60-second native video clip for LinkedIn from the podcast or YouTube content. Native video has a separate distribution lane on LinkedIn and does not compete with your text posts. This effectively gives you a fourth weekly touchpoint without diluting text post performance.

Also test: carousel (PDF) format for the mechanism/tactical posts. The "Five Components of Your Effective Hourly Rate" content would work as a 5-slide carousel where text-only failed.

## Section 4: Theme selection rules

### Rule 13 — Every theme must pass the "silent loss" test

Before approving a weekly theme, ask: **Can this be framed as money the surgeon is currently losing without knowing it?**

| Theme | Silent loss frame? | Performance |
|---|---|---|
| Tax Structure Optimization | Yes — "$130K on the table" | 7,274 imp breakout |
| Reimbursement Shift | Yes — "$32K/year revenue loss" | 2,335 imp breakout |
| Tax Return as Diagnostic | Yes — "$172K gap in retirement capacity" | 3,387 imp breakout |
| S-Corp Salary Optimization | Partially — "$25K cost" but technical | 214 max, no breakout |
| Effective Hourly Rate | Weak — comparison, not loss | 435 max, no breakout |
| April 15 Trifecta | Weak — deadline-driven, not loss-driven | 393 max, no breakout |

Themes that pass the silent loss test get 5–70x more distribution than themes that don't.

### Rule 14 — Limit posts per theme to 3

The content batches produced 5 posts per theme per week. The data shows that only 1 (sometimes 2) posts per theme ever get meaningful traction. The other 3–4 posts get <150 impressions and generate zero engagement, training the algorithm to suppress subsequent posts.

**New standard:** 3 LinkedIn posts per weekly theme. No more.

### Rule 15 — Retire the "money-wealth-orthopedics" tag set

Posts using the `#money #wealth #orthopedics` hashtag combination averaged 79 impressions across 22 posts. Not a single one broke 139 impressions. Posts using the `#orthopedicsurgeon #orthopedicsurgery #physicianfinance` tags averaged 985 impressions with 5 breakout posts.

The first tag set is generic. The second is identity-specific. Use the identity-specific tags on every post going forward.

## Section 5: Voice and positioning rules

### Rule 16 — Position as diagnostician, not educator

Posts that teach ("here's how QBI works," "five components of your hourly rate") underperform posts that diagnose ("you're losing $130K and here's the structural gap"). Your audience doesn't come to LinkedIn to learn tax code. They come to find out if they're making a mistake.

**Educator voice (underperformed):** "A cash balance plan can shelter $150K–$350K+ per year in tax-deferred contributions. Most surgeons over 50 don't have one. Let me put that in perspective."

**Diagnostician voice (breakout):** "One surgeon I spoke with was leaving $130K on the table. Every year. For six years. He wasn't doing anything wrong. His CPA was competent. His investments were fine. But three structural gaps had been silently draining his wealth."

The diagnostician voice creates the response: *"Wait — am I making that mistake too?"* The educator voice creates: *"Interesting, I'll save this for later."* The first drives engagement. The second drives nothing.

### Rule 17 — Use the surgeon-as-patient metaphor consistently

Your best-performing reframe was treating the tax return as "a 30-page diagnostic report." Your audience thinks in clinical terms. Map financial concepts to clinical ones whenever possible:

- Tax return → diagnostic report
- Financial structure → treatment plan
- Overlooked deductions → missed diagnosis
- Quarterly review → follow-up appointment
- Financial advisor → specialist referral

This metaphor earns trust because it speaks the surgeon's native language. It also differentiates you from every other financial advisor posting generic wealth content on LinkedIn.

### Rule 18 — Specific stories over abstract claims

"A surgeon I spoke with" outperforms "most surgeons" every time. Even anonymized specificity (age, income bracket, state, specialty within orthopedics) creates more trust than generalized statements.

**Weak:** "Most orthopedic practices track revenue closely."
**Strong:** "I sat with a surgeon last month — $800K in practice income, frustrated that he was 'getting killed on taxes.'"

The second version has a person, a number, an emotion, and a direct quote. The first has a statistic about "most" people. Specificity wins.

## Section 6: Content batch production checklist

Use this checklist when generating each weekly batch. Every LinkedIn post must pass all items before approval.

### Pre-write

- [ ] Weekly theme passes the "silent loss" test (Rule 13)
- [ ] Only 3 LinkedIn posts planned for this theme (Rule 14)
- [ ] Posts scheduled for Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday only (Rule 10)
- [ ] At least one native video or carousel is planned alongside text posts (Rule 12)
- [ ] No recap/summary post included in the batch (Rule 11)

### Hook check (every post)

- [ ] First sentence contains a dollar figure, percentage, or specific number (Rule 1)
- [ ] Opening creates a mental math problem for the reader (Rule 2)
- [ ] A trusted advisor is challenged or a trust gap is implied within the first two sentences (Rule 3)
- [ ] No throat-clearing phrases in the first three lines (Rule 4)
- [ ] No "Not X, but Y" / "Not because X, because Y" formulaic pivots anywhere in the post (Rule 4)
- [ ] No acronyms in the opening sentence (Rule 5)

### Structure check (every post)

- [ ] Post is between 180–230 words (Rule 6)
- [ ] Post covers exactly one idea with one number, one story, one takeaway (Rule 7)
- [ ] Primary frame is loss/cost, not gain/benefit (Rule 8)
- [ ] Closing is a diagnostic question, not a CTA or recap (Rule 9)

### Voice check (every post)

- [ ] Tone is diagnostician, not educator (Rule 16)
- [ ] At least one clinical metaphor is used where appropriate (Rule 17)
- [ ] Stories use specific details (income, age, scenario) rather than "most surgeons" generalizations (Rule 18)

### Tag and format check

- [ ] Uses `#orthopedicsurgeon #orthopedicsurgery #physicianfinance` tags (Rule 15)
- [ ] Text posts are text-only (no links in body — LinkedIn suppresses external links)
- [ ] Video clips are uploaded natively (not YouTube links)

## Quick reference: Hook templates that work

Use these as starting structures. Customize with real numbers and scenarios.

1. **The gap:** "[Surgeon] was [doing X]. The available capacity was [Y]. That [Z] gap compounds every year."
2. **The loss:** "One surgeon was leaving $[X] on the table. Every year. For [N] years."
3. **The contrast:** "$[X] per hour to $[Y] per hour. Same collections. Same hours. Same cases."
4. **The advisor miss:** "[Policy/rule change] happened. Your [advisor type] told you. Your [other advisor type] probably didn't."
5. **The challenge:** "Your [advisor] told you to [common advice]. That advice might be costing you $[X] a year."
6. **The reframe:** "Your [document/process] isn't [what they think it is]. It's [what it actually is]."

## Quick reference: Hooks to avoid

- Listicle openers: "Five components of..." / "Three numbers that..."
- Passive framing: "There is a question worth asking..." / "There's a deduction that..."
- Meta/recap: "Four weeks. Four topics." / "Over the past month, we've walked through..."
- Calendar hooks: "Q1 is wrapping up..." / "As tax season approaches..."
- Brochure tone: "A cash balance plan can shelter..." / "The QBI deduction allows..."
- Jargon leads: "OBBBA tied Medicare physician payments to 75% of the MEI..."
- AI pivot: "Not because X. Because Y." / "Not X, but Y." (state the cause directly)
