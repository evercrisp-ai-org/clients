Here's a scenario playing out in orthopedic practices right now. A surgeon reviews his Q1 numbers and notices something his practice manager already flagged: a drop in reimbursements on several of his most-performed procedures.

"It's that CMS efficiency adjustment. About 2.5% across the board on the non-time-based codes."

And the natural next thought?

"I guess I'll just have to add a few more cases to the schedule to make up the difference."

This is the instinctive response when reimbursement drops. Revenue goes down, so volume goes up. It feels logical. And it's one of the most expensive ways to solve the problem.

## The Imaging Result

Let me frame this the way you would in a clinical setting. Think of the CMS reimbursement cut as an imaging result. The image itself (a 2.5% reduction in per-procedure revenue on non-time-based orthopedic codes) is the finding. But the finding isn't the diagnosis.

Most surgeons will look at this result and treat the most obvious symptom: less revenue. The reflexive prescription? More procedures. More volume. More time in the OR.

But any experienced surgeon knows that treating a symptom without understanding the underlying condition leads to poor outcomes. And that's exactly what happens when the only response to a reimbursement cut is "I'll just do more."

## The Math That Makes "More Volume" Expensive

Let's get specific. Say you're performing 400 procedures a year at an average reimbursement of $3,200. The 2.5% CMS efficiency adjustment reduces your per-procedure revenue by $80.

Across 400 procedures, that's $32,000 in annual revenue loss.

The instinct is to add cases. To replace $32,000 in lost revenue, you'd need to add approximately 10 procedures at $3,200 each.

Sounds straightforward. But here's where the math turns.

Those 10 additional procedures aren't free. Each one carries marginal costs: OR time, anesthesia, surgical supplies, implant fees, staff hours, facility charges. At the margin, these costs consume roughly 60% of the additional revenue. That's not a guess. That's the reality of high-overhead surgical procedures where your fixed costs are already absorbed by your existing caseload.

So your 10 additional procedures generate $32,000 in gross revenue, but $19,200 goes right back out the door in marginal costs.

Net recovery: $12,800.

You just worked 30 to 40 additional hours in the OR to recover 40 cents on every dollar you lost.

And there's a cost that doesn't show up in the accounting: the time itself. Those are hours you could have spent with your family, on continuing education, on practice development, or simply recovering from the physical toll of surgery. At surgeon income levels, your time is the most expensive input you have. Spending more of it to chase a diminishing return is the definition of working harder, not smarter.

## The Response Nobody Considers First

Now compare that to a different approach.

The average orthopedic practice runs overhead between 50% and 60% of collections. For a practice collecting $1.6 million annually (our 400-procedure surgeon at $3,200 average), that's $800K to $960K flowing out of the practice in overhead before the surgeon keeps a dollar.

A 2% reduction in overhead, across a $1.6M practice, saves $32,000.

That's the entire reimbursement loss, recovered. No additional procedures. No additional OR time. No additional staff costs. No additional physical wear.

And 2% is realistic. In practice structures like these, there are almost always opportunities in three categories that go unexamined:

**Supply and vendor contracts.** When was the last time your practice benchmarked its supply costs against peer practices? Implant pricing, surgical supplies, and office materials are negotiable, and most practices haven't renegotiated in years. A 5% reduction in supply costs alone can recover $10K to $20K annually for a typical orthopedic practice.

**Staffing efficiency.** And to be clear, I'm not talking about cutting staff. Most practices that have grown over time carry staffing structures built for an earlier era of the practice. Aligning staffing ratios with current patient volume, reviewing scheduling workflows, and clarifying role allocation can reduce staffing overhead by 1% to 3% without reducing quality of care.

**Facility utilization.** If you own your space or have a long-term lease, are you using it efficiently? Unused operatory time, underutilized square footage, and energy costs all add up. For practices that own their building, a cost segregation study (which we discussed in Week 1) can also accelerate depreciation deductions, creating additional tax savings on top of the overhead reduction.

The point is simple. A 2% overhead reduction is the financial equivalent of adding 25 procedures to your caseload, but without the marginal costs that eat into every additional case.

## The Highest-Leverage Move: Income Flow Restructuring

Overhead optimization gets you back to even. But there's a third response that most surgeons never consider, because it doesn't come from their practice manager. It comes from their financial advisor (or should).

When revenue per procedure drops, every dollar your practice earns becomes more valuable. That means the way those dollars flow from the practice entity to your personal wealth matters more than ever.

This is where the structural concepts from the last two weeks come into focus. If you're losing $32,000 in top-line revenue but your S-Corp salary hasn't been recalibrated, your retirement plan is underfunded, or your entity structure isn't optimized for tax efficiency, you're compounding the loss. The reimbursement cut takes $32K off the top. A leaky structure lets another $50K to $100K drain out the bottom.

Conversely, if you use this moment to tighten the structure (recalibrate your salary for maximum retirement plan capacity, establish or increase a cash balance plan, ensure your entity is positioned to keep the most of every remaining dollar), you can more than offset the revenue loss through structural efficiency alone.

The reimbursement cut is real. But how much of it you actually feel depends entirely on how well your income flows from the practice to your personal balance sheet.

## What the Cut Actually Reveals

Here's what I keep coming back to. The 2.5% efficiency adjustment is frustrating. I get it. Nobody wants to earn less per procedure. But the cut itself is small enough that, for most well-structured practices, it's absorbable without any change in surgical volume.

The surgeons who feel it most are the ones whose practices were already running at the margin, with overhead unchecked, structures unoptimized, and every dollar of revenue already spoken for before it reached the surgeon's pocket.

In that sense, the reimbursement cut is a diagnostic tool in its own right. It doesn't change how much you earn as much as it reveals how much of what you earn is being consumed by an inefficient structure.

And that revelation, uncomfortable as it might be, is actually valuable. Because structural inefficiency is fixable. A reimbursement cut from CMS is not.

## The Prescription

You wouldn't treat every fracture with the same approach. Complex pathology calls for a tailored treatment plan. The same applies here.

If the CMS cut showed up in your Q1 numbers and your first thought was "I need to do more cases," I'd encourage you to slow down and consider the three responses in order of efficiency:

1. **Overhead optimization.** Benchmark your practice's overhead against peers. Look at supply contracts, staffing ratios, and facility costs. A 2% reduction recovers the full $32K with zero additional procedures.

2. **Income flow restructuring.** Review how your practice income flows to your personal wealth. Is your S-Corp salary calibrated? Is your retirement plan maximizing its capacity? Is every dollar of reduced revenue being kept as efficiently as possible?

3. **Volume adjustment.** If the first two responses still leave a gap, then (and only then) consider adding cases. But do it with eyes open to the marginal economics: at 60% marginal cost, each additional procedure recovers far less than its face value.

The strongest response to external pressure is internal precision. You already practice that principle in the OR. It applies to your financial structure just as directly.

If nobody has looked at your practice overhead and income flow in coordination (not just one or the other, but both, as a system), this is the quarter to have that conversation. The reimbursement environment isn't getting more generous. But your structure can always get more efficient.
