## HOOK (15–30 seconds)

If you make $800K a year and feel like you should have more to show for it, you're right.

And no, the answer isn't that taxes are too high.

The real answer is that your financial structure has three holes in it that nobody's told you about.

Today I'm going to walk you through each one, show you exactly what they're costing you, and explain what it takes to fix them.

---

## CONTEXT AND STAKES (30–60 seconds)

I work with orthopedic surgeons every day. And here's the pattern I see over and over: a surgeon earning $700K, $800K, even north of a million dollars, and they feel like they should be further along. More saved. More invested. More in control.

They blame the tax rate. They blame their CPA. Sometimes they blame themselves for spending too much.

But when I actually dig into their financials, the culprit is almost never any of those things. It's the structure, the way their practice income flows from the entity to their personal wealth. And there are three specific structural gaps that account for most of the leakage.

Let me walk you through them.

---

## KEY POINT 1: The S-Corp Salary Trap (60–90 seconds)

Most surgeon practice owners run their practice through an S-Corp. And somewhere in that S-Corp is a number, your reasonable compensation, that was probably set years ago by your CPA.

Here's the problem. That number isn't just a payroll figure. It interacts with three things: your self-employment tax, your retirement plan contributions, and your QBI deduction. Get it wrong, and the ripple effects touch everything.

A surgeon earning $800K with an S-Corp salary set at $400K (which is common) might be overpaying self-employment tax by about $25K a year. Or, if the salary is set too low, they might be limiting their retirement plan contributions by even more than that.

The right number isn't the highest or the lowest. It's the one that puts the most money to work across all three variables.

And most surgeons have never seen the math on that trade-off.

---

## KEY POINT 2: The Cash Balance Plan Gap (60–90 seconds)

This is the one that makes me want to stand on a table and wave my arms.

In 2026, the most you can put into a 401(k) with catch-up contributions is $32,500. That's fine if you're making $80K. But at $800K, it's a drop in the bucket.

A cash balance plan lets you contribute an additional $150K to $350K per year, on top of the 401(k), all tax-deferred. It's actuarially designed, it's perfectly legal, and it's the most powerful wealth-building tool for high-income practice owners.

And yet most orthopedic surgeons over 50 don't have one.

Why? Because their CPA handles compliance, not optimization. And their investment advisor may not specialize in practice-level retirement plan design.

At surgeon-level income, the absence of a cash balance plan costs you somewhere between $45K and $80K a year in lost tax-deferred compounding.

Over a decade, that gap becomes a canyon.

---

## KEY POINT 3: The Cost Segregation Blind Spot (60–90 seconds)

If you own your practice building (or any investment real estate) and you haven't done a cost segregation study, you're almost certainly leaving money on the table.

A cost segregation study reclassifies components of your building (things like electrical systems, cabinetry, specialized flooring) into shorter depreciation categories. Instead of depreciating the whole building over 39 years, portions get accelerated to 5, 7, or 15 years.

And now that the One Big Beautiful Bill Act reinstated 100% bonus depreciation permanently, the impact is even larger. We're talking about $15K to $30K a year in additional deductions for a typical surgeon-owned medical office.

That's money you're already entitled to. Nobody's hiding it from you. It's just that nobody ran the study.

---

## KEY POINT 4: The Cumulative Cost (60 seconds)

So let's add it up.

S-Corp salary misalignment: $25K a year.

Missing cash balance plan: $45K to $80K a year.

No cost segregation study: $15K to $30K a year.

Total: $85K to $130K per year. Left on the table. Through structural neglect.

Over ten years (and I've seen this with real clients) that's over a million dollars. Not from bad investments. Not from excessive spending. From a structure that was never built correctly.

---

## KEY POINT 5: Why CPAs Aren't the Villain (30–45 seconds)

And I want to be clear about something. This is not your CPA's fault.

CPAs are trained for compliance: filing accurate returns, meeting deadlines, following the rules. And most of them do that excellently.

But compliance and optimization are two different disciplines. Your CPA's job is to make sure your return is correct. The question of whether your structure is optimal (whether your entity, your retirement plans, and your depreciation are all working together) is a different conversation. And it's one most surgeons have never had.

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (30–60 seconds)

You didn't spend a decade in training to be efficient in the OR and careless with everything that comes after.

The same precision you bring to a procedure (the attention to every detail, the awareness that small decisions compound into big outcomes) is what your financial structure deserves.

If you've never had someone look at all three of these leaks together, your entity structure, your retirement plan design, and your real estate depreciation, in a coordinated way? That's the conversation to have this quarter.

Because at the end of the day, wealth is determined by what you keep, and what you keep is determined by your structure.
