A cash balance plan can shelter $150K–$350K per year in tax-deferred contributions.

Most surgeons over 50 don't have one.

Let me put that in perspective.

In 2026, the 401(k) contribution limit is $32,500 (with catch-up). That's it. For a surgeon earning $800K, that shelters about 4% of your income.

A cash balance plan, stacked on top of the 401(k), can shelter an additional $150K to $350K, depending on your age. That's 20% to 45% of your income, protected from taxation while it compounds.

The math at surgeon-level income is staggering:

An additional $200K per year in tax-deferred contributions, compounding at 6%, becomes $2.6M over ten years.

The tax deferral alone saves $74K to $80K annually at a 37% federal rate.

So why don't more surgeons have one?

Three reasons I hear most often:

→ "My CPA never mentioned it." (CPAs focus on compliance, not optimization.)
→ "I didn't know it existed." (You're not alone.)
→ "It seemed complicated." (The setup takes 60–90 days. The payoff lasts decades.)

If you're an orthopedic practice owner earning $700K+ and you're over 50, this is the single highest-leverage addition to your financial structure.

Not next year. This year. The setup window for meaningful 2026 contributions is open now.
