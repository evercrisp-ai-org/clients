## HOOK (15–30 seconds)

What if I told you that the salary you're paying yourself (the number you've barely thought about since your CPA set it up) could be the single most impactful number in your entire financial plan?

Not your investment returns. Not your tax rate. Your salary.

Today I'm going to break down why, and show you the math that most CPAs never put in front of their surgeon clients.

---

## CONTEXT AND STAKES (30–60 seconds)

If you're an orthopedic surgeon running your practice through an S-Corp (and most of you are) somewhere in your payroll is a number called your "reasonable compensation." It's the W-2 salary you pay yourself.

When it was set up, your CPA probably told you to keep it as low as possible. The logic: lower salary means lower payroll taxes. And that's true, as far as it goes.

But at $745K in practice income, that one piece of advice is interacting with two other financial variables in ways that most surgeons have never seen. And the net effect? It might be costing you $15K to $25K per year in lost wealth-building capacity.

Let me show you what's happening.

---

## KEY POINT 1: Why Most CPAs Default to "Keep It Low" (60–90 seconds)

First, let's give credit where it's due. Your CPA is doing their job. They're focused on compliance: making sure your return is accurate, your filings are on time, and you're not creating IRS problems.

In that world, a lower S-Corp salary makes sense. It reduces your Medicare tax exposure. It's clean. It's defensible. And for most clients in the $100K to $300K range, it works pretty well.

But at surgeon-level income, the compliance-optimal salary and the wealth-optimal salary are often two very different numbers. Your CPA is solving for compliance. The question is: who's solving for wealth?

---

## KEY POINT 2: The Payroll Tax Math, Smaller Than You Think (60–90 seconds)

Let's look at the actual numbers.

The difference in payroll taxes between a $300K salary and a $360K salary is driven by the Medicare tax: 2.9% plus the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above $200K. On the incremental $60K, that's roughly $5,200 per year.

That's real money. But it's the only number most surgeons see. And when it's the only number you see, it looks like the obvious move: keep the salary low and pocket the $5,200.

But here's what's happening on the other side of the ledger.

---

## KEY POINT 3: The Retirement Plan Unlock (60–90 seconds)

Your 401(k) employer contribution, your profit-sharing contribution, and your cash balance plan contribution are all calculated based on your W-2 compensation.

In 2026, the employee 401(k) limit is $24,500 ($32,500 with the catch-up if you're 50 or older). The employer match? That's based on your salary. The cash balance plan? Also based on your salary.

At a $300K salary, your total retirement plan capacity might be around $115K.

At $360K, it could be $150K or more, depending on your age and plan design.

That's a $35K to $40K swing. In tax-deferred contributions. Every year.

You're saving $5,200 in payroll taxes to give up $35K to $40K in retirement contributions.

That is not a good trade.

---

## KEY POINT 4: The QBI Deduction Wrinkle (60–90 seconds)

And there's a third variable that makes this even more nuanced.

The Qualified Business Income deduction (made permanent at 20% by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act) lets you deduct 20% of your pass-through income. But the deduction is limited in part by your W-2 wages.

So if you set your salary too low, you can actually limit your QBI deduction. And if you set it too high, you shrink the pass-through income the deduction applies to.

There's a sweet spot. And finding it requires modeling all three variables (payroll taxes, retirement contributions, and QBI) together.

This is not something you can calculate on the back of a napkin. But it's absolutely something you can ask your advisor to model in a single meeting.

---

## KEY POINT 5: The Annual Review Cadence (30–45 seconds)

One more thing. Your S-Corp salary is not a set-it-and-forget-it number.

Your income changes. Contribution limits change: in 2026, the 401(k) limit went up to $24,500 and the catch-up to $8,000. Tax laws change: OBBBA made the QBI deduction permanent. Your age changes, and that affects cash balance plan capacity.

This number should be reviewed every year, ideally in Q1, when you have your prior-year data and enough runway to adjust payroll for the current year.

If it's been more than two years since someone looked at this, it's almost certainly wrong. Not because anyone made a mistake. Because the inputs changed and the number didn't.

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (30–60 seconds)

Control over your financial life starts with understanding the numbers your own structure is built on.

Most surgeons can describe the biomechanics of every implant they use. They can explain the rationale for every surgical approach they choose. But they can't explain why their salary is set where it is.

That's a gap worth closing.

The right salary isn't the lowest one or the highest one. It's the one that puts the most money to work, across your taxes, your retirement, and your long-term wealth.

And finding it? That's a conversation worth having this quarter.
