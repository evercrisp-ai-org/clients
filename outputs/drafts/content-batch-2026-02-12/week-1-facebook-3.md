Something that still surprises me: most orthopedic surgeons are contributing just $32,500 a year to their retirement plans. That's the 401(k) limit with catch-up.

For a surgeon earning $800K, that shelters about 4% of income.

There's a retirement plan structure (a cash balance plan, stacked on top of the 401(k)) that can shelter $150K to $350K per year, depending on age. That's 20% to 45% of income, compounding tax-deferred.

The difference over a decade, at 6% growth? Roughly $2.6 million.

I'm not saying everyone needs one. But if you're an orthopedic practice owner over 50 earning $700K+ and you've never heard of a cash balance plan, it's worth a conversation with your advisor.

The setup window for meaningful 2026 contributions is open right now.
