If you want a cash balance plan for 2026, the clock starts April 16.

Here's why timing matters. A cash balance plan is a defined benefit retirement plan that allows annual tax-deferred contributions of $150,000 to $350,000, depending on your age, compensation, and plan design. It sits on top of your 401(k), not instead of it. For surgeons over 50, it's the single highest-leverage tax-sheltered savings vehicle available.

But establishing a new plan isn't a phone call. It requires a plan document drafted by an ERISA attorney, actuarial certification, and IRS compliance setup. That process takes 6 to 10 weeks from start to finish.

Start in April or May? You can have a plan in place by July 1, with half-year funding of $75,000 to $150,000.

Start in September? The actuaries are backed up. The attorneys are swamped. And the honest answer becomes: "We can set it up for January. This year's window has closed."

That's $75,000 to $150,000 in tax-deferred savings, gone, because the conversation happened four months too late.

If this is relevant to your situation, the next step is simple: call your financial advisor this month and ask about establishment timelines.
