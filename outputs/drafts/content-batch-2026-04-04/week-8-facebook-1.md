Here's something I keep coming back to.

Surgeons are some of the most disciplined professionals in the world when it comes to seeking peer review and second opinions in clinical practice. Complex case? Get another set of eyes. Ambiguous imaging? Send it to a colleague. High-stakes surgical plan? Pressure-test it with someone whose training gives them a different vantage point.

That instinct is good medicine. It's what separates a competent surgeon from an exceptional one.

But when it comes to finances, most surgeons have a plan that was set up years ago and has never been independently reviewed. One CPA handling the tax return. One advisor managing the portfolio. One estate plan drafted when the kids were little. Each professional doing their job well, but nobody looking at how those pieces fit together.

This isn't a criticism. It's an observation about where the same rigor you bring to the OR could transform your financial life.

If nothing else, asking "has anyone else looked at this?" is free. And at surgeon income levels, the answer to that question tends to be worth a lot more than you'd expect.
