## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

Here's the landscape right now. Private equity consolidation in orthopedics is in its second wave. The first wave (roughly 2018 to 2022) was about platform building: acquiring anchor practices, establishing management services organizations, and proving the model. The second wave is about scale. The platforms that survived are now acquiring feeder practices, building regional density, and preparing for their own exits (selling the platform to a larger PE fund at a higher multiple).

What that means for you, the practicing orthopedic surgeon, is that the volume of inbound interest is higher than it's ever been. You will be approached. If you haven't been already, you will be this conference season.

That's neither good nor bad. It's information. And your job, before you sit down at any dinner, is to have a framework for evaluating what you hear. That's what this episode is about.

We're going to cover five things today. First, what the current PE landscape actually looks like. Second, why the 5-year exit timeline matters even if you're not interested in PE. Third, three questions to ask any acquirer before you engage seriously. Fourth, how to tell the difference between genuine partnership and financial extraction. And fifth, what "options later" actually means, and why starting the planning process gives you leverage, not commitment.

Let's get into it.

## SEGMENT 1: The Current PE Landscape in Orthopedics (3-5 minutes)

Let me set the table with where things stand.

The PE model in orthopedics works like this. A platform acquires a large anchor practice, typically at a 6x to 8x EBITDA multiple. Then it acquires smaller practices at lower multiples (3x to 5x) and integrates them into the platform. The combined entity's EBITDA is higher, and the multiple expansion (from the smaller practices' lower multiples to the platform's higher valuation) creates the arbitrage that drives PE returns.

The surgeon who sells to the platform gets a mix of cash at close and rollover equity. The cash is the immediate liquidity event. The rollover equity (typically 25% to 35% of the deal value) stays invested in the platform, with the promise that when the platform itself is sold (the "second bite of the apple"), the rollover equity will be worth significantly more.

That's the pitch. And in some cases, it works exactly as described.

But here's what the pitch decks tend to minimize. The rollover equity is illiquid. You can't sell it, trade it, or access it until the platform exits. That exit timeline is typically 4 to 7 years. And the return on rollover equity depends entirely on the platform's ability to execute its growth plan, which you have limited ability to influence after the sale.

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

The second element that deserves attention is post-close compensation. In most PE deals, the surgeon's compensation is restructured after closing. Clinical compensation is tied to productivity metrics (wRVUs, collections per provider) with management fees, administrative costs, and platform overhead deducted before the surgeon sees a dollar. The net result is often a 15% to 25% reduction in take-home compensation compared to pre-sale levels.

That's not a secret. But it's rarely the first thing mentioned over dinner.

## SEGMENT 2: The 5-Year Exit Timeline (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Whether or not PE is the right path for you, the 5-year exit timeline is the single most valuable framework for any surgeon thinking about practice transition.

Here's why. The difference between a $4.2 million exit and a $2.8 million exit isn't market timing. It isn't negotiation skill. It's three years of planning that most surgeons never do.

Let me walk through this quickly. Consider a surgeon at 55 with a practice valued at $2.8 million today. With a 5-year timeline, Year 5 is entity restructuring and valuation baseline. Year 4 is personal goodwill documentation and revenue diversification. Year 3 is associate recruitment and systematization. Year 2 is buyer identification and earnout framework development. Year 1 is execution.

That sequence does three things. First, it separates personal goodwill (which qualifies for capital gains treatment at 23.8%) from enterprise goodwill (which may be taxed at ordinary income rates up to 40.8% on certain asset allocations). On a $4 million sale, that distinction alone can save $300,000 to $500,000 in taxes. Second, it increases the EBITDA multiple by proving the practice can operate without the founder. Third, it creates a competitive buyer process that drives the price up rather than accepting the first offer that arrives.

The result, in this illustrative scenario: a $4.2 million exit at 60 versus a $2.8 million distressed-timeline sale. A $1.4 million difference. Same surgeon. Same practice. Same market. The only variable is when the planning started.

## SEGMENT 3: Three Questions for Any Acquirer (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Conference season is here. Before you sit down with any PE firm (or hospital system, or management services organization), there are three questions worth asking. These aren't confrontational. They're diagnostic. And the answers will tell you more about the deal than any pitch deck ever will.

**Question one: What was the timeline from first conversation to close for your most recent acquisition?**

This tells you how the platform operates. If the answer is "we moved quickly, about four months," that's a signal that the platform is optimizing for volume, not for the surgeon's financial outcome. Clean deals, well-structured deals, typically take 9 to 14 months from LOI to close. Anything faster usually means the surgeon didn't have time to optimize their side of the transaction: personal goodwill documentation, entity restructuring, tax planning.

**Question two: How was personal goodwill handled in the transaction?**

If the acquirer looks confused by this question, that tells you something. If they say "our attorneys handle that during due diligence," that tells you something else. In a well-structured deal, personal goodwill allocation is negotiated as a core deal term, not treated as a tax footnote. The surgeon's advisory team should be driving the goodwill allocation, not the buyer's counsel.

**Question three: What does the earnout structure look like three years after close?**

The headline multiple means nothing if 40% of the purchase price is contingent on post-sale performance metrics that the surgeon may not control. Ask for specifics: What are the earnout triggers? What happens if the surgeon reduces clinical hours? What if a key staff member leaves? What if reimbursement rates decline further? The three-year view reveals whether the deal is designed for the surgeon's transition or for the platform's growth.

Those three answers tell you everything. Timeline tells you the pace. Goodwill tells you the sophistication. Earnout tells you the risk.

## SEGMENT 4: Partnership vs. Extraction (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Every PE pitch will use the word "partnership." And in some cases, it's accurate. Genuine partnerships exist in orthopedic PE. But so does financial extraction dressed in partnership language.

Here's how to tell the difference. Four signals.

**Signal one: Compensation trajectory.** In a genuine partnership, the surgeon's total compensation (clinical plus equity appreciation) is projected to increase over the hold period. In an extraction, clinical compensation declines post-close, and the only upside comes from rollover equity, which the surgeon can't access for years. Ask for the projected compensation trajectory, year by year, for five years post-close. If the platform won't model it, that's your answer.

**Signal two: Clinical autonomy.** In a genuine partnership, the surgeon retains meaningful control over clinical decision-making: case selection, surgical approach, implant choice, scheduling. In an extraction, clinical decisions are increasingly influenced by platform economics: preferred vendor contracts, utilization targets, and standardized protocols that prioritize efficiency over clinical judgment. Ask what clinical decisions the platform makes versus what the surgeon decides. Get it in writing.

**Signal three: Governance.** In a genuine partnership, the surgeon has a seat at the table for decisions that affect the practice: hiring, expansion, capital expenditures, strategic direction. In an extraction, governance decisions are made by the platform's management team, and the surgeon is consulted but not empowered. Ask about the governance structure. Specifically, ask what decisions require surgeon approval versus platform approval.

**Signal four: Second-bite economics.** The "second bite of the apple," the return on rollover equity when the platform is sold, is where the biggest upside theoretically lives. In a genuine partnership, the economics of the second bite are transparent: you know the platform's current valuation, the projected exit timeline, the expected multiple, and your pro-rata share. In an extraction, the second-bite projections are vague, heavily caveated, and the platform's management team takes a disproportionate share of the upside through carried interest and management fee structures. Ask for the waterfall. If they won't share it, that's extraction with a handshake.

## SEGMENT 5: Options Later Means Leverage (3-5 minutes)

Here's the part that I want you to carry with you into every conference conversation this season.

Starting the planning process does not mean you're selling. It does not mean you're committed to a PE deal. It does not mean you're leaving medicine.

What it means is that you're building leverage.

A surgeon who has completed a valuation baseline, documented personal goodwill, diversified revenue, and recruited an associate is a surgeon who can evaluate any offer from a position of strength. They can say no. They can walk away. They can wait for a better market. They can sell to a hospital system instead of PE. They can bring in a partner and keep operating. They can retire on their own terms.

A surgeon who hasn't done any of that is a surgeon who takes whatever's available when they're ready to leave. And "whatever's available" is always worth less than what a prepared seller can command.

The 5-year timeline creates options. Options create leverage. And leverage, in any negotiation, is the most valuable thing you can bring to the table.

So when you sit down at a conference dinner this season, and someone slides a pitch deck across the table, the right response isn't yes or no. The right response is: "I'm interested. And I'm building toward a decision on my timeline, not yours."

That's a position of strength. And it starts with the planning you do this year.

## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

Three things to take with you into conference season.

First, the 5-year exit timeline. If you're within five years of a potential transition (and at some point, every surgeon is), start the protocol now. Valuation baseline. Entity restructuring. Personal goodwill documentation. These are Year 5 activities, and they're the ones that create the most value by the time you reach Year 1.

Second, the three diagnostic questions. Timeline, goodwill, earnout. Ask them of any acquirer. The answers will tell you whether the deal is designed for you or for the platform.

Third, the partnership test. Compensation trajectory, clinical autonomy, governance, second-bite economics. Four signals that distinguish real partnership from extraction. You deserve clarity on all four before you engage seriously.

And if you take nothing else from this episode: conference season is a wonderful time to start thinking. Just make sure you're thinking on your timeline, not someone else's.

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

The surgeons who get the best outcomes in practice transitions share one trait. They started planning early enough to have choices.

Not early enough to be anxious about it. Not early enough to signal to the market that they were selling. Just early enough to be ready when the moment came.

That's what the 5-year timeline gives you. Not a commitment to sell. Not a countdown clock. Just the quiet confidence that comes from knowing your options, understanding your numbers, and being able to evaluate any offer through a framework you built yourself.

Conference season is here. Enjoy the conversations. Learn from your colleagues. Listen to the pitches. And when someone buys you dinner and talks about partnership, smile, ask your three questions, and remember: the surgeon who planned ahead always eats better than the surgeon who showed up hungry.
