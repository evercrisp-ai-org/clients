## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

Tax season is wrapping up. April 15 is next week. You're either filing, extending, or putting it out of your mind entirely.

And here's what's going to happen for most surgeons. You'll move on. Back to the OR, back to the schedule, back to the demands of patient care. Your CPA will go quiet for a few months. And the financial chapter of the year will feel, at least psychologically, closed.

Six months later, sometime around November, someone will tap you on the shoulder and say, "We need to figure out your year-end tax strategy." And you'll scramble.

That pattern is expensive. How expensive? Based on what I see in practice structures at surgeon income levels, letting the April-to-October window pass without action costs $35,000 to $60,000 per year in missed optimization. That's not a guess. That's what happens when four specific, time-sensitive planning moves don't get made because nobody put them on the calendar.

Today, I want to give you the calendar.

## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

Here's the reality. The months between April 15 and October 15 are the most valuable planning period of the financial year. The deadlines are behind you. Current-year data is arriving. You have time to implement, and nothing is forcing reactive decisions.

Tax season, by contrast, is reactive. You're looking backward at a year that already happened, trying to minimize a bill that's already been calculated. April through October is proactive. You're looking forward, building the structure that will determine next year's bill before it ever gets calculated.

And yet most surgeons treat this period as dead time. Nothing happens between April 15 and the year-end push. That's the gap we're closing today.

I'm going to walk through four specific financial moves, each with a clear deadline, that are best initiated during this window. These aren't theoretical. They're the same moves that, in practice, separate the surgeons who build wealth efficiently from the ones who feel like they should have more to show for their income. Let's get into it.

---

## SEGMENT 1: Why April Through October Is the Real Planning Season (3-5 minutes)

Let me start with why this window matters so much.

Most people think of tax season as "the" financial planning season. January through April. But at surgeon income levels, tax season is mostly compliance. You're filing a return that reflects decisions made (or not made) in the prior year. By the time your CPA is preparing the return, the opportunities for that tax year are largely gone.

The real planning season starts April 16.

Here's why. After April 15, three things are true simultaneously. First, you have current-year income data starting to come in. Q1 is closed. Collections are tracking. You can see the trajectory. Second, you have time. Seven months until year-end. Enough runway to implement structural changes, establish new plans, and make informed decisions based on real data. Third, you have no deadline pressure. There's no filing date looming. No payment due. Nobody's calling. And paradoxically, that lack of urgency is exactly why most people do nothing.

Think of it this way. In surgery, the best outcomes come from elective procedures with proper pre-operative planning. Not from emergency cases at 2 a.m. The April-to-October window is your elective planning window. Year-end is the emergency room.

---

## SEGMENT 2: Move 1, S-Corp Salary Recalibration, Deadline May 15 (3-5 minutes)

The first move is the simplest, and it unlocks everything that follows.

Your S-Corp reasonable compensation was probably set some time ago. Maybe when you started the practice. Maybe when your CPA set up the initial structure. And unless someone has actively revisited it, it's sitting at whatever number felt reasonable at the time.

The problem is that "reasonable" isn't a fixed number. It changes with your revenue, your role in the practice, your retirement plan structure, and the current regulatory environment. A salary that was optimized three years ago may be leaving money on the table today.

Here's the specific math. Let's say you're currently paying yourself $300,000 through the S-Corp. That's a common number I see in surgeon practice structures. If you recalibrate to $350,000, you pick up roughly $20,000 in additional retirement plan contribution capacity, because your 401(k) employer match and your cash balance plan funding (if you have one) are both calculated against your W-2 compensation.

The cost? About $7,650 in additional payroll taxes (the Medicare portion on the incremental $50,000). The net benefit: $12,000 to $15,000 per year in additional tax-deferred compounding. That's a positive trade-off by any measure.

The deadline is May 15. That gives your payroll provider time to adjust withholding for the remainder of the year, and it gives your retirement plan administrator time to update contribution calculations. If you wait until Q4, the payroll adjustments get compressed, and you may miss contribution windows.

One conversation. One adjustment. May 15.

## SEGMENT 3: Move 2, Cash Balance Plan Establishment, Deadline July 1 (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

If you want a cash balance plan for 2026, the clock starts April 16. Let me explain why.

A cash balance plan is a defined benefit retirement plan that allows annual tax-deferred contributions of $150,000 to $350,000, depending on your age, compensation, and plan design. For surgeons over 50 earning $700K or more, this is the single highest-leverage tax-sheltered savings vehicle available. It sits on top of your 401(k), not instead of it.

But here's the constraint that catches people off guard. Establishing a new cash balance plan requires three things: a plan document drafted by an ERISA attorney, actuarial certification by a credentialed actuary, and IRS compliance setup. That process takes 6 to 10 weeks from engagement to completion.

If you start the process in April or May, you can have a plan in place with a July 1 effective date. That means half-year funding: $75,000 to $150,000 in tax-deferred contributions in the first year alone. Still meaningful. Still worth the effort.

If you start in September? The actuaries are backed up. The attorneys are swamped with year-end work. And by the time the plan document is ready, you may have missed the funding window entirely. I've seen this happen. A surgeon calls in October, ready to establish a cash balance plan, and the honest answer is: "We can set it up for January. For this year, the window has closed."

That's $75,000 to $150,000 in tax-deferred savings that evaporated because the conversation happened four months too late.

July 1 is the practical deadline for meaningful first-year funding. Every month of delay reduces what you can shelter.

---

## SEGMENT 4: Move 3, Entity Restructuring Evaluation, Deadline August 31 (3-5 minutes)

This one takes longer and requires more coordination, which is exactly why it needs to start early.

If your practice structure hasn't been reviewed in three or more years, you may be operating under an entity setup that no longer fits your financial reality. Common triggers for restructuring include adding a partner or associate, meaningful revenue shifts, ASC ownership considerations, or being within five years of a potential practice transition.

Entity restructuring isn't a weekend project. Legal documents need drafting. Tax elections need filing (some with specific IRS deadlines). Operating agreements need updating. Banking relationships may need restructuring. And all of this must be complete before January 1 to be effective for the next tax year.

August 31 is the latest practical start date for a clean implementation by year-end. Start later, and you're either rushing the legal work (which increases errors and costs) or pushing the effective date to the following year (which costs you a full year of optimized structure).

The evaluation itself takes two to four weeks. If the conclusion is "your current structure is fine," you've lost nothing but a few hours. If the conclusion is "restructuring would save you $30,000 to $50,000 per year in taxes and position your practice better for a future transition," you'll be glad you started in the summer instead of the fall.

---

## SEGMENT 5: Move 4, Roth Conversion Analysis, Deadline September 30 (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

The final move is one that's become more nuanced since OBBBA made the 37% top rate permanent.

The traditional advice was simple: if you're in a high bracket now and expect to be in a lower bracket later, do a Roth conversion. Pay the taxes today at a known rate and enjoy tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.

But here's the question that deserves more scrutiny. At 37% federal plus 5% state, you're converting at a 42% combined rate. If you retire in a no-income-tax state and withdraw at an effective federal rate of 22% to 24%, the math on the conversion depends entirely on the growth period and the amount. For small conversions in years where your income dips, the numbers work beautifully. For large conversions at peak earning years, the math may actually favor keeping the money in a traditional IRA and paying lower rates in retirement.

The point isn't that Roth conversions are bad. The point is that they require analysis, not reflex.

And the analysis needs data. Specifically, it needs a clear picture of your current-year income, which you won't have with any precision until Q2 or Q3. Running the conversion analysis in the summer, when you have six months of income data and enough time to execute before December 31, is the optimal timing.

September 30 is the deadline. After that, you're guessing at income numbers rather than modeling them. And a Roth conversion based on a guess can easily push you into a higher bracket or trigger phase-outs you didn't anticipate.

Four moves. Four deadlines. One protocol.

S-Corp salary recalibration by May 15. Cash balance plan establishment by July 1. Entity restructuring evaluation by August 31. Roth conversion analysis by September 30.

If you do nothing else after listening to this episode, put one item on your calendar this week: a 30-minute call with your financial advisor or CPA to set the planning agenda for April through October. That one call, before April 15, is worth more than the last three months of tax preparation. Because it shifts you from reactive to proactive. From scrambling in December to executing a plan you built in the spring.

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

In surgery, the preparation before the procedure determines the outcome more than the procedure itself. You know this intuitively. The imaging review, the patient positioning, the instrument selection, the mental rehearsal. All of that happens before the first incision. And it's what separates a good outcome from a great one.

Your financial life works the same way. The six months of preparation between now and year-end determine whether December is a scramble or a celebration. Whether you're reacting to deadlines or executing a plan you built six months earlier.

The surgeons who build the most wealth per dollar earned aren't the ones with the highest collections. They're the ones who treat the planning window with the same discipline they bring to the OR.

April 15 is next week. The window opens the day after. Walk through it with a protocol.
