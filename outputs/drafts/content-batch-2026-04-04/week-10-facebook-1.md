Conference hallway conversations this week: "So-and-so sold to PE and got a great multiple." What they don't mention is usually the more important part of the story.

The rollover equity they're still holding (30-35% of the sale price, illiquid, in someone else's control for 3-5 more years). The compensation adjustment post-close (surgeon pay often declines 15-25% through productivity thresholds and management fees). And the fact that they're now an employee of the entity that bought their practice, with less autonomy than they had the day before closing.

PE deals can be great. Genuinely. Some surgeons come out with real financial freedom and a practice environment they enjoy. But the headline number ("they got 10x!") never tells the full story. The structure of the deal, the tax treatment, the post-sale compensation, the rollover terms: that's where the real outcome lives.

If you're hearing these conversations at a conference this spring, listen with curiosity. Ask good questions. And before you sit down with anyone who wants to buy your practice, make sure you understand what you'd actually be getting, and what you'd be giving up.

The best practice transitions happen when the surgeon has had time to plan, not just time to react to a compelling pitch over dinner.
