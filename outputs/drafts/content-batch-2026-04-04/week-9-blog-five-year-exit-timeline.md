If a patient came to you with a degenerative condition, you wouldn't wait until they couldn't walk to start treatment. You'd assess the imaging, establish a baseline, and build a multi-year management plan. You'd sequence the interventions. You'd give yourself time.

Your practice exit deserves the same approach.

Most surgeons start thinking about practice transition somewhere around 12 to 18 months before they want to leave. By that point, the timeline is already compressed. There's no room to restructure the entity, document personal goodwill, recruit an associate, or position the practice for competitive bids. The result? A sale that happens on someone else's terms, at a price determined by urgency rather than strategy.

Now that April 15 is behind you and conference season is in full swing, practice transition is likely showing up in every other hallway conversation. PE firms are hosting dinners. Colleagues are comparing deal terms. And the question "have you thought about what's next?" is getting harder to deflect.

Good. Let it land. But before you respond to it, let's build the protocol.

## Why Five Years (and Not Two)

The difference between a 5-year exit timeline and a 2-year timeline comes down to one word: options.

With two years, you take what's available. The entity structure is whatever it is. The valuation reflects the practice as it stands. The buyer pool is whoever's currently interested. And the negotiating leverage tilts toward the buyer, because you've signaled that you're ready to leave.

With five years, you shape the outcome. You restructure the entity while there's no transaction pressure. You document personal goodwill when it can be established credibly (not asserted retroactively during negotiations). You diversify revenue to increase the multiple. You bring in an associate to prove the practice can operate without you. And when buyers come to the table, you have the most powerful thing a seller can have: the ability to walk away.

Let's walk through what each year looks like.

## Year 5: Valuation Baseline and Entity Restructuring

This is the diagnostic year. Before you can build a treatment plan, you need imaging.

Start with a practice valuation. Not the back-of-the-envelope number you carry in your head, but a formal assessment of the practice's enterprise value, your personal goodwill, and the gap between them. This baseline becomes the benchmark against which every future decision is measured.

At the same time, evaluate your entity structure. If you're operating as a single-entity S-Corp and you're five years from a potential transition, this is the moment to explore whether separating personal goodwill into a distinct entity, establishing a management company, or restructuring ownership makes sense. Entity changes take time to season. The IRS looks more favorably on structures that have been in place for years, not months. Starting in Year 5 gives you the runway to make those changes credibly.

Consider a surgeon at age 55, with a practice currently valued at $2.8 million based on existing operations. In Year 5, a formal valuation reveals that the enterprise value is $2.8 million, but $1.1 million of that is attributable to the surgeon's personal goodwill (reputation, referral relationships, patient loyalty). That distinction matters enormously at the point of sale, because personal goodwill qualifies for long-term capital gains treatment. But only if it's documented now, not three months before closing.

## Year 4: Personal Goodwill Documentation and Revenue Diversification

Year 4 is about building value that the practice can carry forward after you leave.

Personal goodwill documentation starts here. This means non-compete agreements, referral source analysis, patient retention data, and evidence that the practice's revenue is tied to your individual reputation and relationships. The goal isn't to inflate value artificially. The goal is to capture, on paper, the value that already exists in your professional identity but would otherwise be invisible in a transaction.

Revenue diversification also begins in Year 4. If 100% of your practice revenue comes from surgical procedures you personally perform, the practice's transferable value is limited. Buyers pay higher multiples for diversified revenue: ancillary services, ASC ownership stakes, non-surgical revenue streams, and referral networks that generate value beyond any single provider.

In our illustrative scenario, the surgeon adds a 20% ownership stake in an ambulatory surgery center and builds a structured referral network with three primary care practices. These additions increase practice enterprise value from $2.8 million to $3.4 million by Year 3, because they represent revenue that doesn't walk out the door when the surgeon does.

## Year 3: Associate Recruitment and Systematization

This is the year that most directly impacts valuation, because it addresses the question every buyer asks: "Can this practice survive without the founder?"

If the answer is no, the multiple drops. If the answer is yes, the multiple rises. The difference in orthopedic practice valuations between surgeon-dependent and systematized practices is significant: a move from 3x EBITDA to 4x EBITDA on a practice earning $700,000 in normalized EBITDA is the difference between a $2.1 million and a $2.8 million valuation on that metric alone.

Recruiting an associate in Year 3 gives you two full years to integrate them, transfer patient relationships, and demonstrate that the practice can produce consistent revenue with a second provider. It also reduces your personal clinical load, which can be reinvested into transition planning, relationship building with potential buyers, or simply into the quality of your remaining working years.

Systematization goes hand in hand with this. Documented workflows, codified protocols, standardized patient communication, structured staff roles. These are the operational elements that make a practice transferable rather than personality-dependent.

## Year 2: Buyer Identification and Earnout Framework

By Year 2, the practice is positioned. The entity is structured. Personal goodwill is documented. Revenue is diversified. An associate is integrated. Now it's time to understand the buyer landscape.

Buyer identification in Year 2 (not Year 1) gives you time to evaluate options without pressure. The landscape includes private equity platforms, hospital systems, individual physician buyers, and management services organizations. Each comes with a different deal structure, a different earnout framework, and a different post-sale reality for the selling surgeon.

The earnout deserves particular attention. In most PE transactions, a portion of the purchase price is contingent on post-sale performance. Understanding the earnout structure (how long, based on what metrics, what happens if targets aren't met) is worth more than understanding the headline multiple. A $4.2 million deal with a 40% earnout tied to three years of revenue targets is a fundamentally different transaction than a $3.8 million deal with 100% cash at close.

Year 2 is when you develop your negotiating framework: what you'll accept, what you won't, and what the walk-away number looks like. Having this framework before any buyer makes an offer is the difference between negotiating from strength and reacting from hope.

## Year 1: Execution, Closing, and Wealth Transition

Year 1 is execution. The treatment plan has been built. The imaging has been reviewed. The positioning is complete. Now you operate.

The closing itself, while significant, is the smallest part of the process in terms of strategic impact. The value was created in Years 5 through 2. Year 1 is about executing cleanly: finalizing deal terms, managing the tax consequences of the sale, and transitioning the proceeds into a wealth structure that serves your next chapter.

Tax planning at the point of sale is critical and should have been in motion since Year 5. The difference between ordinary income treatment and long-term capital gains treatment on a $4.2 million sale can exceed $600,000 in tax liability. Personal goodwill allocation, installment sale structuring, charitable planning through donor-advised funds, and state tax residency decisions all factor in. None of these can be optimized in the final weeks before closing.

## The Math That Makes This Real

Here's how the 5-year approach changes the outcome for our illustrative surgeon.

Starting position at age 55: a practice valued at $2.8 million under current operations.

With a 5-year timeline: Year 5 restructuring separates personal goodwill and adds $200,000 in documented enterprise value. Year 4 revenue diversification (ASC stake, referral network) increases practice value to $3.4 million. Year 3 associate recruitment reduces surgeon-dependence, pushing the EBITDA multiple from 3x to 4x and the practice valuation to $4.2 million. Year 2 buyer identification in a competitive market yields multiple offers. Year 1 execution closes at $4.2 million with optimized tax treatment.

Without the timeline: the same surgeon at 60, ready to leave, sells the practice in its current state for $2.8 million. No personal goodwill documentation. No entity restructuring. No competitive buyer process. The sale happens on a compressed timeline, with limited negotiating leverage and suboptimal tax treatment.

The difference: $1.4 million. Same surgeon. Same practice. Same market. The only variable is when the planning started.

Based on current tax law, the 5-year approach also yields roughly $300,000 to $500,000 in additional tax savings through proper personal goodwill allocation and capital gains treatment. The all-in financial impact of starting five years early instead of two can exceed $1.7 million.

## The Surgeons Who Exit Well

The surgeons who get the best outcomes in practice transitions share something in common. They didn't get lucky with timing or stumble into a generous buyer. They started early enough to have choices.

Conference season is a wonderful time to start thinking about what's next. You'll hear pitches. You'll compare notes with colleagues. You'll sit through presentations about "partnership opportunities" and "liquidity events." All of that is valuable input.

Just make sure you're processing it through your own timeline, not someone else's. A PE firm buying dinner has their timeline. Your colleague who just closed a deal had their timeline. The surgeon sitting across the table at the conference has theirs.

Yours starts with one question: if you want options in five years, what do you need to start doing now?

The answer, as with any complex case, is a protocol. Sequenced. Time-bound. Built on a baseline assessment and refined as new information emerges.

You've been building treatment plans your entire career. This one just happens to be for you.
