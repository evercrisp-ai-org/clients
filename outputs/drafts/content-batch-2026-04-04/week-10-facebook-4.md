Something I wish more surgeons knew earlier: when you eventually sell your practice, there's a massive tax difference between how the IRS treats the value attributed to "you" versus the value attributed to "the business."

The "you" part (your reputation, your referral relationships, your surgical skill) is called personal goodwill. When properly documented, it's taxed at capital gains rates: 23.8%.

The "business" part (equipment, systems, brand, transferable operations) may be taxed at ordinary income rates on certain allocations: up to 40.8%.

On a $4M practice sale, the difference in tax treatment is approximately $678,000. Same deal, same price. The only variable is whether the surgeon documented personal goodwill before the sale.

And here's the part that catches people off guard: this documentation has to happen years before any transaction. Non-compete agreements, referral source analysis, patient relationship evidence. The IRS doesn't accept after-the-fact claims. By the time a PE firm or a buyer calls, you need this work to already be done.

If you're anywhere within five years of a potential practice transition (of any kind), the personal goodwill conversation is one of the most valuable planning conversations you can have. The cost of establishing the documentation is minimal. The cost of not having it is $678,000.
