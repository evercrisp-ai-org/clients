## Content

### Caption Script

Consider a surgeon, mid-career, who has built a practice worth $3 million. They own a home with $500K in equity. They've accumulated a $2 million investment portfolio across retirement and taxable accounts.

Add it up. Practice: $3 million. Home equity: $500K. Portfolio: $2 million. Total net worth: $5.5 million.

The practice represents $3 million out of $5.5 million. That's 55%. Even using more generous calculations, it's 46%.

Now think about that through an investment lens. If your financial advisor put 46% of your portfolio into a single stock that you can't sell for five years, has no liquidity, no hedging options, and whose value depends entirely on one person's continued ability to work, you would fire that advisor. Immediately.

But that's exactly the position most practice-owning surgeons are in. And almost nobody frames it this way.

Your practice is a wonderful asset. But from a portfolio construction standpoint, it represents concentration risk that would be considered reckless in any other asset class.
