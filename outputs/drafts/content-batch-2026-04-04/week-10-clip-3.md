## Content

### Caption Script

The difference between ordinary income and capital gains on a $4M practice sale? $678,000 in taxes. Let me show you the math.

Without personal goodwill documentation, the sale proceeds may be taxed as ordinary income. At 37% federal plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, that's a combined rate of 40.8%. On $4 million, the tax bill comes to approximately $1.63 million.

With personal goodwill properly documented before the sale, the proceeds can be characterized as long-term capital gains. At 20% federal plus 3.8% NIIT, the rate drops to 23.8%. The tax bill: approximately $952,000.

Same deal. Same practice. Same sale price. The only variable: whether the surgeon documented personal goodwill before negotiations began.

And here's the critical part. This documentation (non-compete agreements, referral source analysis, patient loyalty metrics) must be established years before any transaction. The IRS scrutinizes after-the-fact claims. Starting this work after a PE firm calls is already late.

$678,000. That's the cost of not planning ahead.
