If you're curious about what a financial second opinion actually involves, here's the short version.

A good one covers four areas:

1. Entity structure. Is your S-Corp (or LLC, or multi-entity setup) still calibrated for how your practice looks today? Revenue changes, partner additions, and practice transition planning all affect what the optimal structure should be.

2. Retirement plan design. Are you using the full contribution capacity available to you? Most surgeons max out a 401(k) at $31,000 and stop there. With a cash balance plan, the available capacity can be $200,000 or more per year.

3. Tax strategy. Is your effective tax rate where it should be at your income level? Are there coordination opportunities between your tax return and your investment accounts that nobody has connected?

4. Investment allocation. Not stock-picking. Coordination. Are the right assets in the right accounts? Is tax-loss harvesting happening systematically?

The key word in all of this is "coordination." Each area might be handled competently by the professional responsible for it. The second opinion looks at how they work together.

If you have a good CPA, a good advisor, and a good attorney, that's a strong foundation. The question is whether anyone has ever looked at that foundation as a single structure.
