Something I keep thinking about this conference season: how many surgeons approach their practice exit the way they'd never approach a complex surgical case.

If a patient presented with a condition that needed a staged treatment plan, you wouldn't compress everything into a single visit. You'd assess, plan, stage the interventions, and give each phase the time it needs to work.

A practice transition works the same way.

Year 5: the diagnostic. Valuation baseline and entity structure review.
Year 4: the preparation. Personal goodwill documentation and revenue diversification.
Year 3: the setup. Associate recruitment and systematization.
Year 2: the approach. Buyer identification and framework development.
Year 1: the procedure. Execution and closing.

Each phase builds on the one before it. Skip a phase, and the ones that follow are compromised.

The surgeons who feel best about their transitions aren't the ones who got the highest price. They're the ones who had enough time to do it right.

If you're within five years of thinking about what's next (even loosely), the first step is the simplest: a valuation baseline. Everything else builds from there.
