## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

If I told you that 46% of your net worth was concentrated in a single, illiquid asset that you can't sell in pieces, can't hedge, and can't diversify without a major life event, you'd probably call that reckless.

If I told you that same asset was also your primary source of income, your identity, and the thing you've spent the last two decades building, you might start to see the complexity.

That asset is your practice. And for most orthopedic surgeons, it represents the single largest bet in their entire financial life. A bet that most have never analyzed as an investment, never stress-tested against downside scenarios, and never developed a plan to de-risk.

Today, we're going to change that.

---

## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

Conference season is in full swing right now. If you're attending any of the major orthopedic meetings this spring, you've already noticed: practice transition is the conversation. In the hallways. At the dinners. In the exhibit hall. PE firms have booths and business cards and polished presentations. Colleagues are sharing stories about who sold, what the multiple was, and what they're doing next.

All of that is useful information. But it's also incomplete information. Because the PE conversation, as important as it is, is really a subset of a much bigger question that most surgeons haven't asked themselves: What is my practice actually worth to me, not just as a sale price, but as a component of my overall wealth? And is the concentration of my net worth in this single asset a strategic choice, or just something that happened over time?

That's what this episode is about. Not PE specifically (we covered that in depth in this week's blog). This is about the broader concentration risk question and three strategies for addressing it, whether you ever sell to PE or not.

## SEGMENT 1: The Concentration Math (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Let me give you a number that most surgeons have never calculated for themselves.

Consider a surgeon, mid-career, who has built a practice worth $3M based on a conservative valuation. That same surgeon owns a home worth $1.5M (with maybe $500K in equity after the mortgage). And they've accumulated a $2M investment portfolio across retirement accounts and taxable accounts.

Add it up. Practice: $3M. Home equity: $500K. Portfolio: $2M. Total net worth: roughly $5.5M. (And if we use the full home value of $1.5M, the total is $6.5M, but let's use the equity figure since that's what's actually accessible.)

The practice represents $3M out of $5.5M. That's 55% of net worth in a single asset. Even using the more generous total, it's 46%.

Now, pause and think about that through an investment lens. If your financial advisor called you and said, "I've put 46% of your portfolio into a single stock that you can't sell for at least five years, that has no liquidity, no hedging options, and whose value depends entirely on one person's continued ability to work," you would fire that advisor. Immediately.

But that's exactly the position most practice-owning surgeons are in. And almost nobody frames it this way.

The practice is a wonderful asset. It generates income. It builds equity. It creates professional fulfillment. But from a pure portfolio construction standpoint, it represents a level of concentration that would be considered reckless in any other asset class.

Understanding this isn't meant to create anxiety. It's meant to create clarity. Because once you see your practice as what it is (the dominant position in your personal balance sheet), the strategic questions that follow become much more obvious.

---

## SEGMENT 2: The Most Dangerous Sentence in Surgeon Finance (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

There's one sentence I hear from surgeons that tells me everything about their financial risk. Six words: "My practice IS my retirement plan."

I understand why surgeons say this. The practice throws off significant income. It has value that can theoretically be monetized at exit. It feels like a self-contained financial engine. Why would you need anything else?

Here's why that sentence is so dangerous. A retirement plan has specific characteristics. It compounds over time through reinvestment. It's diversified across multiple asset classes. It's protected against a range of risks (market downturns, disability, death). And it's structured to convert accumulated value into reliable income at a specific point in the future.

Your practice has none of those characteristics.

A practice doesn't compound. The value doesn't grow automatically just because you own it. It grows only if you actively invest time, energy, and capital into it.

A practice isn't diversified. It's a single asset in a single industry in a single geographic market. Its value depends on reimbursement rates, referral patterns, your personal health, and local competition.

A practice isn't protected against the risks that matter most. If you're injured and can't operate for 12 months, the practice value declines. If CMS cuts reimbursement further, the practice economics change. If a hospital system opens a competing orthopedic center two miles away, your patient volume is at risk.

And a practice doesn't automatically convert to retirement income. Selling a practice is a complex, multi-year process that depends on market conditions, buyer interest, your personal goodwill documentation, and your willingness to navigate the transaction.

When a surgeon says "my practice is my retirement plan," they're treating a concentrated, illiquid, undiversified operating asset as though it were a 401(k). That conflation creates a false sense of security that delays the actual work of building a diversified financial foundation.

The practice can be part of a retirement plan. A significant part. But the sentence works only when it includes one more word: "My practice is part of my retirement plan."

## SEGMENT 3: Three De-Risking Strategies (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

So what do you do about concentration risk in your practice? Here are three strategies that reduce your exposure without requiring you to sell tomorrow.

**Strategy 1: Diversify Revenue Sources Within the Practice**

The most immediate form of de-risking is reducing the practice's dependence on your personal production. That could mean bringing on an associate who generates independent revenue. It could mean developing an ASC (ambulatory surgery center) ownership stake that creates income beyond your surgical fees. Or it could mean building ancillary revenue streams (imaging, physical therapy partnerships, product royalties) that don't depend on you being in the OR.

Every revenue source that doesn't require your hands, your time, or your physical presence in the building reduces the concentration risk. It also, critically, increases the practice's value to a future buyer, because a practice that can generate revenue without the founding surgeon is worth more than one that can't.

**Strategy 2: Establish Personal Goodwill Documentation Now**

Let me give you the math on personal goodwill, because this is one of the most powerful and most overlooked planning moves for any surgeon within five years of a potential practice transition.

Personal goodwill is the value attributable to your individual reputation, referral relationships, surgical skill, and patient loyalty. It's distinct from enterprise goodwill (the practice's systems, brand, and transferable operations).

On a $4M practice sale, without personal goodwill documentation, the proceeds may be taxed at ordinary income rates: 37% federal plus 3.8% NIIT, for a combined rate of 40.8%. The tax bill: approximately $1.63M.

With personal goodwill properly documented and established before the sale, the proceeds can be characterized as long-term capital gains: 20% federal plus 3.8% NIIT, or 23.8%. The tax bill: approximately $952K.

That's $678,000. Same deal. Same price. Same practice. The only variable is documentation that must be in place years before any transaction.

Non-compete agreements, referral source analysis, patient loyalty metrics, personal branding evidence. All of this can be established right now, regardless of whether you plan to sell in three years or ten. The cost of establishing it is minimal. The cost of not having it when you need it is $678,000.

**Strategy 3: Build a Transition Timeline (Even If You're Not Planning to Leave)**

The third strategy is perhaps the most counterintuitive: start planning your exit even if you have no intention of exiting soon.

A transition timeline isn't a commitment to sell. It's a framework that ensures you have options when you want them. The timeline includes establishing a valuation baseline (what is the practice actually worth today?), documenting personal goodwill, identifying potential successor structures, and building the operational independence that makes the practice transferable.

The surgeons who get the best outcomes in practice transitions, whether they sell to PE, transition to a partner, or wind down gradually, are the ones who started planning five years before the event. That lead time creates leverage. It creates choices. And it prevents the compressed, reactive decision-making that leads to suboptimal outcomes.

## SEGMENT 4: PE as One Form of De-Risking (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Conference season means PE conversations. So let's address this directly: private equity can be a legitimate de-risking strategy. Selling a portion of your practice value to a PE firm converts concentrated, illiquid equity into cash and diversifiable assets. That's real de-risking.

But PE is one option, not the only option. And the terms of the deal determine whether it actually reduces your risk or just restructures it.

Consider a surgeon looking at a PE pitch deck. It's beautiful. It's also missing four things.

First, rollover equity. Most PE deals require the surgeon to reinvest 30% to 35% of the sale price in the acquiring entity. On a $4M deal, that's $1.4M that stays illiquid, in someone else's control, for another 3 to 5 years. You've traded one form of concentration for another.

Second, compensation adjustments. Post-acquisition, surgeon compensation often declines 15% to 25% through productivity thresholds, management fees, and operational changes. The Year 1 guarantee is not the long-term reality.

Third, EBITDA normalization. How the buyer calculates your practice's earnings directly affects valuation and earn-out targets. Creative normalization can inflate the headline number while setting performance targets that are difficult to hit.

Fourth, the hold period. For 3 to 5 years after the sale, the surgeon is an employee of the acquiring entity. Reduced autonomy. New reporting structures. New compensation formulas. The financial trade-off may be worth it. The lifestyle trade-off deserves equal analysis.

I'm not anti-PE. Some PE deals are excellent, and the surgeon comes out with genuine financial freedom and a practice environment they're happy with. The key is walking into any conversation with the same diagnostic rigor you'd apply to a complex surgical case. Understand the full picture. Evaluate the terms. And make sure the deal structure actually serves your goals, not just the pitch deck's narrative.

## SEGMENT 5: Your Practice as an Investment (3-5 minutes)

Here's a question almost no surgeon has ever been asked: what return are you earning on your practice?

I don't mean revenue. I don't mean collections. I mean: if you treat your practice ownership as an investment of both capital and time, what is the actual return on that investment?

Here's how to think about it. Your practice has a market value (let's say $3M, based on a conservative appraisal). You are also investing your time in it, time that has an opportunity cost. If you're working 2,400 clinical hours per year and your effective hourly take-home is $225, your total annual take-home from the practice is roughly $540K.

That $540K represents the "dividend" your practice pays you. On a $3M asset, that's an 18% annual return. Which sounds excellent.

But now factor in risk. This return comes from a single, illiquid, non-diversified asset that depends on your continued health, reimbursement stability, and local market conditions. An 18% return with that risk profile is... acceptable. Maybe. But it's not the slam dunk it appears on the surface, because a diversified portfolio earning 8% with dramatically lower risk and full liquidity may produce comparable risk-adjusted returns over a 20-year horizon.

The point of this exercise isn't to convince you to sell your practice. It's to convince you to analyze it. Treat your practice ownership with the same financial rigor you'd apply to any asset of comparable size. Calculate the return. Assess the risk. And ask yourself whether the concentration is intentional or accidental.

## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

Here's one thing to do this week, especially if you're at a conference and the practice transition conversation is swirling around you.

Calculate your concentration ratio. Add up the value of all your assets: practice, home equity, investment accounts, retirement accounts, any other holdings. Then divide your practice value by the total.

If the number is above 40%, you have meaningful concentration risk. That's not a crisis. It's a diagnosis. And like any good diagnosis, it points directly to a treatment plan.

The three strategies we covered today (diversifying revenue, documenting personal goodwill, and building a transition timeline) are available to you right now, regardless of whether you plan to sell in two years or twenty. Each one reduces your concentration risk and increases your optionality.

The worst time to start de-risking is when you need to. The best time is before the need becomes urgent.

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

You built your practice with precision, skill, and an enormous investment of your best years. It represents not just financial value, but professional identity, community standing, and the tangible result of decades of training and work.

It deserves to be treated with the same financial rigor you'd apply to any asset of its size. Not because the practice is "just an investment" (it's far more than that), but because the financial decisions surrounding it affect everything else in your life. Your retirement security. Your family's future. Your ability to choose what comes next on your own terms.

The question isn't whether you'll exit your practice eventually. You will. Every surgeon does. The question is whether that exit will be on your terms, at a time of your choosing, with a structure that preserves the value you've built. Or whether it will be reactive, compressed, and shaped by circumstances you didn't anticipate.

Concentration risk is the gap between those two outcomes. Closing that gap starts with seeing it clearly.
