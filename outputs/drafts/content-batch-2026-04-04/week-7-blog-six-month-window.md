Tax season is wrapping up. In a few days, your CPA will be somewhere between relieved and exhausted. You'll click "submit" (or "extend"), and then you'll move on.

Back to the OR. Back to the schedule. Back to the rhythm that defines most of your year.

And that instinct to move on? That's exactly the problem.

Because the six months between April 15 and October 15 are when the most valuable financial moves of the year get made. Or missed. The difference between those two outcomes comes down to whether you have a protocol for the window, or whether you just let it close.

## The Window Most Surgeons Waste

Consider a surgeon earning $820K through an S-Corp, who files an extension every year and doesn't think about taxes again until October. That's not unusual. It's the norm for high-income practice owners with demanding schedules.

But here's what happens in that gap. Six months pass. The S-Corp salary stays where it was last year. The retirement plan stays the same. The entity structure stays the same. And by the time October rolls around, every meaningful optimization window has either narrowed or closed.

The cost of inaction during this period is real. Based on current tax law and typical surgeon income levels, the missed optimization value sits between $35,000 and $60,000 in Year 1 alone. Compounded over a decade, that gap becomes six figures.

The surgeons who capture that value aren't doing anything exotic. They're following a protocol, the same way you'd follow a post-operative rehab timeline. Sequenced, time-bound, and grounded in the principle that early action compounds better than late reaction.

## The Four-Move Protocol

Here are four specific moves that are best initiated during the April-to-October window, each with a deadline that matters.

### Move 1: S-Corp Salary Recalibration (Deadline: May 15)

Your S-Corp reasonable compensation was probably set when your practice looked different than it does today. Revenue has changed. Reimbursement has changed. Your retirement plan structure may have changed. But the salary? That number tends to stay frozen in time.

Recalibrating your S-Corp salary in the first half of the year gives your payroll provider time to adjust withholding and gives your retirement plan administrator time to update contribution calculations. Waiting until Q4 compresses both.

Here's the math that makes this worth prioritizing. A surgeon currently paying herself $300,000 through the S-Corp, who recalibrates to $350,000, unlocks an additional $20,000 or more in annual retirement plan contributions (because employer match and cash balance plan funding are both calculated against W-2 compensation). The incremental payroll tax cost of the higher salary is roughly $7,650 (the additional $50,000 times the 15.3% self-employment equivalent, though only the Medicare portion applies above the FICA cap). The net benefit: $12,000 to $15,000 per year in additional tax-deferred compounding.

That's a one-time conversation with your payroll provider. May 15 gives you enough runway to implement cleanly for the full second half of the year.

### Move 2: Cash Balance Plan Establishment (Deadline: July 1)

If you're over 50, earning $700K or more, and you don't have a cash balance plan, this is the single highest-leverage structural addition available to you.

A cash balance plan allows annual tax-deferred contributions of $150,000 to $350,000 (depending on age, compensation, and plan design), on top of your 401(k). But there's a critical timing constraint: establishing a new plan requires plan document preparation, actuarial certification, and IRS compliance setup. That process takes 6 to 10 weeks from engagement to completion.

Starting in April or May gives you a July 1 effective date, which means half-year funding of $75,000 to $150,000 in the first year. Waiting until September or October usually means the opportunity is lost for the current tax year entirely, because plan documents can't be backdated and actuaries don't work on compressed timelines.

The July 1 date is the practical deadline for meaningful first-year funding. Every month of delay reduces what you can shelter.

### Move 3: Entity Restructuring Evaluation (Deadline: August 31)

If your practice structure hasn't been reviewed in three or more years, the April-to-October window is the right time to evaluate whether your current entity setup still serves your financial goals.

Common triggers for restructuring: you've added a partner or associate, your revenue has shifted meaningfully, you're considering an ASC ownership stake, or you're within five years of a potential practice transition. Any of these changes may warrant a shift from a single-entity S-Corp to a multi-entity structure, or vice versa.

Entity restructuring takes time. Legal documents need drafting. Tax elections need filing. Operating agreements need updating. And all of this must be complete before January 1 to be effective for the next tax year. August 31 is the latest practical start date to ensure a clean implementation by year-end.

### Move 4: Roth Conversion Analysis (Deadline: September 30)

With OBBBA making the 37% top rate permanent, the traditional calculus on Roth conversions has shifted. The question is no longer "should I do a Roth conversion?" The question is "at what rate am I converting, and is that rate higher or lower than what I'll pay in retirement?"

The answer depends on your full-year income picture, which starts to clarify in Q2 and Q3. Running the conversion analysis in the summer, when you have enough data to model but enough time to execute, lets you identify specific conversion amounts that optimize for your projected retirement tax bracket, your state tax trajectory, and your estate planning goals.

September 30 is the latest date to run this analysis with enough time to execute a conversion before December 31. Wait until November, and you're guessing at income numbers instead of modeling them.

## The Protocol in Practice

Here's what this looks like for a surgeon who files an extension on April 15 and commits to the protocol.

By May 15, the S-Corp salary has been recalibrated from $300,000 to $350,000. The payroll adjustment is in place and retirement plan contributions are being calculated against the new W-2.

By July 1, a cash balance plan has been established with a half-year funding target of $100,000. The plan document is filed. The first contribution is scheduled.

By August 31, an entity restructuring evaluation has been completed. The review identifies an opportunity to separate personal goodwill into a distinct entity in preparation for a potential practice transition in three to five years. Legal documents are in progress for a January 1 effective date.

By September 30, a Roth conversion analysis identifies a $40,000 conversion opportunity, taking advantage of a quarter where income was lower than projected due to a surgical conference and vacation schedule.

Total first-year value of these four moves: $35,000 to $60,000 in tax-advantaged savings and structural optimization. If the same surgeon had waited until October or later, Move 2 (the cash balance plan) would have been impossible and Move 4 (the Roth conversion) would have been severely constrained by incomplete data. The practical value of starting early: roughly $40,000 to $50,000 in captured opportunity that would have otherwise expired.

## The Underlying Principle

In surgery, the preparation before the procedure determines the outcome more than the procedure itself. Pre-operative planning, imaging review, patient positioning, instrument selection: all of that happens before the first incision. And it's what separates a good outcome from a great one.

Your financial life works the same way. The six months of preparation between April 15 and year-end determine whether December is a scramble or a celebration. Whether you're reacting to deadlines or executing a plan you built six months earlier.

The surgeons who build wealth most efficiently aren't the ones with the highest collections. They're the ones who treat the planning window with the same discipline they bring to the OR.

April 15 is next week. The window opens the day after. The question is whether you'll walk through it with a protocol, or let it close with a promise to "get to it next year."
