## Content

### Caption Script

There's one sentence I hear from surgeons that tells me everything about their financial risk. Six words: "My practice IS my retirement plan."

I understand why surgeons say this. The practice generates significant income. It has value that can be monetized at exit. It feels like a self-contained financial engine.

But a retirement plan has specific characteristics. It compounds through reinvestment. It's diversified across multiple asset classes. It's protected against risks like disability and market shifts. And it converts accumulated value into reliable income at a specific point in the future.

Your practice has none of those characteristics.

It doesn't compound automatically. It doesn't diversify your risk. If you can't operate for 12 months, the practice value declines. And selling a practice requires a complex, multi-year process that depends on market conditions you can't control.

Your practice can be part of a retirement plan. A significant part. But when it IS the retirement plan, you're treating a concentrated, illiquid, undiversified operating asset as though it were a 401(k). That's the definition of concentration risk.
