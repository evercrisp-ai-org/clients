## Clip Script

**[COLD START]**

37% is the top federal tax rate. And under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, it is now permanent.

That single fact changes the Roth conversion math for surgeons — and most people haven't updated their thinking.

The traditional argument was: convert now, while rates are low, before they go up. At a 37% permanent top rate, that argument gets more complicated.

The question is not "should I do the backdoor Roth" — at this income level, the $7,500 IRA contribution is a small part of the puzzle. The question is whether a strategic conversion of $50,000 to $200,000 makes sense this year specifically.

And the answer depends on five things working together.

First, your combined rate — federal plus state. A California surgeon pays 50.3% on conversion income. Paying that today to avoid 25% in retirement is the wrong direction.

Second, your retirement state. Planning to retire in a no-income-tax state? That 13.3% you save on every converted dollar changes the math substantially.

Third, Medicare IRMAA. Roth conversion income counts in MAGI. A $100,000 conversion could add $2,000 to $5,000 in annual Medicare surcharges for two years. Model it.

Fourth, the charitable offset. A $100,000 DAF contribution this year can offset $100,000 of Roth conversion income. These strategies coordinate.

Fifth, your RMD trajectory. If you have $3 million in pre-tax retirement accounts and no Roth assets, the required distributions starting at 73 could push your taxable income well above your current marginal rate. Converting now — at a known rate — prevents the surprise later.

July is when you model this. Not October.
