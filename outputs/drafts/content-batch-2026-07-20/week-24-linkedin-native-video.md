## Talking Points with Timestamps

**[0:00-0:10] — Hook**
$9,750. That's the 2026 HSA maximum for a surgeon 55 or older — the only triple-tax vehicle in the tax code. Most surgeons contribute zero to it, because they're enrolled in a plan that doesn't qualify.

**[0:10-0:25] — The opportunity**
Open enrollment, in October or November, is the one annual window to correct that. The HDHP-HSA combination: contributions are deductible, growth is tax-deferred, and qualified distributions are tax-free. At a 37% marginal rate, the $9,750 maximum produces $3,608 in immediate federal tax savings — and over a 10-year window at 7% growth, the compounded difference approaches $135,000 in tax-free assets.

**[0:25-0:40] — The disqualifier**
The most common trap: a spouse on a non-HDHP employer plan that covers the family. If family coverage isn't through the HDHP, HSA eligibility is gone — even with an open account. This is an enrollment-window fix, not a year-round fix. You can only correct the plan election in October or November.

**[0:40-0:55] — The August action**
Before the enrollment window opens: confirm whether your current plan is HDHP-qualified (minimum deductible of $1,650 self-only or $3,300 family in 2026), and run the HDHP-PPO net analysis with your advisor. The enrollment packet shouldn't be the first time you're making this decision.

**[0:55-1:00] — Diagnostic close**
Is your current health plan HDHP-qualified? If you don't know, that's the conversation to have in August.
