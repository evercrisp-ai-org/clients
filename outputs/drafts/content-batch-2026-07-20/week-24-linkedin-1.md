## Content

$9,750. That's the maximum HSA contribution for a surgeon over 55 in 2026 — $8,750 family limit plus the $1,000 catch-up.

Most orthopedic surgeons contribute $0, because they're enrolled in a plan that doesn't qualify.

Open enrollment isn't a benefits administration event. It's the one annual window to correct that.

The HDHP-HSA combination is the only triple-tax structure in the tax code: deductible contributions, tax-deferred growth, and tax-free distributions for qualified medical expenses. At a 37% marginal rate, the $9,750 maximum produces $3,608 in immediate federal tax savings — before a dollar of growth. Over a 10-year pre-retirement window at 7% growth, the compounded difference between maxing and not maxing approaches $135,000 in tax-free assets.

The disqualifier that catches most surgeons: a spouse on a non-HDHP employer plan that covers the family. If the family coverage isn't through the HDHP, the surgeon isn't eligible to contribute — even with an open HSA account.

The analysis takes one conversation with your advisor and one call to HR. August is the right time for both of them — before the enrollment window opens in October.

The question worth asking before enrollment: is my current health plan HDHP-qualified?
