## Content

$135,000. That's the approximate compounded difference — over 10 years at 7% growth — between a surgeon who maxes the HSA and one who doesn't.

The health savings account is the only triple-tax vehicle in the tax code. Contributions are deductible. Growth is tax-deferred. Qualified distributions are tax-free. For a surgeon at the 37% marginal rate, the 2026 maximum contribution of $9,750 (for a surgeon 55 or older) produces $3,608 in immediate federal tax savings per year.

The catch: the HSA requires enrollment in a High Deductible Health Plan. Many orthopedic surgeons either aren't enrolled in an HDHP or think they are but aren't — because the deductible threshold has moved.

Open enrollment, in October or November, is the annual window to fix the plan election. The analysis — whether the current plan qualifies, whether the HDHP is the better net option, whether the spouse's plan creates a disqualifier — takes one conversation with an advisor.

That conversation should happen in August, before the window opens.
