## Content

Here is a scenario worth thinking about.

A surgeon has $200,000 in appreciated stock she has been planning to give to charity for two years. In November, she calls her advisor: "Let's finally do the donor-advised fund."

The advisor submits the application. Tells her it takes six to eight weeks to process. The stock transfer takes another two weeks to settle.

By the time the transfer clears, it is January 4th. The 2026 deduction has moved to 2027. The $74,000 income tax savings and the $47,600 in avoided capital gains she expected in her current-year return won't arrive until next filing season — if she plans carefully for it then.

She did not make a mistake. Her charitable intent was real. Her financial planning was sound in principle. The timing was wrong by about three months.

Year-end tax planning does not fail on strategy. It fails on initiation date.

The moves that require the most planning — charitable vehicle setup, retirement plan amendments, Roth conversion modeling — are also the moves that require the longest lead time. By November, two of those windows are either closed or badly compressed.

The surgeons who capture the full value of year-end planning start the conversation in July.

What year-end move are you still waiting to begin?
