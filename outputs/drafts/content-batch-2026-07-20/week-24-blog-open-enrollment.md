## Content

Open enrollment is treated as a benefits administration event in most surgical practices. You receive a packet, confirm your dependents are still on the plan, and move on.

That's a missed tax planning window. For an orthopedic surgeon at $1 million in income, the benefit elections you make in November for the following January are a tax decision worth reviewing with the same attention you'd give a retirement plan amendment.

**The HSA: The One Account That Survives Both the Working Years and Retirement**

The health savings account is the only triple-tax vehicle in the code: contributions are deductible, growth is tax-deferred, and distributions for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.

For 2026, the family HSA contribution limit is $8,750. At age 55 or older, the catch-up contribution adds another $1,000. For a surgeon at 57, the combined limit is $9,750.

At a 37% marginal rate, maxing the HSA produces $3,608 in immediate federal tax savings per year. Over a 10-year pre-retirement window, with 7% annual growth, the compounded difference between maxing and not maxing the HSA approaches $135,000 in tax-free assets.

The catch: HSA eligibility requires enrollment in a High Deductible Health Plan. For 2026, the HDHP minimum deductible is $1,650 for self-only coverage and $3,300 for family coverage. If your current plan doesn't meet that threshold, you are ineligible to contribute to an HSA regardless of whether you have the account open.

Open enrollment is when this gets resolved. If your current plan is not HDHP-qualified, the window to switch is October or November, for a January 1 effective date.

**The HDHP-HSA Analysis for Surgeons**

The standard objection to an HDHP is out-of-pocket exposure. Orthopedic surgeons, who often have access to workplace medical resources and whose spouses or families may have specific ongoing healthcare needs, are right to evaluate this carefully.

The analysis worth running in August: compare the premium savings and HSA contribution benefit of the HDHP against the expected out-of-pocket cost differential from your current plan. For most surgeons with relatively low utilization, the HDHP-HSA combination wins on a net basis by a significant margin. The tax savings alone frequently exceed the additional out-of-pocket risk.

The one scenario where it doesn't: enrollment in any other disqualifying coverage. If a spouse's non-HDHP employer plan covers the surgeon or the family, the surgeon is ineligible for HSA contributions. Medicare enrollment at 65 also disqualifies. These are the two most common traps that produce unnecessary HSA disqualification.

**FSA Coordination**

If your plan includes a Flexible Spending Account, the 2026 contribution limit is $3,300 for healthcare FSAs. FSA dollars reduce taxable income in the year of contribution — the same mechanism as the HSA, with important differences.

FSAs are use-it-or-lose-it within the plan year (with a limited carryover or grace period depending on the plan). They cannot be invested or compounded. And they cannot be paired with an HSA on a standard basis — an FSA and an HSA cannot coexist unless the FSA is a limited-purpose FSA (dental and vision only).

For a surgeon optimizing for the HSA, the election decision is: decline the standard FSA, elect the HDHP, and maximize the HSA. A limited-purpose FSA can then layer on top without disqualifying HSA eligibility.

**The Dependent Care FSA**

A separate vehicle often overlooked in surgical households with younger children or eldercare responsibilities: the dependent care FSA contributes up to $5,000 per household in pre-tax dollars for qualifying care expenses. For two-income households, this is separate from the healthcare FSA and does not affect HSA eligibility.

If your household has qualifying dependent care expenses — childcare, after-school programs, adult day care for an aging parent — this election should be made at open enrollment. It produces $1,850 in federal tax savings at the 37% rate on the maximum $5,000 contribution.

**The August Diagnostic**

Before open enrollment opens in October, three questions are worth answering now.

Is your current health plan HDHP-qualified? If you don't know the deductible threshold, the answer is probably no — and the answer should be known before the enrollment window.

Are you maxing your HSA? If your plan is HDHP-qualified and you're not contributing $9,750 as a 55+ surgeon, that's the first dollar of tax savings that should be confirmed before any other planning conversation.

Is there a dependent care FSA available in your benefits package that you haven't elected? If the expense qualifies, the $1,850 in savings is immediate.

Open enrollment produces its best outcomes when the decisions are made in August, not November.
