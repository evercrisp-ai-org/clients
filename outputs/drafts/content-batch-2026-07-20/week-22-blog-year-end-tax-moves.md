## Content

November is when most financial content about year-end tax planning shows up in your inbox. That timing is wrong.

Not because the advice is bad. The advice is usually fine. The problem is that two of the five most powerful year-end moves for a surgeon practice owner require August initiation at the absolute latest — and one of them is best started in July. By the time November arrives, those windows are closed.

Here is a cleaner way to think about year-end planning: it is not a December sprint. It is a five-month protocol with staged execution points, each tied to a specific deadline. Miss the August checkpoint, and you do not just delay the strategy — you eliminate it from the 2026 tax year entirely.

**Why Year-End Planning Fails**

Consider a surgeon who intends to establish a donor-advised fund this year. The plan is straightforward: transfer $200,000 of appreciated stock to the fund, take the charitable deduction, and avoid the capital gains that would otherwise be triggered by selling the position. In combined income tax and capital gains terms, the value of that strategy at a 37% marginal rate is approximately $121,600 in tax savings on the $200,000 position.

The surgeon waits until November, as most people do. The account application at a major DAF sponsor takes six weeks to approve and fund. The stock transfer takes another two weeks to settle. By the time the transfer is complete, it is mid-January — and the deduction has moved to the 2027 tax year. The opportunity is not lost permanently, but the 2026 benefit is gone.

That is not a tax code failure. It is a timing failure. And it is entirely preventable.

**The Five Moves — and Why Each Has Its Own Clock**

**Move 1: Charitable Vehicle Setup**

A donor-advised fund is the most powerful giving vehicle for high-income surgeons, allowing appreciated securities to be transferred directly (avoiding capital gains), contributions to be deducted at full fair market value (up to 60% of AGI for cash, 30% for appreciated assets), and the giving itself to happen over time even after the contribution.

The setup clock: major DAF sponsors (Schwab, Fidelity Charitable, Vanguard Charitable) take four to eight weeks to process and fund new accounts. The stock transfer itself then takes additional time to settle and be acknowledged for the current tax year. To ensure the 2026 deduction, accounts should be established and funded by October 31 at the absolute latest — which means the paperwork starts in September. Given typical summer processing slowdowns and the fact that appreciated positions need to be identified and earmarked, July or August is the right initiation window.

**Move 2: Equipment and Technology Purchases Under Section 179**

Section 179 allows the immediate expensing of qualifying business equipment and technology — surgical tools, imaging equipment, practice management software, computers — in the year of purchase, rather than depreciated over time. For surgeon practice owners, this can produce $50,000 to $150,000 or more in deductible expenses depending on what the practice needs.

The planning clock: Section 179 purchases need to be placed in service by December 31. Equipment financing, vendor contracting, and delivery lead times mean that significant purchases should be identified by September and ordered no later than October to ensure in-service status before year-end. More importantly, Section 179 planning requires knowing the practice's approximate current-year income — which means starting the analysis now, when the mid-year picture is clear from the halftime report.

*Note: Verify the 2026 Section 179 deduction limit before finalizing this piece. The 2025 limit was $1.16 million.*

**Move 3: Retirement Plan Amendments**

This is the move most surgeons and their advisors overlook, because it sounds administrative. It is not.

Cash balance plans, 401(k) plans, and profit-sharing plans can be amended — their contribution formulas changed, new features added, or plan designs restructured — but these amendments have hard deadlines tied to the plan year. For most calendar-year plans, significant structural amendments must be adopted by September 30 to apply to the 2026 plan year. If the surgeon missed the July 1 window to establish a new cash balance plan entirely (as discussed in the Week 22 research scan), a September 30 amendment to an existing plan may still allow increased contribution levels.

A surgeon over 55 with an existing cash balance plan that was established with conservative contribution targets may be leaving $50,000 to $100,000 of additional tax-deferred capacity on the table — capacity that a plan amendment could unlock, if initiated before September 30.

*Note: Confirm the specific amendment deadline for the most common cash balance and 401(k) structures before publishing. September 30 applies to many plans but the rule depends on plan type.*

**Move 4: Roth Conversion Analysis**

Roth conversion strategy is best modeled when the full-year income picture is reasonably clear — not in January (too early) and not in November (too late to implement the full strategy). July is the natural modeling window.

With OBBBA making the 37% top rate permanent, the traditional "convert now while rates are low" argument needs sharper reasoning. The conversion is a bet that future tax rates will be higher than current ones. For most surgeons at $700K to $2M in income, the short-term rate environment is fixed. The conversion analysis should focus on the interaction between Roth conversion income and other year-end moves: does a $75,000 Roth conversion push income into a range that triggers Medicare IRMAA surcharges? Does it interact with the QBI deduction? Does it change the optimal charitable giving strategy?

These questions require a full-year income projection and time to implement the answer before year-end. July is when that analysis starts.

**Move 5: Installment Sale Structuring**

For surgeons considering a partial practice sale, a real estate transaction, or any event that will generate capital gains in 2026, installment sale structuring must be decided before the transaction closes. An installment sale allows the seller to receive proceeds over multiple years, spreading the capital gain across tax years and potentially keeping each year below the top bracket thresholds.

Once a sale closes without installment election, the option is gone. The planning clock is the transaction timeline itself — which means this conversation needs to happen now if a closing is anticipated before December 31.

**The Protocol, Applied**

A 56-year-old surgeon approaching year-end with income tracking at $950,000 for 2026 has approximately five months left to capture these five moves. If she initiates all five in July and August: the DAF is established and the stock position identified; the Section 179 purchase list is finalized; the cash balance plan amendment is filed by September 30; the Roth conversion analysis is modeled and implemented in October; and the installment sale election is made before the Q4 closing.

Total first-year tax savings from starting in July: approximately $170,000 to $210,000, depending on the specific numbers. The same surgeon who waits until November captures perhaps $40,000 to $60,000, because only the Roth conversion and the Section 179 purchase (if she moves quickly) remain available.

The gap is not tax code complexity. The gap is timing.

**The Diagnostic**

The mid-year mark has passed. The halftime report is done. The RVU productivity data is in. You have a clear picture of where you stand at the halfway point of 2026.

The question now is what you do with that picture.

Most year-end tax strategies that feel urgent in November were available in July. The surgeons who capture the most of them are not the ones with the most sophisticated advisors. They are the ones who started the conversation five months before everyone else.

That conversation starts in July. Not August. Not November.
