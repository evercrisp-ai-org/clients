$200K deferred into a sale year is a tax mistake hiding as a tax move.

Consider a surgeon planning to sell her practice next year. Her instinct, reinforced by years of routine, is to push income out of this year and into next. Lower this year's bill, deal with next year later.

Next year is the worst possible landing spot. The sale stacks ordinary income and capital gains into one enormous year, pushing more of the total into the top bracket and across the 3.8% net investment income threshold. Every dollar she defers arrives at the most expensive moment she will ever have.

The correct move runs the other direction. With a heavy year coming, she should accelerate income into this year, filling the lower brackets while they are still open and empty.

Same surgeon, same dollars, opposite decision, all because next year is not like a normal year.

This is the question deferral on autopilot never asks: not "can I push this income out," but "what does the year I am pushing it into actually look like?"

If a transition is anywhere on your five-year horizon, which year are you quietly loading up?
