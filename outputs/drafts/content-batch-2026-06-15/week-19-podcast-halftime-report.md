## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

The fall financial review feels like the responsible thing to do. Here is the problem with it: by the time fall arrives, most of your year is already written, and the moves that could have changed it are off the table.

Today I want to give you a different habit, a halftime report you can run in one Saturday. Five numbers, each next to last year, each ending in a decision. It is the difference between shaping your second half and just watching it happen.

---
## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

We are at the midpoint of the year. The first half is essentially done, the summer rhythm is setting in, and for most surgeons there is finally a quiet morning available. That window is the most underused planning asset you have, because it is the one moment where you have real data and real time to act on it.

This matters more at surgeon income levels, where the numbers are large and the calendar is unforgiving. A small drift caught in July is a quick correction. The same drift discovered in December is a missed year.

---
## SEGMENT 1: Why Halftime Beats Year-End (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

There is a reason year-end planning disappoints so many high earners. By December, payroll cannot reasonably add a large employer contribution, the overhead drift has already happened, and your income is what it is. You are reviewing a finished story.

A halftime report is a midpoint check, the same idea as confirming the vital signs partway through a long case. You are not rebuilding the plan. You are catching the variables that drifted and adjusting while the adjustment still changes the outcome.

---
## SEGMENT 2: The Five Numbers (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Five numbers carry the whole report.

One, income versus projection. Annualize your year-to-date compensation and distributions and compare to your plan.

Two, tax pacing. Set taxes paid against projected liability, now that the Q2 estimate is behind you.

Three, retirement progress. Where are you against the $72,000 defined contribution limit, and against any cash balance target.

Four, practice overhead. Your overhead ratio versus last year and your benchmark.

Five, personal spending rate. The real percentage of after-tax income leaving the household.

Each number sits next to last year. Each gets one decision.

---
## SEGMENT 3: The Retirement Pacing Gap (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Let me sit on number three, because it is the one that quietly costs the most. The 2026 defined contribution limit is $72,000. A surgeon intending to max it should be near $36,000 by July.

Picture a surgeon who is only at $18,000, because the 401(k) deferral has been pacing but the employer and profit-sharing pieces have not. That is not a small gap. And it cannot be fixed with a December wish, because payroll has to actually run the contributions across remaining paychecks. Caught in July, it is a simple schedule change. Caught in December, it is a contribution you legally cannot make.

---
## SEGMENT 4: Turning the Report Into Decisions (3-5 minutes)

The point of the exercise is not a tidy spreadsheet. It is a page with five numbers and five decisions. Adjust the September estimate. Reset the retirement pacing with payroll. Investigate the overhead drift before the cause hardens. Decide whether the spending rate holds. Confirm whether income ahead of plan changes your timing decisions for the rest of the year.

Run solo, plan on four or five hours. Run with an advisor who already holds your numbers, it is closer to two hours and a far sharper conversation, because the data gathering is done and the time goes to judgment.

---
## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

One action this week: block a single Saturday morning and pull five numbers onto one page, each beside last year's figure. If any line is off pace, write the one decision that corrects it and the date it happens. That page is your halftime report, and it will do more for your second half than any year-end scramble.

---
## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

You run midpoint checks in surgery because you know that catching drift early is the whole game. Outcomes are decided long before the final step.

Your financial life is no different. Control does not come from one heroic decision at year-end. It comes from the quiet discipline of looking honestly, on time, while the corrections are still small. The year is half written. The rest is still yours to shape, if you choose to look.
