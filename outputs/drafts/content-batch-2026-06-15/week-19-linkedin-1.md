$72,000 in tax-deferred space, and most of it sits empty at midyear.

That is the 2026 defined contribution limit. A surgeon who intends to use all of it should be near $36,000 funded by now. Plenty are sitting closer to $18,000, because the 401(k) deferral has been pacing through payroll while the employer and profit-sharing pieces quietly have not.

Your CPA files the return. Your payroll provider runs what it is told to run. Unless someone is actively pacing the employer contributions across the year, that capacity does not fill itself.

Here is the part that costs real money: this is a midyear problem, not a December one. Contributions have to physically run across your remaining paychecks. Caught now, it is a schedule change. Caught at year-end, it is sheltered space you legally cannot reclaim, and the difference compounds for decades.

The empty room in that account is not a small oversight. At surgeon income, it is one of the largest silent leaks on the books, and it is completely fixable in July.

Where do you actually stand against $36,000 right now?
