A cash balance plan is the single largest tax-deferred shelter most surgeons will ever have access to, and it is also the one with a quiet trapdoor. Depending on age and plan design, it can absorb $150,000 to $350,000 or more in annual contributions, all of it deductible, all of it compounding tax-deferred. The trapdoor is that the plan commits you to a funding range, and treating that commitment like a year-end suggestion is how surgeons walk into penalties and, in the worst cases, a failed plan.

We are at the midpoint of the year. That makes this the right week to ask a blunt question: are you actually on pace?

## A Cash Balance Plan Is a Promise, Not a Piggy Bank

A 401(k) is flexible. You can dial deferrals up or down, and if you contribute nothing in a given month, nothing breaks. A cash balance plan does not work that way. It is a defined benefit plan, which means an actuary sets a required contribution range each year, and the plan expects that range to be funded. Chronic underfunding can trigger an excise tax on the funding shortfall and, if it becomes a pattern, can jeopardize the plan's qualified status entirely.

That is what makes mid-year pacing matter. The plan is a promise you made in January. July is when you confirm you are keeping it.

## The Pacing Math

Consider a 55-year-old surgeon whose plan is designed for a $250,000 annual contribution, layered on top of a 401(k). To stay on schedule, that surgeon should be roughly halfway funded by mid-July, around $125,000 contributed.

Now layer the rest of the stack for 2026. The 401(k) elective deferral is $24,500, with an $8,000 catch-up at age 50 and older, and the full defined contribution side can reach $72,000 with employer and profit-sharing pieces. Add the cash balance plan and a surgeon in their fifties can be deferring well north of $300,000 a year. That is the most powerful wealth-building structure available to a high-income practice owner, and it only works if the funding actually arrives on schedule.

If our surgeon is sitting at $40,000 against a $125,000 mid-year target, that is not a rounding issue. It is a signal that the funding plan and the payroll reality have come apart, and there are still six months to fix it before the gap becomes a problem the actuary has to report.

## What Underfunding Actually Costs

Three things happen when a cash balance plan is chronically underfunded. First, the deduction you were counting on shrinks, raising the current-year tax bill you built the plan to reduce. Second, a funding deficiency can carry an excise tax. Third, and most damaging, a plan that repeatedly misses its required contributions can lose its qualified status, which unwinds years of tax-deferred treatment in a single stroke.

None of that happens because the surgeon did anything reckless. It happens because the contributions were treated as a December decision when they were really a year-long obligation that payroll and cash flow had to support every month.

## The Mid-Year Confirmation

The check itself is short. Pull your cash balance plan's target contribution for the year. Divide by where you should be at this point in the funding schedule. Compare that to what has actually gone in. Then confirm three things with your advisor and plan administrator: that the contributions are pacing, that cash flow supports the remaining funding through year-end, and that the 401(k) and profit-sharing pieces are coordinated so you are not accidentally tripping a contribution limit.

If everything is on pace, the call takes fifteen minutes and you move on with confidence. If it is not, you have found the most expensive gap on your books while there is still time to close it cleanly.

## The Bigger Point

The cash balance plan is a structure built for surgeons who think in long horizons, which is to say, all of you. It rewards consistency and punishes improvisation, the same way a well-run practice does. The contribution is not a year-end errand. It is a commitment you confirm at the midpoint, the way you would confirm any plan whose outcome you actually care about.

Half the year is behind you. The question is simple: is your largest tax shelter halfway full, or quietly falling behind?
