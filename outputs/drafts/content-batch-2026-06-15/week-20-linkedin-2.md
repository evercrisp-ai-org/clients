$40K funded against a $125K target is a problem you want to find in July, not December.

Consider a surgeon with a cash balance plan designed for $250K a year. By midyear the funding schedule says he should be near $125K. He pulls the statement and finds $40K. The deferrals were running fine in his head, but the contributions never actually paced through payroll.

In July, this is a manageable story. There are six months and roughly two dozen pay periods left to close the gap in an orderly way. The deduction stays intact, the plan stays healthy, and nobody is improvising.

The same statement opened in December is a different story entirely. Now he is trying to push $85K through payroll in a few weeks, the deduction is at risk, and a chronic shortfall can put the plan's qualified status in play. The numbers did not change. The runway did.

This is why the cash balance plan deserves its own midyear confirmation, separate from everything else. It is the one account where late attention is not just suboptimal, it is genuinely expensive.

When did you last check your plan's funding against the schedule, not the calendar year?
