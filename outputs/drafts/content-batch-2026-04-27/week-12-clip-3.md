## Content

### Caption Script

You can calculate your practice's normalized EBITDA in 30 minutes. Here's how.

Pull your most recent full-year P&L or tax return. Five steps.

Step one: write down your net income. The bottom line from your most recent full year.

Step two: add back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Your accountant can provide these in five minutes. This gives you your raw EBITDA.

Step three: calculate your compensation above market. Look up MGMA benchmarking data for your specialty and region. Subtract the market figure from your total compensation. That difference is your first add-back.

Step four: list every personal expense running through the practice. Vehicle leases, club memberships, personal travel, family member compensation above market rate. Total them up.

Step five: identify one-time costs from the past two years. Legal disputes, unusual repairs, equipment write-offs, staffing transitions. Anything that happened once and won't recur.

Now add steps two through five to your net income. That's your normalized EBITDA. And it's almost certainly higher than the number you've been carrying in your head.

This number is not precise enough for a transaction. A buyer will do their own analysis. But it gives you a working estimate that changes the conversation from "my practice earns X" to "my practice's earning power is Y, and I have the documentation."

That's a very different starting position.
