The surgeons who get the best outcomes in practice transitions are rarely the ones with the best practices. They're the ones who started preparing earliest.

Two to three years before any transaction, the work begins. Cleaning up the financials. Documenting personal goodwill. Benchmarking compensation. Identifying every expense that a buyer would normalize. Building a multi-year track record of consistent, well-documented earnings.

None of this is glamorous. It's spreadsheets, conversations with your CPA, and methodical documentation. But it creates something that last-minute preparation cannot: leverage.

A buyer who opens your books and finds three years of clean, normalized EBITDA analysis sees a practice that's well-managed and financially sophisticated. That perception changes the negotiation. It changes the multiple. It changes the terms.

A buyer who opens your books and finds one year of hastily assembled data preceded by messy records sees risk. And risk gets discounted.

The preparation doesn't require a sale to be imminent. It doesn't even require a sale to be planned. It just requires the recognition that understanding what your practice is actually worth, in the way a buyer would measure it, is valuable knowledge regardless of your timeline.

Options are created by preparation, not by urgency. The surgeon who knows their normalized EBITDA five years before selling has more leverage than the surgeon who discovers it during diligence.
