## COLD OPEN (30-60 seconds)

If someone offered to buy your practice tomorrow, you'd probably quote a number based on your revenue or net income. Maybe a number a colleague mentioned at a conference. Maybe something your accountant suggested as a rough guide.

The buyer would quote a very different number. And they'd be using a concept you may have never encountered: normalized EBITDA.

Here's why that matters. The gap between your number and the buyer's number is not a negotiation tactic. It's a fundamentally different way of measuring what your practice is worth. And in most cases, the buyer's method produces a higher valuation than your back-of-napkin estimate.

Today, I'm going to walk you through exactly what normalized EBITDA is, how it's calculated, why the gap exists, and how you can calculate your own in about 30 minutes. Because this isn't insider knowledge that buyers are keeping from you. It's a framework that every practice-owning surgeon should understand years before any transaction is on the table.

---

## INTRO AND CONTEXT (1-2 minutes)

This episode is for any surgeon who owns a practice and has ever thought about what it might be worth. Whether a sale is five years away, fifteen years away, or not on your radar at all.

Because here's the thing about practice valuation: it's not a one-time calculation you do when a buyer shows up. It's a financial lens that should inform decisions you're making right now. How you structure your compensation, how you handle family employment, how you manage expenses through the business. All of these choices affect the number a buyer would calculate if they looked at your books today.

And the concept at the center of that calculation is one that most surgeons encounter for the first time during a negotiation, when there's very little time to learn it, and very little leverage to use it.

So let's learn it now, when there's time and space to think about it clearly.

## SEGMENT 1: The Number You Know vs. The Number They Use (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Let me give you two numbers. Both are based on the same practice, the same year of financial data, the same buyer pool.

Number one: $4.4 million.
Number two: $5.5 million.

The first number comes from taking the practice's reported net income of $1.1 million and applying a 4x multiple. Simple math. $1.1 million times four equals $4.4 million. That's probably close to the number the surgeon has in her head.

The second number comes from taking the practice's normalized EBITDA of approximately $1.38 million and applying the same 4x multiple. $1.38 million times four equals $5.5 million, roughly.

That's a $1.1 million gap, and most surgeons don't know it exists until the LOI arrives.

So what is EBITDA? It stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Think of it as the practice's operating cash flow, stripped of financing decisions, tax strategies, and accounting conventions. It answers a simple question: how much cash does this practice generate from operations?

And what is "normalized" EBITDA? It takes that operating cash flow and adjusts for anything that's specific to the current owner. Your personal expenses running through the business. Your above-market salary. Your spouse's above-market compensation. One-time costs that won't recur. Lease terms that reflect a related-party arrangement.

A buyer strips all of that out because they're buying the business, not your lifestyle decisions. They want to know what the practice earns when it's run by a market-rate surgeon with standard expenses. That's the number they base the valuation on.

And here's the part that surprises most surgeons: normalization almost always makes the number go up. Because most of the owner-specific items being adjusted are expenses. Remove those expenses, and the practice's earning power increases. The more you've been running through the business, the larger the gap between reported and normalized, and the higher the valuation.

## SEGMENT 2: The Five Adjustments (5-7 minutes)

Let me walk through the five most common normalization adjustments using a concrete example. [ILLUSTRATIVE] Consider a practice with reported net income of $1.1 million.

### Adjustment 1: Owner Compensation

The surgeon pays herself $800,000 annually. Market compensation for a non-owner orthopedic surgeon in a comparable practice, based on MGMA benchmarking data, is approximately $650,000. The difference, $150,000, gets added back to EBITDA.

This is usually the largest single adjustment, and it's worth understanding why it works this way. A buyer will install a new surgeon (or retain the selling surgeon) at market compensation. The excess above market is effectively profit that was being taken as salary. So a buyer sees it as earnings, not as a fixed cost.

If you've been paying yourself well above market, that's actually good news for your valuation. Every dollar above market comes back as normalized EBITDA.

### Adjustment 2: Family Employment

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Here's an adjustment that catches surgeons off guard. The surgeon's spouse handles billing and some office management at $120,000 per year. The market rate for that role is approximately $55,000. The difference, $65,000, gets added back.

Family employment in practice is common and often serves real purposes: income splitting, benefits coverage, genuine work contribution. But a buyer doesn't price the relationship. They price the role. If the role costs $55,000 on the open market, that's the number in the buyer's model.

At a 4x multiple, this single adjustment is worth $260,000 in valuation. That's a quarter of a million dollars hanging on one line item in your payroll records.

The buyer doesn't care about the relationship. They care about the role and the market rate for that role.

### Adjustment 3: Personal Expenses Through the Business

The surgeon runs a luxury vehicle lease through the practice at $21,600 per year. A buyer strips this entirely. Other common items in this category: country club dues, personal travel, personal insurance premiums, home office expenses beyond what's reasonable.

This adjustment is straightforward. If a buyer wouldn't incur the expense, it gets added back. $21,600 in this example.

### Adjustment 4: One-Time, Non-Recurring Costs

The practice incurred $45,000 in legal expenses from a contract dispute that's been resolved. A buyer normalizes for costs that won't repeat. Other examples: unusual equipment write-offs, settlement payments, a large one-time facility repair, relocation costs for a departing associate.

The logic is simple: if it happened once and won't happen again, it distorts the picture of ongoing earnings. Remove it.

### Adjustment 5: Below-Market Lease Terms

When the surgeon owns the building and leases it to the practice below market, a buyer adjusts rent to the prevailing market rate. This one is nuanced because it can go in either direction. Below-market rent decreases normalized EBITDA (the buyer would pay more). Above-market rent to the surgeon's real estate entity increases it.

In many orthopedic practices, the surgeon does own the real estate, and the lease terms are not at arm's length. This adjustment can be significant in either direction, and it's one that surgeons frequently overlook when doing their own rough calculations.

### The Total

Start with $1,100,000. Add $150,000 (owner comp), $65,000 (family employment), $21,600 (vehicle), $45,000 (legal). Assume the lease is roughly at market.

Normalized EBITDA: $1,381,600.

At 4x: $5,526,400. Compared to $4,400,000 on reported income.

The gap: over $1.1 million. Five adjustments. Each one individually defensible. Together, they change the entire valuation picture.

## SEGMENT 3: Your 30-Minute Homework (3-5 minutes)

`[CLIP-CANDIDATE]`

Here's something you can do this week. Not next quarter. Not when you start thinking about selling. This week.

Pull your most recent full-year P&L or tax return. Block out 30 minutes. And walk through these five steps.

Step one: write down your net income. The bottom line. Whatever your accountant told you the practice earned.

Step two: add back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. If you don't have these numbers handy, your accountant can provide them in five minutes. This gives you raw EBITDA.

Step three: calculate your compensation above market. Go to the MGMA database, or ask your accountant for benchmarking data, and find the median compensation for an orthopedic surgeon in your region and practice type. Subtract that number from your total compensation. Everything above it is your add-back.

Step four: list every personal expense running through the practice. The vehicle. The memberships. The family member's compensation above market. Personal travel. Add them up.

Step five: identify one-time costs from the past two years. Anything that happened once and won't recur. Legal disputes. Unusual repairs. Equipment write-offs. Staffing transitions. Total them.

Now add steps two through five to your net income. That's your normalized EBITDA. And I can tell you right now, it's higher than the number on your tax return.

This is not a precise transaction-ready number. A buyer will do their own detailed analysis. But it gives you a working estimate that's dramatically more accurate than what most surgeons carry around in their heads. And it changes the conversation from "my practice earns X" to "my practice's earning power is Y, and here's the documentation."

That's a very different negotiating position.

## SEGMENT 4: Why Preparation Is Leverage (3-5 minutes)

There's a reason I keep using the word "leverage." Because in any practice transaction, the party who understands the financials most deeply has the most negotiating power.

When a buyer normalizes your EBITDA, they do a thorough job. They have analysts. They have models. They've done this hundreds of times. And they will identify legitimate add-backs, because it's in their interest to arrive at a fair valuation that closes the deal.

But here's what they won't do: advocate for every possible adjustment in your favor. If you've been running $8,000 a year in personal travel through the business and you don't flag it, a buyer might catch it, or they might not. If you have a below-market lease with your real estate LLC that actually inflates EBITDA and you don't disclose it clearly, the buyer will find it and adjust downward, without giving you credit for the above-market items you forgot to mention.

The surgeon who walks into a negotiation with a complete normalization analysis, prepared by their own advisor, documented with benchmarking data, and supported by multiple years of clean financials, controls the narrative. They're not reacting to the buyer's analysis. They're presenting their own.

And there's a compounding effect to preparation. Buyers look at trends. Three to five years of normalized EBITDA, consistently calculated and well-documented, tells a story of a practice that's financially sophisticated and well-managed. One year of clean data preceded by three years of messy records tells a different story entirely.

If a practice transition is anywhere on your horizon, even loosely, the time to start cleaning up and documenting is now. Two to three years of financial preparation before a transaction creates options that last-minute scrambling cannot replicate.

## LISTENER TAKEAWAY (1-2 minutes)

Here's what I'd like you to take away from this episode.

Your practice has two valuations. The one based on what your accountant reports, and the one based on what a buyer would calculate. They are almost certainly different numbers, and the buyer's number is almost certainly higher.

The concept that bridges them, normalized EBITDA, is not complicated. Five categories of adjustments, applied to your existing financial data, produce a more accurate picture of your practice's earning power.

You can calculate a rough version in 30 minutes. I gave you the five steps. Do it this week. Write the number down. Compare it to the number you've been carrying in your head.

And if you want to go deeper, start a conversation with your financial advisor about building a multi-year normalization analysis. The earlier you start, the more leverage you create and the more options you preserve.

## PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE (1-2 minutes)

I want to close with a thought about knowledge and leverage.

In most areas of surgery, the information asymmetry runs in your favor. You understand anatomy that your patients don't. You see risks that they can't. You make decisions based on pattern recognition that takes decades to develop. That expertise is your leverage, and it serves your patients well.

In practice finance, the information asymmetry often runs the other way. Buyers, PE firms, transaction advisors: they understand valuation concepts that most surgeons don't encounter until the deal is already underway. That's not because the concepts are difficult. It's because nobody teaches them in medical school, and by the time a surgeon builds a practice worth selling, there's been no natural moment to learn.

This episode was about collapsing that asymmetry. One concept. Five adjustments. Thirty minutes of homework. That's enough to shift the balance from "I'm discovering this during the negotiation" to "I understood this years ago, and I've been preparing accordingly."

Knowledge, in this context, is leverage. And leverage is how you ensure that the value you've built over a career ends up where it belongs.

Thanks for listening.
