When you sell your practice, the IRS will ask a deceptively simple question: how much of the sale price was you, and how much was the business?

Your answer to that question, backed by documentation, could mean the difference between writing a check for $950,000 and writing one for $1,280,000. That $330,000 gap is entirely within your control. But only if you start the documentation process now. Years before a buyer ever sits across the table.

## The Value That Walks Out the Door

Every orthopedic surgeon who owns a practice has two kinds of goodwill embedded in their business.

Enterprise goodwill is the value of the practice as a going concern. The systems, the staff, the location, the brand, the equipment, the payer contracts tied to the entity. This is what remains after you leave. It transfers with the business.

Personal goodwill is different. It's the value attributable to you. Your surgical reputation. Your referral network. The relationships with primary care physicians and sports medicine colleagues who send patients specifically because of your name. Your years of community presence. Your patient loyalty. This value walks out the door when you do.

The distinction matters because the IRS taxes these two categories differently. And the gap between those tax treatments, at surgeon-level practice values, is measured in hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## The Tax Math That Makes This Worth Starting Now

Consider a surgeon selling a practice valued at $4 million.

Without personal goodwill documentation, the entire sale price is allocated across enterprise assets. The blended tax rate across different asset classes averages approximately 32% (a mix of ordinary income rates on some categories and capital gains on others). Total tax: $1,280,000. The surgeon keeps $2,720,000.

Now run the same sale with proper personal goodwill documentation. The surgeon has spent two years building a defensible record: a non-compete agreement documenting the value of her individual commitment, a referral source analysis showing that 60% of new patients come through relationships tied to her personally, patient retention data demonstrating loyalty to the surgeon rather than the practice, and a formal personal goodwill appraisal.

With this documentation supporting a 40% allocation to personal goodwill, the math changes. The $1.6 million allocated to personal goodwill is taxed at long-term capital gains plus the Net Investment Income Tax: 23.8%. That's $380,800 in tax on the personal goodwill portion. The remaining $2.4 million in enterprise assets is taxed at the blended 32%: $768,000. Total tax: $1,148,800. The surgeon keeps $2,851,200.

The difference: $131,200. On the same sale, at the same price, with the same buyer. The only variable was documentation that started two years before the sale.

And at a 55% personal goodwill allocation, which is achievable with strong documentation for a surgeon whose reputation genuinely drives the practice's patient volume, the savings grow to $180,000 or more. Across realistic allocation ranges, the planning value sits between $130,000 and $350,000.

## Why the Documentation Timeline Is Measured in Years

Here's where most surgeons get caught off guard. Personal goodwill can't be asserted during sale negotiations and retroactively applied. The IRS scrutinizes after-the-fact personal goodwill claims, and courts have consistently held that the documentation must precede the transaction.

There are three components to a defensible personal goodwill record.

**The non-compete agreement.** This is the foundational document. It establishes that you, as an individual, have value to the practice that would be lost if you competed against it. The agreement must be between you and the practice entity (not between you and the buyer), and it must be in place before any acquisition discussions begin. Courts have looked at the timing of non-compete agreements as a key indicator of whether personal goodwill was genuinely pre-existing or manufactured for tax purposes.

**The referral source analysis.** This documents the percentage of the practice's patient volume that flows through your personal relationships rather than through the practice's institutional channels. If 60% of new patient referrals come from physicians who refer specifically to you (and would likely redirect those referrals if you left), that's a quantifiable measure of personal goodwill. The analysis should cover at least 24 months of referral data to establish a credible pattern.

**The personal goodwill appraisal.** A formal valuation conducted by a qualified appraiser who separates the practice's total goodwill into personal and enterprise components. The appraisal should use accepted methodologies (the "with and without" method is common: what would the practice be worth with you vs. without you?). This appraisal must be completed well before any sale process begins.

The entire documentation process takes 12 to 24 months to build properly. If you're planning to sell in the next three to five years, the clock started before you finished reading this paragraph.

## What Happens When You Wait

The surgeon who starts the personal goodwill documentation process three years before a planned exit has time to build a comprehensive, defensible record. The non-compete is seasoned. The referral analysis covers multiple years. The appraisal is completed without the appearance of being manufactured for a pending deal.

The surgeon who starts six months before a sale, or worse, during the sale negotiations, faces a different reality. The IRS can challenge after-the-fact documentation as self-serving. Courts have disallowed personal goodwill allocations where the documentation appeared to have been created specifically to support a tax position in a pending transaction. The timing itself becomes evidence against the claim.

In one frequently cited case, a professional services firm attempted to allocate 75% of a sale to personal goodwill based on documentation prepared during the acquisition process. The court reduced the allocation to 15%, noting that the non-compete agreement was signed simultaneously with the purchase agreement and the referral analysis was conducted specifically for the transaction. The tax difference in that case: over $400,000.

The principle is straightforward: document early, or don't document at all. There is no middle ground that withstands IRS scrutiny at surgeon-level practice values.

## The Connection to Your Exit Timeline

If you read the five-year exit timeline from two weeks ago, personal goodwill documentation belongs in Year 5 or Year 4 of that plan. It's one of the earliest steps in the transition process, because it requires the longest lead time and has the highest dollar impact.

Here's the sequencing that makes the most sense for a surgeon three to five years from a planned exit:

**Year 5 (now, if applicable):** Establish the non-compete agreement between you and your practice entity. Begin tracking referral sources with a systematic process that will produce 24+ months of data.

**Year 4:** Commission the personal goodwill appraisal. Complete the referral source analysis with at least two years of data. Ensure all documentation is formal, dated, and stored properly.

**Year 3:** Review and update the documentation. If anything has changed materially (new partners, referral network shifts, scope of practice changes), refresh the analysis. The documentation should reflect a consistent, multi-year pattern.

**Years 2–1:** The documentation is seasoned and ready to support the allocation during sale negotiations. The buyer's tax advisors and the IRS see a credible, long-standing record rather than a last-minute tax strategy.

## The Underlying Principle

Your reputation, your referral network, your surgical skill, and the patient trust you've built over decades have a dollar value. A significant, measurable, taxable dollar value.

The question is whether you'll take the time to document that value in a way that the IRS recognizes, or whether you'll let it be taxed as if it were just another piece of equipment in the practice.

Personal goodwill is the value of everything you've built that walks out the door when you do. Make sure it's documented. Because undocumented goodwill is worthless in a tax court. And worthless in a tax court means $130,000 to $350,000 that stays with the IRS instead of staying with you.

If you're three to five years from a potential practice transition, this is the planning conversation to have this month. The documentation doesn't require a buyer. It doesn't require a timeline. It only requires the decision to start.
