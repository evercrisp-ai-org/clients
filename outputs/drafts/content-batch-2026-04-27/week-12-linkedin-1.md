$1.1 million. That's the valuation gap between what your accountant shows you and what a buyer actually sees when they open your books.

Consider a practice with reported net income of $1.1 million. Multiply by 4x and you get $4.4 million. That's the number most surgeons carry in their heads.

A buyer runs a different calculation. They adjust for five things: owner compensation above market, family members on payroll at above-market rates, personal expenses through the business, one-time costs that won't recur, and lease terms between related parties. After those adjustments, the same practice shows normalized EBITDA of roughly $1.38 million. At 4x, the valuation is $5.5 million.

The concept creating that gap, normalized EBITDA, is designed to work in your favor as a seller. Every dollar of above-market compensation you've been paying yourself gets added back. Every personal expense running through the business gets added back. The more you've optimized as an owner, the larger the gap, and the higher the valuation.

The catch: you benefit most when you understand this before the negotiation starts. A buyer will normalize regardless. The question is who presents the analysis first.

Which of the five adjustments would move your number the most?
