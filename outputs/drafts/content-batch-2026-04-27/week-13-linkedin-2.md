$2.9 million became $1.7 million in three years. The surgeon didn't make bad investments. He made no plan for what came after.

Consider a surgeon who negotiated well, planned the tax strategy, and closed on strong terms. The wire hit. Then the question arrived: now what?

Without an answer, the defaults took over. Lifestyle expanded 30% because there was no budget framework. A $500,000 speculative investment filled the need to feel productive. Drawdowns continued because the spending felt earned. By year three, 41% of the net proceeds were gone.

The financial erosion was real, but the cause was not financial. It was an identity gap. Twenty-five years of waking up with a clear purpose, a team, a schedule, complex problems to solve with your hands. When that went quiet, spending filled the space that purpose should have occupied.

The surgeon who runs the same exit with a structured next-chapter plan, a defined budget, a systematic investment framework, and purpose-driven activities designed before the sale, lands at $3.2 million by year three. Same starting point. $1.5 million difference.

The question that matters more than any valuation: who are you when you're not a surgeon?
