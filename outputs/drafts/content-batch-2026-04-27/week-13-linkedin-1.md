30% to 40% of exit wealth disappears within three years for surgeons who leave without a plan for what comes after. The cause is rarely a single catastrophic decision. It is the accumulation of unstructured ones.

Consider a surgeon who nets $2.9 million from a well-negotiated sale. Solid outcome. Year one: lifestyle expands 30%, a speculative investment deploys $500,000, and nearly a million dollars moves without a framework. Year two: drawdowns continue, the investment returns nothing. Year three: $1.7 million remains. A 41% decline from a position that looked secure 36 months earlier.

The pattern underneath is not financial illiteracy. It is identity loss. When the OR goes quiet and nothing structured fills the space, spending becomes the default activity. The country club replaces the schedule. The investment replaces the challenge. The lifestyle upgrade replaces the status.

The surgeons who preserve and grow transition wealth share three disciplines: identity work before the exit, purpose architecture for the chapter after, and financial guardrails that protect capital from the gap between the two.

Which of those three is the weakest in your current transition plan?
