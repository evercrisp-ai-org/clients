Quick note for any surgeon thinking about selling a practice someday, even if "someday" is years away.

There's a concept called normalized EBITDA that most surgeons never encounter until a buyer puts an offer on the table. It's the number a buyer actually uses to value your practice, and it's almost always different from the net income on your tax return.

Consider a practice with reported net income of $1.1 million. A straightforward calculation: $1.1M times a 4x multiple equals $4.4M. That's probably close to the number most surgeons would quote if someone asked what their practice is worth.

A buyer runs a different calculation. They adjust for above-market owner compensation, family members on payroll above market rates, personal expenses through the business, and one-time costs. After those adjustments, the same practice shows $1.38M in normalized EBITDA. At 4x, the valuation is $5.5M.

That's a $1.1 million gap. Not because the buyer is being generous. Because the buyer is looking at the practice's earning power without the owner's personal financial choices layered on top.

Understanding this number early gives you time to prepare, document, and present your own analysis before a buyer presents theirs. That's the difference between reacting to someone else's math and controlling the conversation.
