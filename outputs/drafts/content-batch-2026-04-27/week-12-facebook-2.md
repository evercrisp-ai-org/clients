There's a moment in learning financial planning for practice owners when you realize that the number on the tax return and the number a buyer calculates are two completely different figures. For me, it was one of those "how is this not common knowledge" realizations.

The concept is called normalized EBITDA. It strips out everything specific to the current owner, your above-market salary, your personal expenses through the business, your family member's compensation, one-time costs, and shows what the practice actually earns as a standalone operation.

For most surgeon-owned practices, the normalized number is higher than the reported number. Sometimes significantly higher. Because surgeons (sensibly) run personal expenses through the business and pay themselves above what a non-owner would earn. Those choices reduce reported income but don't reduce the practice's actual earning power.

The gap between reported and normalized can be six figures. Sometimes seven.

What struck me is how rarely this concept comes up before a transaction is already underway. By then, the buyer's analysts have already run the normalization. The surgeon is seeing it for the first time while simultaneously trying to negotiate.

If there's one financial concept I'd want every practice-owning surgeon to understand early, well before any sale is on the horizon, this is the one. It changes how you think about what you've built.
