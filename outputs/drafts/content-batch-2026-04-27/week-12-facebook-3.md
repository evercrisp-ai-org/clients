If a buyer opened your practice's books tomorrow, they'd make five adjustments before they calculated what your practice is worth. Here's what those adjustments look like.

1. Owner compensation above market. If you're paying yourself more than a non-owner surgeon would earn for the same work, the excess gets added back to earnings. For an orthopedic surgeon earning $800K when the market rate is $650K, that's a $150K add-back.

2. Family employment above market. If a family member is on payroll at a rate higher than the market price for their role, a buyer adjusts to market. A $120K admin role that markets at $55K becomes a $65K add-back.

3. Personal expenses through the business. Vehicle leases, memberships, personal travel. If the buyer wouldn't pay it, it gets added back. Common in surgeon-owned practices.

4. One-time, non-recurring costs. Legal disputes, unusual repairs, settlement payments. If it happened once and won't happen again, the buyer strips it out.

5. Below-market lease terms. If you own the building and lease it to the practice below market, the buyer adjusts. This one can go in either direction.

These five adjustments, applied to a practice with $1.1M in net income, can push the valuation-relevant number to $1.38M. At a 4x multiple, that's the difference between $4.4M and $5.5M.

Save this list. Walk through it with your most recent P&L. It takes about 30 minutes.
