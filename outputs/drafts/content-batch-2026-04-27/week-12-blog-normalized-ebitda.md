A surgeon told me her practice was worth $4.4 million. She had good reason to think so. Her accountant had walked her through the numbers: roughly $1.1 million in net income, a reasonable multiple, a clean calculation. The math made sense on paper.

After we walked through the normalization process together, the number was $5.5 million.

Same practice. Same year of financial data. Same buyer pool. The only difference was which version of the financials the valuation was based on.

That $1.1 million gap is not unusual. And the concept that creates it, normalized EBITDA, is something most orthopedic surgeons have never encountered until a buyer puts a letter of intent on the table. By then, the leverage has already shifted.

## What EBITDA Actually Is

Before we get to normalization, let's start with the base concept.

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It strips out financing decisions, tax strategies, and accounting conventions to reveal what the business actually earns from operations.

For a surgeon-owned practice, EBITDA removes the noise of how the practice is financed (interest), how it's taxed (which varies by entity structure), and how equipment purchases are written off (depreciation and amortization). What's left is the operating engine: how much cash the practice generates from seeing patients, performing procedures, and running the day-to-day business.

Buyers care about EBITDA because it lets them compare practices on a level playing field. Your tax strategy is irrelevant to the buyer. Your equipment financing terms are irrelevant. Your depreciation schedule is irrelevant. What matters is the cash the practice produces, because that's what the buyer is purchasing.

## Why "Reported" Numbers Aren't What Buyers Use

Here's where it gets interesting. Your practice's reported net income (the number on your tax return or P&L statement) reflects every decision you've made as an owner. And many of those decisions are perfectly rational for a surgeon who owns and operates the practice, but they don't reflect the practice's true earning power.

Consider a practice with reported net income of $1.1 million. [ILLUSTRATIVE] That number already accounts for:

- The surgeon's salary of $800,000, which includes roughly $150,000 above what a buyer would pay a non-owner surgeon to do the same work
- A family member on payroll at $120,000 for an administrative role that would cost $55,000 at market rates
- A luxury vehicle lease of $21,600 per year running through the practice
- One-time legal expenses of $45,000 from a contract dispute that won't recur
- A below-market lease on the office space (which happens to be owned by the surgeon's real estate LLC)

Every one of these is a legitimate business decision for the current owner. The above-market salary? That's how the surgeon takes profit from the practice. The family member? That's a family employment strategy with possible tax benefits. The vehicle? Easier to run it through the business. The legal costs? A one-time event. The lease? Smart real estate structuring.

But a buyer looking at this practice doesn't care about any of those owner-specific choices. The buyer wants to know: if I install a new surgeon at market compensation, eliminate the owner's personal expenses, and strip out one-time costs, what does this practice actually earn?

That's what normalization answers.

## The Five Adjustments: From $1.1M to $1.38M

Let's walk through each adjustment. [ILLUSTRATIVE]

### 1. Owner Compensation Adjustment: +$150,000

The surgeon pays herself $800,000. Market compensation for a non-owner orthopedic surgeon in a similar practice, based on MGMA benchmarking data, is approximately $650,000. The $150,000 difference is added back to EBITDA because a buyer would pay a replacement surgeon the market rate, not the owner's rate.

This is typically the largest single adjustment, and it works in the seller's favor. The more the surgeon has been paying herself above market, the larger the add-back and the higher the normalized EBITDA.

### 2. Family Employment Adjustment: +$65,000

The surgeon's spouse handles billing and some administrative tasks at $120,000 per year. A buyer would staff that role at market rate, approximately $55,000. The $65,000 difference is added back.

This adjustment is common in surgeon-owned practices. Family employment serves legitimate purposes (income splitting, benefits, genuine work contribution), but a buyer normalizes to market compensation for the role, not the relationship.

### 3. Personal Expenses Through the Business: +$21,600

The luxury vehicle lease of $21,600 per year is a personal expense running through the practice. A buyer strips this out entirely. Other common personal expenses that get normalized: country club dues, personal travel coded as business travel, personal insurance premiums, and home office expenses beyond what's reasonable.

### 4. One-Time, Non-Recurring Costs: +$45,000

The $45,000 in legal expenses from a contract dispute was a one-time event. Buyers normalize for costs that won't recur under new ownership. Other examples: a one-time equipment write-off, a settlement payment, relocation costs for a departing associate, or an unusually expensive year of facility repairs.

### 5. Below-Market Lease Terms: Varies

When the surgeon owns the real estate and leases it to the practice below market, a buyer adjusts the rent to market rate. This adjustment can go in either direction. If the practice is paying below-market rent, normalized EBITDA decreases (because the buyer would need to pay market rent). If the practice is paying above-market rent to the surgeon's real estate entity, normalized EBITDA increases.

For this example, let's assume the lease is roughly at market, so no material adjustment is needed. In many real situations, this adjustment can be significant in either direction.

### The Math

Starting point: $1,100,000 (reported net income)

- Owner compensation: +$150,000
- Family employment: +$65,000
- Personal vehicle lease: +$21,600
- One-time legal costs: +$45,000
- Lease adjustment: $0

**Normalized EBITDA: $1,381,600**

At a 4x EBITDA multiple (conservative for an orthopedic practice with stable referral patterns and clean operations):

- Valuation on reported net income: $1,100,000 x 4 = **$4,400,000**
- Valuation on normalized EBITDA: $1,381,600 x 4 = **$5,526,400**

**Valuation difference: $1,126,400**

That's the gap. Over $1.1 million, based entirely on whether the financials are presented as the owner sees them or as the buyer would reconstruct them.

## Why This Works in Your Favor

Here's what most surgeons miss about normalization: it's designed to work in your favor as a seller.

Every dollar of above-market compensation you've been paying yourself gets added back. Every personal expense running through the business gets added back. Every one-time cost that won't recur under new ownership gets added back.

Normalization reveals the practice's true earning power by stripping out the owner's personal financial decisions. For most surgeon-owned practices, the normalized number is significantly higher than the reported number, because surgeons (rationally) run personal expenses through the business and pay themselves above market.

The critical insight: you benefit from normalization most when you understand it before the negotiation begins. A buyer will normalize your financials regardless. The question is whether you've done the work yourself, identified every legitimate add-back, and presented the normalized picture proactively, or whether you're relying on the buyer's analysis, which may not capture every adjustment in your favor.

## The 30-Minute Exercise

You can build a rough normalized EBITDA for your own practice in about 30 minutes. Here's the framework:

**Step 1: Start with your net income.** Pull the bottom line from your most recent full-year P&L or tax return.

**Step 2: Add back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization.** Your accountant can provide these figures quickly. This gives you your raw EBITDA.

**Step 3: Calculate your compensation above market.** Look up MGMA compensation data for your specialty and geographic region. Subtract the market figure from your total compensation (salary plus bonus plus distributions). The difference is your add-back.

**Step 4: List every personal expense running through the practice.** Vehicle leases, club memberships, personal travel, personal insurance, family member compensation above market rate. Total them.

**Step 5: Identify one-time costs from the past 12-24 months.** Legal disputes, unusual repairs, equipment write-offs, staffing transitions, settlement payments. If it won't recur, it gets added back.

Write those five numbers down. Add them to your EBITDA. That's your approximate normalized EBITDA.

The result won't be precise enough for a transaction (a buyer will do their own detailed analysis), but it will give you a working number that is far more accurate than the net income figure most surgeons use when they think about their practice's value.

## Strategic Preparation: 2-3 Years Before a Sale

If a practice transition is anywhere on your horizon (even loosely), the time to start thinking about normalization is now. Not when a buyer calls. Not when a letter of intent arrives. Now.

Three reasons:

**Clean financials compound.** A buyer doesn't just look at one year. They want to see 3-5 years of normalized EBITDA to understand trends. If your financials are messy for the first three years and clean for the last year, the buyer discounts the clean year. If your financials show consistent, well-documented normalized EBITDA over multiple years, the valuation conversation starts from a position of strength.

**Documentation takes time.** Personal goodwill analysis, non-compete agreements, referral source documentation, compensation benchmarking studies: these are not documents you create the week before a negotiation. They require professional preparation and a credible paper trail that predates any buyer conversation.

**Leverage shifts with preparation.** A surgeon who walks into a negotiation with a complete normalized EBITDA analysis, supported by benchmarking data and multi-year documentation, is in a fundamentally different position than a surgeon who hands over tax returns and lets the buyer do the math. The first surgeon controls the narrative. The second one reacts to it.

## What This Means for You

Normalized EBITDA is not an abstract accounting concept. It's the number that determines what your practice is worth to someone who wants to buy it. And for most surgeon-owned practices, it's significantly higher than the number sitting on your tax return.

The gap between reported and normalized is real money. In the illustrative example above, it's over $1.1 million. For some practices with more personal expenses flowing through the business, or with higher above-market compensation, the gap can be even larger.

Understanding this concept early, calculating your own normalized EBITDA, and preparing your financials with a future transaction in mind is one of the highest-return activities a practice-owning surgeon can undertake. It costs nothing but a few hours of focused analysis. And it can add hundreds of thousands of dollars to your exit.

The number your accountant shows you and the number a buyer sees are two very different figures. Knowing both is how you enter a negotiation with clarity instead of surprise.
