$120,000 for a part-time admin role. That's the line item a buyer will adjust first when they open your books.

Consider a surgeon whose spouse handles billing and some office management. The arrangement makes sense: genuine work contribution, income splitting benefits, and someone the surgeon trusts running a critical function. The salary: $120,000.

A buyer sees the same role differently. They price the function, not the relationship. Market rate for that position: approximately $55,000. The $65,000 difference gets added back to normalized EBITDA as an owner-specific adjustment.

Here's where it gets interesting. At a 4x valuation multiple, that single payroll adjustment is worth $260,000 in practice valuation. A quarter of a million dollars, tied to one line item.

This adjustment applies whether the family member does excellent work or minimal work. The buyer's model doesn't evaluate performance. It evaluates what the role costs on the open market and normalizes accordingly.

Knowing this doesn't mean you should change the arrangement. Family employment often serves real strategic purposes. It means understanding that a buyer will measure it differently than you do, and that the gap between your measurement and theirs is worth real money in a transaction.

What would a buyer normalize on your payroll if they looked at it today?
