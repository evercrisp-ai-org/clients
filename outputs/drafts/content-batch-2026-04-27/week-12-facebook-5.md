I've been thinking about information asymmetry this week.

In the operating room, the surgeon holds the advantage. You understand anatomy, risk, timing, and technique at a depth your patient cannot replicate. That expertise is your leverage, and it serves your patients well.

In practice finance, the advantage often sits on the other side of the table. Buyers, PE analysts, and transaction advisors understand valuation concepts, normalization methods, and deal structures that most surgeons have never studied. That gap is not because the concepts are complex. It's because there's been no natural moment in a surgical career to learn them.

Medical school doesn't teach EBITDA. Fellowship doesn't teach normalization. And by the time you've built a practice worth selling, you've spent two decades mastering something else entirely.

The good news: the concepts themselves are not difficult. They're learnable. Five normalization adjustments. One 30-minute calculation. A few hours of focused study. That's enough to close the gap and shift the leverage back to where it belongs.

Knowledge, in this context, is leverage. Not theoretical leverage. Practical leverage that shows up in negotiations, in deal terms, and in the number that lands in your account at closing.

The value you've built over a career deserves to be understood by the person who built it.
