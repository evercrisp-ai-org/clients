Here's something that rarely comes up in practice transition conversations: what does the first Monday morning feel like?

For 20 or 30 years, that question answered itself. You knew where you were going. You knew what the day demanded. You had a team waiting for you and patients counting on your skill. Monday morning had weight, purpose, and direction.

The first Monday after the OR is different. The alarm doesn't go off at 5:30 because there's no case at 7:00. Nobody needs you in a particular room at a particular time. The skills you've spent decades refining are, at least for the moment, not in active use.

That moment is not a problem to solve. It's a transition to design.

The surgeons who thrive after the OR are the ones who filled that Monday morning before it arrived. A board meeting. A teaching session. A mentorship call. A morning at the gym followed by two hours on a project that uses their medical expertise in a completely different context.

They didn't wait for purpose to appear. They built it. They gave the next chapter the same planning and intentionality they brought to every surgical schedule.

If your Monday mornings after the OR feel uncertain, you don't need more money. You need a plan for what your time is for.
