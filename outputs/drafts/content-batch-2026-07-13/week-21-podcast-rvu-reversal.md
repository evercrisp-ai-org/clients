## Content

---

**[COLD OPEN — 30-60 seconds]**

For years, the conventional wisdom has been that independent orthopedic surgeons are the productivity leaders. You built your practice, you controlled your schedule, you ran your own infrastructure, and by every measure, independent practice owners outworked their hospital-employed counterparts on raw procedure volume.

I want to walk you through some data that flips that assumption. And then I want to talk about what it means for the financial picture specifically. Because if you own an orthopedic practice and you are anywhere in the 3-to-10-year window of considering what comes next, this data matters to your exit outcome in ways that most advisors have not connected yet.

Let's get into it.

---

**[INTRO AND CONTEXT — 1-2 minutes]**

Welcome back. I'm Jared Paul, CFP, and this is Capable Wealth, built for orthopedic surgeons navigating the financial complexity of life beyond the OR. This week we're closing out July's content arc on the halftime theme. We've talked about the halftime report, the cash balance plan pacing check, and the income acceleration versus deferral decision. This episode pulls back the lens to look at a market-level shift that affects everything we've been discussing about practice valuation and exit timing.

We're talking about work RVU benchmarks. Specifically, a reversal in who's generating more of them: hospital-employed orthopedists or independent practice owners. And we're going to talk about why the financial implication of that reversal is bigger than most surgeons realize.

If you are a practice owner who is paying close attention to your valuation, this is the episode to sit with. And if you're newer to the practice transition conversation, this will give you a data point that will help you think about your long-term positioning.

Let's start with the data.

---

**[SEGMENT 1: THE DATA — 3-5 minutes]**

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: starts here]

Hospital-employed orthopedic surgeons now generate more work RVUs per year than their independent counterparts. That's a first. The driver is infrastructure, and it carries a direct financial implication for practice valuations.

For those who track MGMA and AAOS compensation surveys, this is documented in the most recent data cycle. The productivity gap runs roughly 10 to 15 percent, consistent across multiple benchmarking sources, and it reverses the long-standing pattern where independent practice owners led on productivity.

What's driving it?

The gap is almost entirely infrastructure. Hospital systems have made significant investments in scheduling infrastructure over the past several years: protected OR time, dedicated case coordinators, referral channeling systems. When a hospital-employed surgeon walks into the OR, someone else has handled the scheduling, the patient prep coordination, and the administrative follow-up. The surgeon shows up and operates.

Independent practice owners are doing that work too, just often alongside surgery. At a median compensation of $800K and a typical 10-12 hour clinical day, the administrative burden of running a practice is being absorbed by the same person performing the procedures. Something has to give, and what gives is RVU output.

Add the CMS efficiency adjustment (that 2.5% cut to non-time-based orthopedic procedures in effect since January), and independent practices are absorbing both a revenue headwind and an administrative burden that employed physicians are mostly insulated from. The result is a measurable productivity gap.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: ends here]

Now, here's why I want you to care about this beyond the immediate clinical question.

---

**[SEGMENT 2: THE VALUATION IMPLICATION — 3-5 minutes]**

When buyers evaluate a practice for acquisition (and this applies whether you are talking to a PE firm, a hospital system, or an individual physician buyer), they build their valuation model on normalized EBITDA. The normalization step adjusts for the owner's compensation, any personal expenses running through the business, and the baseline assumptions about ongoing revenue.

Here is where the RVU reversal starts to affect your outcome.

The standard normalization has always been: replace the owner's salary with a market rate for an equivalent employed surgeon. If you have been paying yourself $875K and the market rate for a hospital-employed orthopedist at comparable volume is $700K, the normalization adds back $175K to EBITDA, which flows directly into a higher valuation.

What is changing is the revenue side.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: starts here]

More buyers, particularly PE firms and hospital system acquisition teams, are now running a stress test on the forward revenue. Their question: if we install a replacement surgeon who operates at the hospital productivity benchmark rather than at the selling surgeon's personal RVU level, what does the revenue look like?

If the selling surgeon outperforms the hospital benchmark significantly, this is positive for the buyer's model: it gives them upside. If the selling surgeon's RVU output has been tracking near or below the hospital benchmark, buyers may discount the forward revenue assumption.

Here's what that looks like in numbers.

Consider a practice with $1.45 million in normalized EBITDA, valued at a 4x multiple. That's a $5.8 million practice. Now apply a 10% productivity-adjusted haircut on the forward revenue assumption, representing the buyer's model of what happens when the selling surgeon's institutional relationships and scheduling preferences are no longer running the practice. That 10% revenue adjustment reduces normalized EBITDA to roughly $1.31 million. At 4x, the practice is worth $5.24 million.

The RVU shift, unaddressed, has moved $560,000 out of this surgeon's exit outcome before a single term has been negotiated.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: ends here]

The cause is a market benchmark that changed and a practice that had not adapted to the new data.

The surgeons who are positioned best for exit right now are the ones whose productivity data tells a story of independence premium: they outperform the hospital benchmark, which signals that their clinical volume and referral relationships are personal, transferable, and documentable. That story commands a higher multiple.

---

**[SEGMENT 3: THE PE ANGLE — 3-5 minutes]**

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: starts here]

Private equity firms in orthopedics are using this RVU data actively. I want to be clear about what that means in practice.

When a PE firm evaluates a practice, they are building two models: the acquisition model and the exit model. The acquisition model tells them what to pay now. The exit model tells them what they can sell the platform for in four or five years. Both models depend heavily on physician productivity assumptions.

A surgeon who outperforms the hospital benchmark on work RVUs is a better acquisition target than one who underperforms, because it means post-acquisition productivity is likely to hold or improve as the surgeon's administrative burden decreases within a larger infrastructure. PE firms like productivity upside with low execution risk.

A surgeon who underperforms the hospital benchmark introduces a recovery timeline into the model. The PE firm assumes they will close the productivity gap over the hold period, but that assumption reduces what they are willing to pay today. They are pricing in the cost of fixing the infrastructure problem that created the gap.

[CLIP-CANDIDATE: ends here]

None of this is unfair. It is rational. The data says what it says, and buyers use it.

What is unfair is when a surgeon who built an excellent practice and served patients well walks into a transaction without understanding that one market-level shift has been quietly repositioning their practice's value. The RVU reversal is not something you can ignore until a deal is on the table. By then, the analysis has already been run. Your leverage comes from running it first.

---

**[SEGMENT 4: THREE MOVES — 3-5 minutes]**

Let me give you the practical response to this data. Three moves.

The first move is to get your own RVU baseline. If you have not compared your annual work RVU output against the current MGMA or AAOS benchmarks for your subspecialty and employment model in the past 12 months, that number is now a meaningful gap in your financial plan. Your billing service can pull this. Your practice management system has it. You can have this number in 48 hours. The question is whether you want it before a buyer does.

The second move is to audit the infrastructure gaps that are driving the productivity differential. The hospital productivity advantage is almost entirely about scheduling efficiency, case coordination, and administrative burden. These are all improvable in an independent practice without a hospital system behind you. If you benchmarked your administrative overhead ratio against MGMA standards two or three years ago but haven't revisited it, the current data may look different. A 10 to 15 percent improvement in RVU output through operational improvements in scheduling and coordination is worth roughly $500K to $600K in practice value at a 4x multiple, with clinical hours unchanged. That is a meaningful return on an operational project that most practice owners have the capacity to execute.

The third move is to reconsider your exit timeline against the current market dynamics. If the RVU reversal is narrowing the window of peak practice valuation, and you are 3 to 5 years from a transaction, the preparation work we have discussed throughout this year's content arc (personal goodwill documentation, practice systematization, reducing surgeon-dependence, establishing the productivity story) all of that work has an added urgency now. The surgeons who get to the market in a position of strength are the ones who started the preparation before the buyer's analyst ran the model.

---

**[LISTENER TAKEAWAY — 1-2 minutes]**

Here is the one thing to do this week.

Pull your practice's work RVU output for the trailing 12 months. Ask your billing service or practice manager for the number. Then look up the MGMA benchmark for your subspecialty. If you don't have the benchmark handy, AAOS publishes compensation and productivity data that is accessible to members.

Compare your output against the hospital-employed benchmark. If you are above it, you have a productivity premium that is worth documenting and protecting. If you are at or below it, you have an operational gap that, in the current market, is costing you real money in eventual exit value.

That number is the starting point for everything else we've talked about in this episode.

---

**[PHILOSOPHICAL CLOSE — 1-2 minutes]**

I want to close with something that I think gets lost in conversations about benchmarks and multiples and exit timelines.

You built an independent practice because you believed in something. Autonomy, ownership, the ability to make clinical decisions for your patients without a system's incentives sitting between you and the right call. That belief was right. The outcomes it produced were real. And the practice you built carries financial value that no RVU benchmark can fully capture.

The work RVU reversal is real data. So is the infrastructure gap that created it. So is the valuation implication I described. All of it is real, and all of it is worth responding to with the same precision you bring to the OR.

Responding to it means building the structures (operational, documentational, financial) that let the value you created show up in the transaction when the time comes.

The surgeons I most admire in this space are the ones who understood what they had built, protected it, and exited on terms they had shaped.

That is the goal. And the data I shared today tells you where to start.

I'll see you next week.
